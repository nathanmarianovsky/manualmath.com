Power Series
A power series expansion of a function is defined as:
$\eqalign{ f(x) = \sum_{n = 0}^\infty a_n x^n }$
an infinite polynomial where $a_n$ represents the coefficients. Now given that we have a region of convergence for this power series, then we can also talk about its derivatives:
$\eqalign{ f'(x) &= \sum_{n = 0}^\infty n a_n x^{n - 1} \\ f''(x) &= \sum_{n = 0}^\infty n(n - 1)a_n x^{n - 2} }$
Application in Differential Equations
Given a homogeneous linear second order differential equation:
$\eqalign{ y'' + p(x)y' + q(x)y = 0 }$
where $p(x)$ and $q(x)$ are $\textit{analytic functions}$, can be locally expressed as a power series, then the solution can be determined in the form of a power series. The main task when using a power series as a solution becomes determining the coefficients. Typically the approach is to take all of the sums and to try to combine them under a single sum so that the equation becomes:
$\eqalign{ \sum_{n = 0}^\infty k(n) x^n = 0 }$
where $k(n)$ represents the function that will contain the coefficients and $n$. Now like a polynomial, the power series is only identically zero iff all of its coefficients are zero. So to determine the coefficients it becomes a task of solving:
$\eqalign{ k(n) = 0 }$
which can sometimes provide an explicit form for all of the coefficients or just be left as a recurrence relation with some initial condition(s).
