Characteristic Polynomial
Given a linear constant coefficient second order differential equation:
$\eqalign{ y'' + ay' + by = 0 }$
where:
$\eqalign{ a,b \in \mathbb{R} }$
suppose that the solution takes on the form:
$\eqalign{ y(x) = e^{mx} \hspace{.5cm} \text{where} \hspace{.5cm} m \in \mathbb{R} }$
and plug into the differential equation:
$\eqalign{ 0 &= y'' + ay' + by \\ 0 &= m^2e^{mx} + ame^{mx} + be^{mx} \\ 0 &= e^{mx}(m^2 + am + b) \\ 0 &= m^2 + am + b }$
to arrive at what is known as the $\textit{characterisitc polynomial}$. Now depending upon the values that $m$ takes on, the solution takes on different forms. So to isolate $m$, use the quadratic formula:
$\eqalign{ m = \frac{-a \pm \sqrt{a^2 - 4b}}{2} }$
Two Real Roots
Lets assume that the discriminant is strictly positive:
$\eqalign{ a^2 - 4b > 0 }$
With this we know that there are going to be two real values that $m$ can take on. So using the principle of superposition for homogeneous differential equations the general solution will take on the form:
$\eqalign{ y(x) = c_1e^{m_1x} + c_2e^{m_2x} }$
Repeated Root
Lets assume that the discriminant is zero now:
$\eqalign{ a^2 - 4b = 0 }$
With this we know that there is going to be one real value that $m$ can take on. So lets use reduction of order to obtain the second solution with the setup:
$\eqalign{ y_2 &= ve^{mx} \\ y_2' &= v'e^{mx} + mve^{mx} \\ y_2'' &= v''e^{mx} + 2mv'e^{mx} + m^2ve^{mx} }$
Plugging this in gives:
$\eqalign{ 0 &= y_2'' + ay_2' + by_2 \\ 0 &= (v''e^{mx} + 2mv'e^{mx} + m^2ve^{mx}) + a(v'e^{mx} + mve^{mx}) + bve^{mx} \\ 0 &= e^{mx}\Big[ v'' + v'(2m + a) + v(y_1'' + ay_1' + by_1) \Big] \\ 0 &= v'' + v'(2m + a) }$
Now make the substitution:
$\eqalign{ w = v' }$
and continue:
$\eqalign{ 0 &= v'' + v'(2m + a) \\ 0 &= w' + w(2m + a) \\ \frac{dw}{dx} &= -w(2m + a) \\ }$
Now use the fact that the discriminant is zero which implies $m = -\frac{a}{2}$ and that $2m + a = 0$:
$\eqalign{ \frac{dw}{dx} &= 0 \\ w &= D_1 }$
Using this to solve for $v$:
$\eqalign{ v' &= w \\ \frac{dv}{dx} &= D_1 \\ \int dv &= D_1\int dx \\ v &= D_1x + D_2 }$
With this the second solution takes on the form:
$\eqalign{ y_2(x) = xe^{mx} }$
which means that the general solution is written down as:
$\eqalign{ y(x) = c_1e^{mx} + c_2xe^{mx} }$
Two Imaginary Roots
Lets assume that the discriminant is strictly negative:
$\eqalign{ a^2 - 4b < 0 }$
With this we know that there are going to be two imaginary values that $m$ can take on. So using the principle of superposition for homogeneous differential equations the general solution will take on the form:
$\eqalign{ y(x) = c_1e^{m_1x} + c_2e^{m_2x} }$
where:
$\eqalign{ m_1,m_2 \in \mathbb{C} }$
Unfortunately nobody likes having imaginary exponentials therefore lets make use of Euler's Formula:
$\eqalign{ e^{iz} = \cos(z) + i\sin(z) \hspace{.5cm} \text{where} \hspace{.5cm} z \in \mathbb{C} }$
Take the general solution and assume that the roots can be written as $m_1,m_2 = \alpha \pm \beta i$:
$\eqalign{ y(x) &= c_1e^{m_1x} + c_2e^{m_2x} \\ &= c_1e^{(\alpha + \beta i)x} + c_2e^{(\alpha - \beta i)x} \\ &= e^{\alpha x} (c_1e^{i(\beta x)} + c_2e^{i(-\beta x)}) \\ &= e^{\alpha x} \Big[ c_1(\cos(\beta x) + i\sin(\beta x)) + c_2(\cos(-\beta x) + i\sin(-\beta x)) \Big] \\ &= e^{\alpha x} \Big[ (c_1 + c_2)\cos(\beta x) + (c_1i - c_2i)\sin(\beta x) \Big] \\ &= e^{\alpha x} \Big[ k_1\cos(\beta x) + k_2\sin(\beta x) \Big] \\ }$
where:
$\eqalign{ k_1 = c_1 + c_2 \hspace{.5cm} \text{and} \hspace{.5cm} k_2 = i(c_1 - c_2) }$
Motivating the Guess for the Solution
When deriving the characteristic polynomial we assumed that the solution took on a particular form, but you have to wonder why specifically the exponential function and not some other function. To explain this I am going to make use of a particular operator and its associated eigenfunctions and eigenvalues. Take the derivative operator and setup the following relationship:
$\eqalign{ \frac{d}{dx} f(x) = \lambda f(x) }$
where $f(x)$ and $\lambda$ are known as the $\textit{eigenfunction}$ and $\textit{eigenvalue}$ respectively associated to the derivative operator. Now using techniques from first order separable differential equations the eigenfunction takes on the form:
$\eqalign{ \frac{df}{dx} &= \lambda f \\ \frac{df}{f} &= \lambda dx \\ \int \frac{df}{f} &= \int \lambda dx \\ \ln|f| &= \lambda x \\ f(x) &= e^{\lambda x} }$
where the constant of integration is ignored since by definition and multiple of an eigenfunction is also an eigenfunction. So now we have the eigenfunction and eigenvalue to the derivative operator, using this take the differential equation and rewrite it:
$\eqalign{ 0 &= y'' + ay' + by \\ &= \frac{d^2}{dx^2}y + a\frac{d}{dx}y + by \\ &= \frac{d}{dx}\Big( \frac{d}{dx}y \Big) + a\frac{d}{dx}y + by \\ &= \frac{d}{dx} (\lambda y) + a(\lambda y) + by \\ &= \lambda\frac{d}{dx}y + a\lambda y + by \\ &= \lambda^2 y + a\lambda y + by \\ &= (\lambda^2 + a\lambda + b)y \\ &= \lambda^2 + a\lambda + b }$
which provides us with the characteristic polynomial. Now we know that the characterisitc polynomial actually provides us with the eigenvalues to our guess for the solution which is the associated eigenfunction.
