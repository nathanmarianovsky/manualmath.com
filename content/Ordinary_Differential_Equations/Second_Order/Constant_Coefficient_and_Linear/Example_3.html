Problem
Given the differential equation:
$\eqalign{ 4y'' - 5y' = 0 }$
where:
$\eqalign{ y(-2) = 0 \hspace{.5cm} \text{and} \hspace{.5cm} y'(-2) = 7 }$
determine the particular solution.
Solution
Since this is a homogeneous linear constant coefficient equation the general solution will be given by the homogeneous solution. First we assume that:
$\eqalign{ y(x) = e^{mx} }$
and plug in:
$\eqalign{ 0 &= 4y'' - 5y' \\ 0 &= 4m^2e^{mx} - 5me^{mx} \\ 0 &= 4m^2 - 5m \\ 0 &= m(4m - 5) \\ m &= 0, \frac{5}{4} }$
to get two real distinct values for $m$. With this the homogeneous solution is given by:
$\eqalign{ y_h(x) = c_1 + c_2e^{\frac{5}{4}x} }$
where the first derivative is:
$\eqalign{ y_h'(x) = \frac{5}{4}c_2e^{\frac{5}{4}x} }$
Now using the initial conditions:
$\eqalign{ y_h(-2) &= c_1 + c_2e^{\frac{5}{4}(-2)} = 0 \\ y_h'(-2) &= \frac{5}{4}c_2e^{\frac{5}{4}(-2)} = 7 }$
From the second equation:
$\eqalign{ c_2 = \frac{28}{5}e^{\frac{5}{2}} }$
and from the first equation:
$\eqalign{ c_1 &= -c_2e^{-\frac{5}{2}} \\ &= -\frac{28}{5} }$
With this the particular solution is written down as:
$\eqalign{ y(x) = -\frac{28}{5} + \frac{28}{5}e^{\frac{15}{4}x} }$
