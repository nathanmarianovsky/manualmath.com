Problem
Given the differential equation:
$\eqalign{ y'' + 9y = x^3 }$
where:
$\eqalign{ y(0) = 0 \hspace{.5cm} \text{and} \hspace{.5cm} y'(0) = 0 }$
determine the particular solution.
Solution
Since this is a non-homogeneous linear constant coefficient equation the general solution will be given by the addition of the homogeneous and non-homogeneous solutions. First we assume that:
$\eqalign{ y(x) = e^{mx} }$
and plug in to solve the homogeneous portion:
$\eqalign{ 0 &= y'' + 9y \\ 0 &= m^2e^{mx} + 9e^{mx} \\ 0 &= m^2 + 9 \\ 0 &= (m + 3i)(m - 3o) \\ m &= \pm 3i }$
to get two complex distinct values for $m$. With this the homogeneous solution is given by:
$\eqalign{ y_h(x) = c_1\cos(3x) + c_2\sin(3x) }$
Now for the non-homogeneous solution, using the method of undetermined coefficients we will guess that the solution takes on the form:
$\eqalign{ y_{nh}(x) = Ax^3 + Bx^2 + Cx + D }$
Plugging into the differential equation gives:
$\eqalign{ x^3 &= y_{nh}'' + 9y_{nh} \\ x^3 &= (6Ax + 2B) + 9(Ax^3 + Bx^2 + Cx + D) \\ x^3 &= 9Ax^3 + 9Bx^2 + (6A + 9C)x + (2B + 9D) \\ A &= \frac{1}{9} \\ B &= 0 \\ C &= \frac{2}{27} \\ D &= 0 }$
With this the general solution is given by:
$\eqalign{ y(x) = c_1\cos(3x) + c_2\sin(3x) + \frac{1}{9}x^3 + \frac{2}{27}x }$
where the first derivative is:
$\eqalign{ y'(x) = -3c_1\sin(3x) + 3c_2\cos(3x) + \frac{1}{3}x^2 + \frac{2}{27} }$
Now using the initial conditions:
$\eqalign{ y(0) &= c_1\cos(3(0)) + c_2\sin(3(0)) + \frac{1}{9}(0)^3 + \frac{2}{27}(0) = 0 \\ y'(0) &= -3c_1\sin(3(0)) + 3c_2\cos(3(0)) + \frac{1}{3}(0)^2 + \frac{2}{27} = 0 }$
From the equations:
$\eqalign{ c_1 &= 0 \\ c_2 &= -\frac{2}{81} }$
With this the particular solution is written down as:
$\eqalign{ y(x) = -\frac{2}{81}\sin(3x) + \frac{1}{9}x^3 + \frac{2}{27}x }$
