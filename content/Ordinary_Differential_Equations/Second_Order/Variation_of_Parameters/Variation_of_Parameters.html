Setup
Given a non-homogeneous linear second order differential equation:
$\eqalign{ y'' + p(x)y' + q(x)y = f(x) }$
the non-homogeneous solution can always be written down as:
$\eqalign{ y_{nh} = v_1y_1 + v_2y_2 }$
where:
$\eqalign{ y_h = c_1y_1 + c_2y_2 \hspace{.5cm} \text{and} \hspace{.5cm} c_1,c_2 \in \mathbb{R} }$
In order to determine the functions $v_1$ and $v_2$, the following system of equations has to be satisfied:
$\eqalign{ v_1'y_1 + v_2'y_2 &= 0 \\ v_1'y_1' + v_2'y_2' &= f(x) }$
Solving the System with the Wronskian
Looking at the system of equations shows that we can rewrite it in matrix form as:
$\eqalign{ \begin{pmatrix} y_1 & y_2 \\ y_1' & y_2' \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} v_1' \\ v_2' \end{pmatrix} &= \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ f(x) \end{pmatrix} }$
and using the inverse the system can be solved as:
$\eqalign{ \frac{d}{dx} \begin{pmatrix} v_1 \\ v_2 \end{pmatrix} &= \frac{1}{W(y_1,y_2)} \begin{pmatrix} y_2' & -y_2 \\ -y_1' & y_1 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ f(x) \end{pmatrix} \\ \begin{pmatrix} v_1 \\ v_2 \end{pmatrix} &= \int \frac{1}{W(y_1,y_2)} \begin{pmatrix} y_2' & -y_2 \\ -y_1' & y_1 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ f(x) \end{pmatrix} dx }$
where:
$\eqalign{ W(y_1,y_2) = y_1y_2' - y_2y_1' }$
is the Wronskian.
Deriving the Equations
Taking the differential equation:
$\eqalign{ y'' + p(x)y' + q(x)y = f(x) }$
we first assume:
$\eqalign{ y_{nh} = v_1y_1 + v_2y_2 }$
where $y_1$ and $y_2$ are the two independent solutions to the homogeneous equation. Differentiation once gives:
$\eqalign{ y_{nh}' = v_1'y_1 + v_1y_1' + v_2'y_2 + v_2y_2' }$
Now we choose to let:
$\eqalign{ v_1'y_1 + v_2'y_2 = 0 }$
which is the first of our equations and by differentiating once more:
$\eqalign{ y_{nh}' &= v_1y_1' + v_2y_2' \\ y_{nh}'' &= v_1'y_1' + v_1y_1'' + v_2'y_2' + v_2y_2'' }$
and plugging into the differential equation:
$\eqalign{ \require{cancel} f(x) &= y_{nh}'' + p(x)y_{nh}' + q(x)y_{nh} \\ &= (v_1'y_1' + v_1y_1'' + v_2'y_2' + v_2y_2'') + p(x)(v_1y_1' + v_2y_2') + q(x)(v_1y_1 + v_2y_2) \\ &= v_1'y_1' + v_1\cancelto{0}{(y_1'' + p(x)y_1' + q(x)y_1)} + v_2'y_2' + v_2\cancelto{0}{(y_2'' + p(x)y_2' + q(x)y_2)} \\ &= v_1'y_1' + v_2'y_2' }$
provides us with the second equation to the system. Note that unlike the method of undetermined coefficients or the annihilator method, variation of parameters is far more generalized since it could apply to any linear second order equation.
