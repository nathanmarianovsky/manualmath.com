Problem
Given the differential equation:
$\eqalign{ y'' - 3y' - 40y = \sin(2x) }$
determine the general solution using the annihilator method.
Solution
We begin by noting that the annihilator operator from the table is going to be given by:
$\eqalign{ A(D) = D^2 + 4 }$
Now rewriting the differential equation using operators and applying the annihilator to both sides gives:
$\eqalign{ \sin(2x) &= y'' - 3y' - 40y \\ \sin(2x) &= (D^2 - 3D - 40)y \\ (D^2 + 4)\sin(2x) &= (D^2 + 4)(D^2 - 3D - 40)y \\ 0 &= (D^2 + 4)(D^2 - 3D - 40)y }$
With this we are left with solving the characteristic polynomial:
$\eqalign{ 0 &= (\lambda^2 + 4)(\lambda^2 - 3\lambda - 40) \\ 0 &= (\lambda + 2i)(\lambda - 2i)(\lambda + 5)(\lambda - 8) \\ \lambda &= -5, 8, \pm 2i }$
Knowing this we can write down the fundamental set of solutions as:
$\eqalign{ \mathcal{S} = \{ e^{-5x}, e^{8x}, \sin(2x), \cos(2x) \} }$
We now form our general solution as:
$\eqalign{ y(x) = c_1e^{-5x} + c_2e^{8x} + c_3\sin(2x) + c_4\cos(2x) }$
and plug into the differential equation to determine the constants for the non-homogeneous component:
$\eqalign{ \sin(2x) &= y'' - 3y' - 40y \\ \sin(2x) &= (25c_1e^{-5x} + 64c_2e^{8x} - 4c_3\sin(2x) - 4c_4\cos(2x)) \\ & \hspace{.4cm} - 3(-5c_1e^{-5x} + 8c_2e^{8x} + 2c_3\cos(2x) - 2c_4\sin(2x)) \\ & \hspace{.4cm} - 40(c_1e^{-5x} + c_2e^{8x} + c_3\sin(2x) + c_4\cos(2x)) \\ \sin(2x) &= (-44c_3 + 6c_4)\sin(2x) + (-6c_3 - 44c_4)\cos(2x) }$
which can be rewritten in matrix form and using the inverse a unique solution can be obtained:
$\eqalign{ \begin{pmatrix} -44 & 6 \\ -6 & -44 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} c_3 \\ c_4 \end{pmatrix} &= \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} \\ \begin{pmatrix} c_3 \\ c_4 \end{pmatrix} &= \frac{1}{1972} \begin{pmatrix} -44 & -6 \\ 6 & -44 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} \\ &= \begin{pmatrix} -\frac{44}{1972} \\ \frac{6}{1972} \end{pmatrix} }$
With this the general solution is given by:
$\eqalign{ y(x) = c_1e^{-5x} + c_2e^{8x} - \frac{11}{493}\sin(2x) + \frac{3}{986}\cos(2x) }$
