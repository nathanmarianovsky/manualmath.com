Problem
Prove that the annihilator operator for:
$\eqalign{ f(x) = k_1\cosh(\alpha x) + k_2\sinh(\alpha x) \hspace{.5cm} \text{where} \hspace{.5cm} k_1,k_2,\alpha \in \mathbb{R} }$
is given by:
$\eqalign{ A(D) = D^2 - \alpha^2 }$
Solution
The easiest way to prove is to simply plug in to show that it goes to zero:
$\eqalign{ A(D)f(x) &= (D^2 - \alpha^2)(k_1\cosh(\alpha x) + k_2\sinh(\alpha x)) \\ &= D^2(k_1\cosh(\alpha x) + k_2\sinh(\alpha x)) - \alpha^2(k_1\cosh(\alpha x) + k_2\sinh(\alpha x)) \\ &= \alpha^2(k_1\cosh(\alpha x) + k_2\sinh(\alpha x)) - \alpha^2(k_1\cosh(\alpha x) + k_2\sinh(\alpha x)) \\ &= 0 }$
