Problem
Given the differential equation:
$\eqalign{ y'' + 9y = 0 }$
and a solution:
$\eqalign{ y_1(x) = \sin(3x) }$
Use reduction of order to determine a second solution.
Solution
For reduction of order we have the setup:
$\eqalign{ y_2 &= v\sin(3x) \\ y_2' &= v'\sin(3x) + 3v\cos(3x) \\ y_2'' &= v''\sin(3x) + 6v'\cos(3x) - 9v\sin(3x) }$
Plugging this in gives:
$\eqalign{ 0 &= y_2'' + 9y_2 \\ 0 &= (v''\sin(3x) + 6v'\cos(3x) - 9v\sin(3x)) + 9v\sin(3x) \\ 0 &= v''\sin(3x) + 6v'\cos(3x) }$
Now make the substitution:
$\eqalign{ w = v' }$
and continue:
$\eqalign{ 0 &= v''\sin(3x) + 6v'\cos(3x) \\ 0 &= w'\sin(3x) + 6w\cos(3x) \\ \frac{dw}{dx}\sin(3x) &= -6w\cos(3x) \\ \frac{dw}{w} &= -\frac{6\cos(3x)}{\sin(3x)} dx \\ \int \frac{dw}{w} &= \int -\frac{6\cos(3x)}{\sin(3x)} dx \\ \ln|w| &= -2\ln|\sin(3x)| + C \\ w &= D_1\csc^2(3x) }$
Now to solve for $v$:
$\eqalign{ v' &= w \\ \frac{dv}{dx} &= D_1\csc^2(3x) \\ \int dv &= D_1\int \csc^2(3x) dx \\ v &= -\frac{1}{3}D_1\cot(3x) + D_2 }$
With this the second solution takes on the form:
$\eqalign{ y_2 = v\sin(3x) = \Big( -\frac{1}{3}D_1\cot(3x) + D_2 \Big)\sin(3x) = -\frac{D_1}{3}\cos(3x) + D_2\sin(3x) }$
This seems to take on the form of the homogeneous solution, but so if we want the second solution that would be part of the fundamental set it actually is:
$\eqalign{ y_2 = \cos(3x) }$
