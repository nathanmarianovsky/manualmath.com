Problem
Given the differential equation:
$\eqalign{ x^2y'' + 2xy' - 6y = 0 }$
and a solution:
$\eqalign{ y_1(x) = x^2 }$
Use reduction of order to determine a second solution.
Solution
For reduction of order we have the setup:
$\eqalign{ y_2 &= vx^2 \\ y_2' &= v'x^2 + 2vx \\ y_2'' &= v''x^2 + 4v'x + 2v }$
Plugging this in gives:
$\eqalign{ 0 &= x^2y_2'' + 2xy_2' - 6y \\ 0 &= x^2(v''x^2 + 4v'x + 2v) + 2x(v'x^2 + 2vx) - 6vx^2 \\ 0 &= v''x^4 + 6v'x^3 }$
Now make the substitution:
$\eqalign{ w = v' }$
and continue:
$\eqalign{ 0 &= v''x^4 + 6v'x^3 \\ 0 &= w'x^4 + 6wx^3 \\ \frac{dw}{dx}x^4 &= -6wx^3 \\ \frac{dw}{w} &= -\frac{6}{x} dx \\ \int \frac{dw}{w} &= \int -\frac{6}{x} dx \\ \ln|w| &= -6\ln|x| + C \\ w &= D_1x^{-6} }$
Now to solve for $v$:
$\eqalign{ v' &= w \\ \frac{dv}{dx} &= D_1x^{-6} \\ \int dv &= D_1\int x^{-6} dx \\ v &= \frac{D_1}{-5} x^{-5} + D_2 }$
With this the second solution takes on the form:
$\eqalign{ y_2 = vx^2 = \Big( \frac{D_1}{-5} x^{-5} + D_2 \Big)x^2 = -\frac{D_1}{5}x^{-3} + D_2x^2 }$
This seems to take on the form of the homogeneous solution, but so if we want the second solution that would be part of the fundamental set it actually is:
$\eqalign{ y_2 = x^{-3} }$
