Problem
Given the differential equation:
$\eqalign{ xy'' + y' = 0 }$
and a solution:
$\eqalign{ y_1(x) = \ln(x) }$
Use reduction of order to determine a second solution.
Solution
For reduction of order we have the setup:
$\eqalign{ y_2 &= v\ln(x) \\ y_2' &= v'\ln(x) + vx^{-1} \\ y_2'' &= v''\ln(x) + 2v'x^{-1} - vx^{-2} }$
Plugging this in gives:
$\eqalign{ 0 &= xy_2'' + y_2' \\ 0 &= x(v''\ln(x) + 2v'x^{-1} - vx^{-2}) + (v'\ln(x) + vx^{-1}) \\ 0 &= v''x\ln(x) + v'(\ln(x) + 2) }$
Now make the substitution:
$\eqalign{ w = v' }$
and continue:
$\eqalign{ 0 &= v''x\ln(x) + v'(\ln(x) + 2) \\ 0 &= w'x\ln(x) + w(\ln(x) + 2) \\ \frac{dw}{dx}x\ln(x) &= -w(\ln(x) + 2) \\ \frac{dw}{w} &= -\frac{\ln(x) + 2}{x\ln(x)} dx \\ \int \frac{dw}{w} &= -\int \Big( \frac{1}{x} + \frac{2}{x\ln(x)} \Big) dx \\ \ln|w| &= -\ln|x| - 2\ln|\ln(x)| + C \\ w &= \frac{D_1}{x\ln^2(x)} }$
where dropping the absolute value is allowed since the original domain is already restricted to the positive real numbers due to the first solution. Now to solve for $v$:
$\eqalign{ v' &= w \\ \frac{dv}{dx} &= \frac{D_1}{x\ln^2(x)} \\ \int dv &= D_1\int \frac{1}{x\ln^2(x)} dx \\ v &= -\frac{D_1}{\ln(x)} + D_2 }$
With this the second solution takes on the form:
$\eqalign{ y_2 = vx^2 = \Big( -\frac{D_1}{\ln(x)} + D_2 \Big)\ln(x) = -D_1 + D_2\ln(x) }$
This seems to take on the form of the homogeneous solution, but so if we want the second solution that would be part of the fundamental set it actually is:
$\eqalign{ y_2 = 1 }$
