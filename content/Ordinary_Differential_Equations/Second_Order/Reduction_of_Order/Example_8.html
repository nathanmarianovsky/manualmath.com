Problem
Given the differential equation:
$\eqalign{ y'' + ay' + by = 0 }$
where:
$\eqalign{ a,b \in \mathbb{R} }$
and a solution:
$\eqalign{ y_1(x) = e^{mx} }$
Use reduction of order to determine a second solution. What does the solution look like when $2m + a = 0$? How about when $m \in \mathbb{C}$?
Solution
For reduction of order we have the setup:
$\eqalign{ y_2 &= ve^{mx} \\ y_2' &= v'e^{mx} + mve^{mx} \\ y_2'' &= v''e^{mx} + 2mv'e^{mx} + m^2ve^{mx} }$
Plugging this in gives:
$\eqalign{ 0 &= y_2'' + ay_2' + by_2 \\ 0 &= (v''e^{mx} + 2mv'e^{mx} + m^2ve^{mx}) + a(v'e^{mx} + mve^{mx}) + bve^{mx} \\ 0 &= e^{mx}\Big[ v'' + v'(2m + a) + v(y_1'' + ay_1' + by_1) \Big] \\ 0 &= v'' + v'(2m + a) }$
Now make the substitution:
$\eqalign{ w = v' }$
and continue:
$\eqalign{ 0 &= v'' + v'(2m + a) \\ 0 &= w' + w(2m + a) \\ \frac{dw}{dx} &= -w(2m + a) \\ \frac{dw}{w} &= -(2m + a) dx \\ \int \frac{dw}{w} &= \int -(2m + a) dx \\ \ln|w| &= -(2m + a)x + C \\ w &= D_1e^{-(2m + a)x} }$
Now to solve for $v$:
$\eqalign{ v' &= w \\ \frac{dv}{dx} &= D_1e^{-(2m + a)x} \\ \int dv &= D_1\int e^{-(2m + a)x} dx \\ v &= \frac{D_1}{-(2m + a)}e^{-(2m + a)x} + D_2 }$
With this the second solution takes on the form:
$\eqalign{ y_2 = ve^{mx} = \Big( \frac{D_1}{-(2m + a)}e^{-(2m + a)x} + D_2 \Big)e^{mx} = \frac{D_1}{-(2m + a)}e^{-(m + a)x} + D_2e^{mx} }$
This seems to take on the form of the homogeneous solution, but so if we want the second solution that would be part of the fundamental set it actually is:
$\eqalign{ y_2 = e^{-(m + a)x} }$
Of course this assumed that $2m + a \neq 0$. If this was not the case, then:
$\eqalign{ v' &= w \\ \frac{dv}{dx} &= D_1 \\ \int dv &= D_1\int dx \\ v &= D_1x + D_2 }$
With this the second solution takes on the form:
$\eqalign{ y_2 = ve^{mx} = \Big( D_1x + D_2 \Big)e^{mx} = D_1xe^{mx} + D_2e^{mx} }$
This seems to take on the form of the homogeneous solution, but so if we want the second solution that would be part of the fundamental set it actually is:
$\eqalign{ y_2 = xe^x }$
Lastly if $m \in \mathbb{C}$ and we let $m = k + li$ where $l \neq 0$, then $m + a \in \mathbb{C}$. This implies that the original solution when assuming that $2m + a \neq 0$ will remain the same except the for the fact that the constant will be imaginary instead of real.
