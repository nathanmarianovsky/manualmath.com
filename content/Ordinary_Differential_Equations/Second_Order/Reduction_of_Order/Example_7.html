Problem
Given the differential equation:
$\eqalign{ xy'' - xy' + y = 0 }$
verify that:
$\eqalign{ y_1(x) = x }$
is a solution and use reduction of order to determine a second solution in the form of an infinite series.
Solution
First to verify:
$\eqalign{ 0 &= xy_1'' - xy_1' + y_1 \\ 0 &= x(0) - x(1) + (x) \\ 0 &= 0 }$
Now for reduction of order we have the setup:
$\eqalign{ y_2 &= vx \\ y_2' &= v'x + v \\ y_2'' &= v''x + 2v' }$
Plugging this in gives:
$\eqalign{ 0 &= xy_2'' - xy_2' + y_2 \\ 0 &= x(v''x + 2v') - x(v'x + v) + vx \\ 0 &= v''x^2 + v'(2x - x^2) }$
Now make the substitution:
$\eqalign{ w = v' }$
and continue:
$\eqalign{ 0 &= v''x^2 + v'(2x - x^2) \\ 0 &= w'x^2 + w(2x - x^2) \\ \frac{dw}{dx}x^2 &= w(x^2 - 2x) \\ \frac{dw}{w} &= 1 - \frac{2}{x} dx \\ \int \frac{dw}{w} &= \int \Big( 1 - \frac{2}{x} \Big) dx \\ \ln|w| &= x - 2\ln|x| + C \\ w &= D_1\frac{e^x}{x^2} }$
Now to solve for $v$:
$\eqalign{ v' &= w \\ \frac{dv}{dx} &= D_1\frac{e^x}{x^2} \\ \int dv &= D_1\int \frac{e^x}{x^2} dx \\ v &= D_1\int \Bigg( \sum_{n = 0}^\infty \frac{x^{n - 2}}{n!} \Bigg) dx \\ &= \sum_{n = 0}^\infty \frac{D_1}{n!} \int x^{n - 2} dx \\ &= \sum_{n = 0}^\infty \frac{D_1}{(n - 1)n!} x^{n - 1} + D_2 }$
where the interchange of the order with the summation and integration is allowed so long as we are inside the radius of convergence. With this the second solution takes on the form:
$\eqalign{ y_2 = vx = \Big( \sum_{n = 0}^\infty \frac{D_1}{(n - 1)n!} x^{n - 1} + D_2 \Big)x = \sum_{n = 0}^\infty \frac{D_1}{(n - 1)n!} x^n + D_2x }$
This seems to take on the form of the homogeneous solution, but so if we want the second solution that would be part of the fundamental set it actually is:
$\eqalign{ y_2 = \sum_{n = 0}^\infty \frac{x^n}{(n - 1)n!} }$
