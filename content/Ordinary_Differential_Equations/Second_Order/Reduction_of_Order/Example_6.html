Problem
Given the differential equation:
$\eqalign{ y'' - 4y = 0 }$
and a solution:
$\eqalign{ y_1(x) = e^{-2x} }$
Use reduction of order to determine a second solution.
Solution
For reduction of order we have the setup:
$\eqalign{ y_2 &= ve^{-2x} \\ y_2' &= v'e^{-2x} - 2ve^{-2x} \\ y_2'' &= v''e^{-2x} - 4v'e^{-2x} + 4ve^{-2x} }$
Plugging this in gives:
$\eqalign{ 0 &= y_2'' - 4y_2 \\ 0 &= (v''e^{-2x} - 4v'e^{-2x} + 4ve^{-2x}) - 4(ve^{-2x}) \\ 0 &= (v'' - 4v')e^{-2x} \\ 0 &= v'' - 4v' }$
Now make the substitution:
$\eqalign{ w = v' }$
and continue:
$\eqalign{ 0 &= v'' - 4v' \\ 0 &= w' - 4w \\ \frac{dw}{dx} &= 4w \\ \frac{dw}{w} &= 4 dx \\ \int \frac{dw}{w} &= \int 4 dx \\ \ln|w| &= 4x + C \\ w &= D_1e^{4x} }$
Now to solve for $v$:
$\eqalign{ v' &= w \\ \frac{dv}{dx} &= D_1e^{4x} \\ \int dv &= D_1\int e^{4x} dx \\ v &= \frac{D_1}{4}e^{4x} + D_2 }$
With this the second solution takes on the form:
$\eqalign{ y_2 = ve^{-2x} = \Big( \frac{D_1}{4}e^{4x} + D_2 \Big)e^{-2x} = \frac{D_1}{4}e^{2x} + D_2e^{-2x} }$
This seems to take on the form of the homogeneous solution, but so if we want the second solution that would be part of the fundamental set it actually is:
$\eqalign{ y_2 = e^{2x} }$
