The Setup
Given a linear homogeneous second order differential equation:
$\eqalign{ y'' + p(x)y' + q(x)y = 0 }$
where $y_1(x)$ is a known solution, $\textit{reduction of order}$ provides a systematic way of obtaining the second solution.
Getting the Second Solution
Assume that the second function can be written in the form:
$\eqalign{ y_2(x) = v(x)y_1(x) }$
where $v(x)$ is an unknown function. Taking derivatives gives:
$\eqalign{ y_2 &= vy_1 \\ y_2' &= v'y_1' + vy_1' \\ y_2'' &= v''y_1 + 2v'y_1' + vy_1'' }$
and plugging into the differential equation yields:
$\eqalign{ 0 &= y_2'' + p(x)y_2' + q(x)y_2 \\ 0 &= (v''y_1 + 2v'y_1' + vy_1'') + p(x)(v'y_1' + vy_1') + q(x)(vy_1) \\ 0 &= y_1v'' + (2y_1' + p(x)y_1)v' + (y_1'' + p(x)y_1' + q(x)y_1)v \\ 0 &= y_1v'' + (2y_1' + p(x)y_1)v' }$
a differential equation that can be reduced to first order. Let:
$\eqalign{ w = v' }$
to rewrite the result as a separable first order equation:
$\eqalign{ 0 &= y_1w' + (2y_1' + p(x)y_1)w \\ y_1\frac{dw}{dx} &= -(2y_1' + p(x)y_1)w \\ \frac{dw}{w} &= -\frac{2y_1' + p(x)y_1}{y_1} dx \\ \int \frac{dw}{w} &= \int -\frac{2y_1'(x) + p(x)y_1(x)}{y_1(x)} dx \\ \ln|w| &= K(x) + C \\ w &= D_1e^{K(x)} \\ }$
where:
$\eqalign{ K(x) = \int -\frac{2y_1'(x) + p(x)y_1(x)}{y_1(x)} dx \hspace{.5cm} \text{and} \hspace{.5cm} D_1 = e^C }$
Now to solve for $v$:
$\eqalign{ v' &= w \\ \frac{dv}{dx} &= w \\ dv &= D_1e^{K(x)} dx \\ \int dv &= \int D_1e^{K(x)} dx \\ v &= D_1\int e^{K(x)} dx + D_2 }$
With this the second solution can be written down as:
$\eqalign{ y_2(x) = v(x)y_1(x) = D_1y_1(x)\int e^{K(x)} dx + D_2y_1(x) }$
where:
$\eqalign{ D_1,D_2 \in \mathbb{R} }$
