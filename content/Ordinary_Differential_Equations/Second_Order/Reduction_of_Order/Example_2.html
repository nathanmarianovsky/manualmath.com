Problem
Given the differential equation:
$\eqalign{ y'' - y = 0 }$
and a solution:
$\eqalign{ \newcommand{\sech}{\mathop{\rm sech}\nolimits} y_1(x) = \cosh(x) }$
Use reduction of order to determine a second solution.
Solution
For reduction of order we have the setup:
$\eqalign{ y_2 &= v\cosh(x) \\ y_2' &= v'\cosh(x) + v\sinh(x) \\ y_2'' &= v''\cosh(x) + 2v'\sinh(x) + v\cosh(x) }$
Plugging this in gives:
$\eqalign{ 0 &= y_2'' - y_2 \\ 0 &= (v''\cosh(x) + 2v'\sinh(x) + v\cosh(x)) - v\cosh(x) \\ 0 &= v''\cosh(x) + 2v'\sinh(x) \\ }$
Now make the substitution:
$\eqalign{ w = v' }$
and continue:
$\eqalign{ 0 &= v''\cosh(x) + 2v'\sinh(x) \\ 0 &= w'\cosh(x) + 2w\sinh(x) \\ \frac{dw}{dx}\cosh(x) &= -2w\sinh(x) \\ \frac{dw}{w} &= -2\tanh(x) dx \\ \int \frac{dw}{w} &= \int -2\tanh(x) dx \\ \ln|w| &= -2\ln|\cosh(x)| + C \\ w &= D_1\sech^2(x) }$
Now to solve for $v$:
$\eqalign{ v' &= w \\ \frac{dv}{dx} &= D_1\sech^2(x) \\ \int dv &= D_1\int \sech^2(x) dx \\ v &= D_1\tanh(x) + D_2 }$
With this the second solution takes on the form:
$\eqalign{ y_2 = v\cosh(x) = D_1\sinh(x) + D_2\cosh(x) }$
This seems to take on the form of the homogeneous solution, but so if we want the second solution that would be part of the fundamental set it actually is:
$\eqalign{ y_2 = \sinh(x) }$
