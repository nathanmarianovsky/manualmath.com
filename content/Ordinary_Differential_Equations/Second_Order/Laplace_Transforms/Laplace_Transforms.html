Definition and Properties
The Laplace transform of any given function is defined as:
$\eqalign{ \mathcal{L}[f(x)] = \int_0^\infty f(x)e^{-sx} dx }$
which satisfies being a linear operator:
$\eqalign{ \mathcal{L}[k_1f_1(x) + k_2f_2(x)] &= \int_0^\infty (k_1f_1(x) + k_2f_2(x))e^{-sx} dx \\ &= k_1\int_0^\infty f_1(x)e^{-sx} dx + k_2\int_0^\infty f_2(x)e^{-sx} dx \\ &= k_1\mathcal{L}[f_1(x)] + k_2\mathcal{L}[f_2(x)] }$
Inverse Transform
The inverse Laplace transform of any given function can be formally computed using a known formula. Unfortunately, the formula includes doing a line integral over the complex plane which is not something that most people want to spend their time doing. Instead the way we commonly use the inverse transform is by looking at a table and just simply work backwards. As a last remark, just like the original transform, the inverse is also a linear operator when using the known fact that the original transform is linear:
$\eqalign{ \mathcal{L}^{-1}\Big[ k_1\mathcal{L}[f_1(x)] + k_2\mathcal{L}[f_2(x)] \Big] &= \mathcal{L}^{-1}\Big[ \mathcal{L}[k_1f_1(x) + k_2f_2(x)] \Big] \\ \mathcal{L}^{-1}\Big[ k_1\mathcal{L}[f_1(x)] + k_2\mathcal{L}[f_2(x)] \Big] &= k_1f_1(x) + k_2f_2(x) \\ \mathcal{L}^{-1}\Big[ k_1\mathcal{L}[f_1(x)] + k_2\mathcal{L}[f_2(x)] \Big] &= k_1\mathcal{L}^{-1}\Big[ \mathcal{L}[f_1(x)] \Big] + k_2\mathcal{L}^{-1}\Big[ \mathcal{L}[f_2(x)] \Big] \\ }$
Knowing the fact that the transform and its inverse are linear operators has nice results. The most important is that linear operators are injective, one-to-one, and thus guarantee that if we are going to be using either transform, the result is going to be unique. This is good to keep in mind when using specifically the inverse transform and the table of common transforms.
Application Towards Differential Equations
The Laplace transform provides a systematic way of changing a differential equation into an algebraic equation. First we need:
$\eqalign{ \mathcal{L}[f'(x)] &= \int_0^\infty f'(x)e^{-sx} dx \\ &= f(x)e^{-sx} \Big|_0^\infty + s\int_0^\infty f(x)e^{-sx} dx \\ &= -f(0) + s\mathcal{L}[f(x)] }$
where integration by parts was used with the assumption that at large values of $x$ the function is dominated by the exponential in order to converge. In other words we have the condition:
$\eqalign{ \exists M \in \mathbb{R}, a \in \mathbb{C} \hspace{.5cm} \text{s.t.} \hspace{.5cm} f(x) \neq Me^{at} }$
This can now be used to define:
$\eqalign{ \mathcal{L}[f''(x)] &= -f'(0) + s\mathcal{L}[f'(x)] \\ &= -f'(0) + s\Big( -f(0) + s\mathcal{L}[f(x)] \Big) \\ &= -f'(0) - sf(0) + s^2\mathcal{L}[f(x)] }$
Now say we have a constant coefficient linear second order equation:
$\eqalign{ y'' + ay' + by = g(x) }$
Apply the transform to both sides and isolate the transform of $y$:
$\eqalign{ g(x) &= y'' + ay' + by \\ \mathcal{L}[g(x)] &= \mathcal{L}[y'' + ay' + by] \\ \mathcal{L}[g(x)] &= \mathcal{L}[y''] + a\mathcal{L}[y'] + b\mathcal{L}[y] \\ \mathcal{L}[g(x)] &= (-y'(0) - sy(0) + s^2\mathcal{L}[y]) + a(-y(0) + s\mathcal{L}[y]) + b\mathcal{L}[y] \\ \mathcal{L}[g(x)] + y'(0) + (s + a)y(0) &= (s^2 + as + b)\mathcal{L}[y] \\ \mathcal{L}[y] &= \frac{\mathcal{L}[g(x)] + y'(0) + (s + a)y(0)}{s^2 + as + b} \\ y &= \mathcal{L}^{-1}\Bigg[ \frac{\mathcal{L}[g(x)] + y'(0) + (s + a)y(0)}{s^2 + as + b} \Bigg] }$
where in the last step the inverse transform is used in order to obtain the original solution. Notice that a consequence of this method is that it requires us to know ahead of time what $y(0)$ and $y'(0)$ are, so Laplace transforms can only be used if we have the initial conditions. At the same time I applied this to a constant coefficient equation, but the transform can definitely be used for other scenarios.
