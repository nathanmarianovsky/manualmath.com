Problem
Suppose that:
$\eqalign{ y_1 &= e^x \\ y_2 &= e^{-x} }$
are two solutions of a homogeneous linear second order differential equation. Explain why:
$\eqalign{ y_3 &= \cosh(x) \\ y_4 &= \sinh(x) }$
are also solutions of the equation.
Solution
Since we are dealing with a homogeneous linear equation, the general solution is given by the homogeneous solution:
$\eqalign{ y_h = c_1e^x + c_2e^{-x} }$
In comparison:
$\eqalign{ \cosh(x) &= \frac{e^x + e^{-x}}{2} \\ \sinh(x) &= \frac{e^x - e^{-x}}{2} }$
which gives:
$\eqalign{ e^x = \cosh(x) + \sinh(x) \\ e^-x = \cosh(x) - \sinh(x) }$
Now plugging into the homogeneous solution:
$\eqalign{ y_h &= c_1e^x + c_2e^{-x} \\ &= c_1(\cosh(x) + \sinh(x)) + c_2(\cosh(x) - \sinh(x)) \\ &= (c_1 + c_2)\cosh(x) + (c_1 - c_2)\sinh(x) \\ &= k_1\cosh(x) + k_2\sinh(x) }$
provides the solution as the combination of two independent solutions, $\cosh(x)$ and $\sinh(x)$, where:
$\eqalign{ k_1 = c_1 + c_2 \hspace{.3cm} \text{and} \hspace{.3cm} k_2 = c_1 - c_2 }$
