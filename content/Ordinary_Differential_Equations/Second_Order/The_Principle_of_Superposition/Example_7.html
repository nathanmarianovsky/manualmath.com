Problem
Given the set of functions:
$\eqalign{ \mathcal{S} = \{ \cos(\ln(x)), \sin(\ln(x)) \} }$
Determine whether $\mathcal{S}$ satisfies as a fundamental set for the differential equation:
$\eqalign{ x^2y'' + xy' + y = 0 }$
Solution
First we have to check for the independence of the functions by looking at the Wronskian:
$\eqalign{ W(y_1, y_2) &= \det \begin{pmatrix} y_1 & y_2 \\ y_1' & y_2' \end{pmatrix} \\ &= \det \begin{pmatrix} \cos(\ln(x)) & \sin(\ln(x)) \\ -\frac{\sin(\ln(x))}{x} & \frac{\cos(\ln(x))}{x} \end{pmatrix} \\ &= \frac{\cos^2(\ln(x))}{x} + \frac{\sin^2(\ln(x))}{x} \\ &= \frac{1}{x} }$
With their independence assured, we form the homogeneous solution:
$\eqalign{ y_h = c_1\cos(\ln(x)) + c_2\sin(\ln(x)) }$
and check that it satisfies the differential equation:
$\eqalign{ 0 &= x^2y_h'' + xy_h' + y_h \\ 0 &= x^2(c_1\cos(\ln(x)) + c_2\sin(\ln(x)))'' \\ & \hspace{.4cm} + x(c_1\cos(\ln(x)) + c_2\sin(\ln(x)))' \\ & \hspace{.4cm} + (c_1\cos(\ln(x)) + c_2\sin(\ln(x))) \\ 0 &= x^2\Big( c_1\frac{\sin(\ln(x)) - \cos(\ln(x))}{x^2} + c_2\frac{-\sin(\ln(x)) - \cos(\ln(x))}{x^2} \Big) \\ & \hspace{.4cm} x\Big( -c_1\frac{\sin(\ln(x))}{x} + c_2\frac{\cos(\ln(x))}{x} \Big) \\ & \hspace{.4cm} (c_1\cos(\ln(x)) + c_2\sin(\ln(x))) \\ 0 &= 0 }$
