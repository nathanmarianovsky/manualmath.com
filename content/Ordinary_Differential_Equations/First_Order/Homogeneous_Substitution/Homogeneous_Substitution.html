The Setup
Given a differential equation in the form:
$\eqalign{ y' = f(x,y) }$
where $y'$ denotes the first derivative, an exact solution can be obtained with the use of a substitution if the function is homogeneous of order zero.
Homogeneous Functions
Before tackling the differential equation, it is important to understand what homogeneous functions are. Formally any function that satisfies:
$\eqalign{ f(tx,ty) = t^n f(x,y) }$
is known as a homogeneous function of degree $n$. For the differential equation we are going to need the function to be homogeneous of degree zero thus giving the condition:
$\eqalign{ f(tx,ty) = f(x,y) }$
Solving the Equation
Now given that the function on the right hand side of the differential equation from above is homogeneous of degree zero, then make the following substitution:
$\eqalign{ y &= ux \\ y' &= u'x + u }$
so that the differential equation becomes separable:
$\eqalign{ y' &= f(x,y) \\ u'x + u &= f(x,ux) \\ u'x + u &= f(1,u) \\ u'x &= f(1,u) - u \\ \frac{du}{dx}x &= f(1,u) - u \\ \frac{du}{f(1,u) - u} &= \frac{dx}{x} \\ \int \frac{du}{f(1,u) - u} &= \int \frac{dx}{x} \\ W(u) &= \ln|x| + C \\ W(u) - \ln|x| &= C }$
where $W(u)$ denotes the antiderivative of $(f(1,u) - u)^{-1}$. To finish off consider the fact that the original equation consisted of $x$'s and $y$'s. So to get back into this form just use the fact that $u = \frac{y}{x}$:
$\eqalign{ W\Big( \frac{y}{x} \Big) - \ln|x| = C }$
Usually the solution is left in implicit form since the algebra is typically too hard to determine an explicit solution.
