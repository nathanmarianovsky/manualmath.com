Problem
Solve the differential equation:
$\eqalign{ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{x + 3y}{3x + y} }$
Solution
To see if the homogeneous substitution can be used, first lets check that the function is homogeneous with degree zero:
$\eqalign{ f(x,y) &= \frac{x + 3y}{3x + y} \\ f(tx,ty) &= \frac{(tx) + 3(ty)}{3(tx) + (ty)} \\ &= \frac{x + 3y}{3x + y} \\ &= f(x,y) }$
With the condition checked, using the substitution:
$\eqalign{ y &= ux \\ y' &= u'x + u }$
and plugging in gives:
$\eqalign{ y' &= \frac{x + 3y}{3x + y} \\ u'x + u &= \frac{x + 3(ux)}{3x + (ux)} \\ u'x + u &= \frac{1 + 3u}{3 + u} \\ \frac{du}{dx}x &= \frac{1 + 3u}{3 + u} - \frac{u(3 + u)}{3 + u} \\ \frac{du}{dx}x &= \frac{1 - u^2}{3 + u} \\ \frac{3 + u}{1 - u^2} du &= \frac{dx}{x} \\ \int \frac{3 + u}{1 - u^2} du &= \int \frac{dx}{x} \\ \ln|1 + u| - 2\ln|1 - u| &= \ln|x| + C \\ \ln\Bigg| \frac{1 + u}{(1 - u)^2} \Bigg| - \ln|x| &= C }$
To finish off consider that the original equation started with $x$'s and $y$'s, but so far we have a solution in terms of $x$'s and $u$'s. So replace all of the $u$'s to get:
$\eqalign{ \ln\Bigg| \frac{1 + \frac{y}{x}}{(1 - \frac{y}{x})^2} \Bigg| - \ln|x| &= C \\ \ln\Bigg| \frac{x^2 + xy}{(x - y)^2} \Bigg| - \ln|x| &= C }$
