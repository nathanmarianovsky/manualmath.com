Problem
Solve the differential equation:
$\eqalign{ y dx + x(\ln(x) - \ln(y) - 1) dy = 0 }$
where $y(1) = e$.
Solution
First rewriting this gives:
$\eqalign{ y dx + x(\ln(x) - \ln(y) - 1) dy &= 0 \\ x(\ln(x) - \ln(y) - 1) dy &= -y dx \\ \frac{dy}{dx} &= -\frac{y}{x(\ln(xy^{-1}) - 1)} }$
Now to see if the homogeneous substitution can be used, first lets check that the function is homogeneous with degree zero:
$\eqalign{ f(x,y) &= -\frac{y}{x(\ln(xy^{-1}) - 1)} \\ f(tx,ty) &= -\frac{(ty)}{(tx)(\ln((tx)(ty)^{-1}) - 1)} \\ &= -\frac{y}{x(\ln(xy^{-1}) - 1)} \\ &= f(x,y) }$
With the condition checked, using the substitution:
$\eqalign{ y &= ux \\ y' &= u'x + u }$
and plugging in gives:
$\eqalign{ y' &= -\frac{y}{x(\ln(xy^{-1}) - 1)} \\ u'x + u &= -\frac{ux}{x(\ln(x(ux)^{-1}) - 1)} \\ u'x + u &= \frac{u}{\ln(u) + 1} \\ \frac{du}{dx}x &= \frac{u}{\ln(u) + 1} - \frac{u(\ln(u) + 1)}{\ln(u) + 1} \\ \frac{du}{dx}x &= -\frac{u\ln(u)}{\ln(u) + 1} \\ \frac{\ln(u) + 1}{u\ln(u)} du &= -\frac{dx}{x} \\ \int \frac{\ln(u) + 1}{u\ln(u)} du &= \int -\frac{dx}{x} \\ \ln|u| + \ln|\ln(u)| &= -\ln|x| + C \\ \ln|ux| + \ln|\ln(u)| &= C }$
To finish off consider that the original equation started with $x$'s and $y$'s, but so far we have a solution in terms of $x$'s and $u$'s. So replace all of the $u$'s to get:
$\eqalign{ \ln|y| + \ln\Bigg| \ln\Big( \frac{y}{x} \Big) \Bigg| = C }$
Now using the initial condition:
$\eqalign{ C &= \ln|y| + \ln\Bigg| \ln\Big( \frac{y}{x} \Big) \Bigg| \\ &= \ln(e) + \ln\Bigg| \ln\Big( \frac{(e)}{(1)} \Big) \Bigg| \\ &= 1 }$
the final solution is written down as:
$\eqalign{ \ln|y| + \ln\Bigg| \ln\Big( \frac{y}{x} \Big) \Bigg| = 1 }$
