Problem
Given the differential equation:
$\eqalign{ (a_0x + a_1y + a_2) dx + (b_0x + b_1y + b_2) dy = 0 }$
and the transformation:
$\eqalign{ t &= x + \alpha \\ z &= y + \beta }$
where:
$\eqalign{ \alpha, \beta \in \mathbb{R} }$
determine $\alpha$ and $\beta$ in terms of the $a$'s and $b$'s s.t. the differential equation can be solved using a homogeneous substitution. You may assume that $a_0b_1 - a_1b_0 \neq 0$ in order to write down a unique solution.
Solution
First reorganizing:
$\eqalign{ \frac{dy}{dx} = -\frac{a_0x + a_1y + a_2}{b_0x + b_1y + b_2} }$
Now using the substitution:
$\eqalign{ t &= x + \alpha \implies dt = dx \\ z &= y + \beta \implies dz = dy }$
and with this the differential equation can be rewritten as:
$\eqalign{ \frac{dy}{dx} &= -\frac{a_0x + a_1y + a_2}{b_0x + b_1y + b_2} \\ \frac{dz}{dt} &= -\frac{a_0(t - \alpha) + a_1(z - \beta) + a_2}{b_0(t - \alpha) + b_1(z - \beta) + b_2} \\ &= -\frac{a_0t + a_1z + (a_2 - a_0\alpha - a_1\beta)}{b_0t + b_1z + (b_2 - b_0\alpha - b_1\beta)} }$
Now for this function to be homogeneous of degree zero we must have the constants on both the numerator and denominator disappear:
$\eqalign{ a_2 - a_0\alpha - a_1\beta &= 0 \\ b_2 - b_0\alpha - b_1\beta &= 0 }$
To solve this system of equations for $\alpha$ and $\beta$ first rewrite in matrix form:
$\eqalign{ \begin{pmatrix} a_0 & a_1 \\ b_0 & b_1 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} \alpha \\ \beta \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} a_2 \\ b_2 \end{pmatrix} }$
and use the inverse to solve the system:
$\eqalign{ \begin{pmatrix} \alpha \\ \beta \end{pmatrix} &= \frac{1}{a_0b_1 - a_1b_0} \begin{pmatrix} b_1 & -a_1 \\ -b_0 & a_0 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} a_2 \\ b_2 \end{pmatrix} \\ &= \begin{pmatrix} \frac{b_1b_2 - a_1a_2}{a_0b_1 - a_1b_0} \\ \frac{a_0a_2 - b_0b_2}{a_0b_1 - a_1b_0} \end{pmatrix} }$
