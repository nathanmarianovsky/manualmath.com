Problem
Solve the differential equation:
$\eqalign{ -y dx + (x + \sqrt{xy}) dy = 0 }$
Solution
First rewriting this gives:
$\eqalign{ -y dx + (x + \sqrt{xy}) dy &= 0 \\ (x + \sqrt{xy}) dy &= y dx \\ \frac{dy}{dx} &= \frac{y}{x + \sqrt{xy}} }$
Now to see if the homogeneous substitution can be used, first lets check that the function is homogeneous with degree zero:
$\eqalign{ f(x,y) &= \frac{y}{x + \sqrt{xy}} \\ f(tx,ty) &= \frac{(ty)}{(tx) + \sqrt{(tx)(ty)}} \\ &= \frac{y}{x + \sqrt{xy}} \\ &= f(x,y) }$
With the condition checked, using the substitution:
$\eqalign{ y &= ux \\ y' &= u'x + u }$
and plugging in gives:
$\eqalign{ y' &= \frac{y}{x + \sqrt{xy}} \\ u'x + u &= \frac{ux}{x + \sqrt{ux^2}} \\ u'x + u &= \frac{u}{1 + \sqrt{u}} \\ \frac{du}{dx}x &= \frac{u}{1 + \sqrt{u}} - \frac{u(1 + \sqrt{u})}{1 + \sqrt{u}} \\ \frac{du}{dx}x &= -\frac{u^{\frac{3}{2}}}{1 + \sqrt{u}} \\ \frac{1 + \sqrt{u}}{u^{\frac{3}{2}}} du &= -\frac{dx}{x} \\ \int \frac{1 + \sqrt{u}}{u^{\frac{3}{2}}} du &= \int -\frac{dx}{x} \\ -2u^{-\frac{1}{2}} + \ln|u| &= -\ln|x| + C \\ \ln|ux| - \frac{2}{\sqrt{u}} &= C }$
To finish off consider that the original equation started with $x$'s and $y$'s, but so far we have a solution in terms of $x$'s and $u$'s. So replace all of the $u$'s to get:
$\eqalign{ \ln|y| - \frac{2}{\sqrt{\frac{y}{x}}} = C }$
