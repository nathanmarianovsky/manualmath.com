Problem
Solve the differential equation:
$\eqalign{ (x^2 + 2y^2)\frac{dy}{dx} = xy }$
where $y(-1) = 1$.
Solution
First rewriting this gives:
$\eqalign{ (x^2 + 2y^2)\frac{dy}{dx} &= xy \\ \frac{dy}{dx} &= \frac{xy}{x^2 + 2y^2} }$
Now to see if the homogeneous substitution can be used, first lets check that the function is homogeneous with degree zero:
$\eqalign{ f(x,y) &= \frac{xy}{x^2 + 2y^2} \\ f(tx,ty) &= \frac{(tx)(ty)}{(tx)^2 + 2(ty)^2} \\ &= \frac{xy}{x^2 + 2y^2} \\ &= f(x,y) }$
With the condition checked, using the substitution:
$\eqalign{ y &= ux \\ y' &= u'x + u }$
and plugging in gives:
$\eqalign{ y' &= \frac{xy}{x^2 + 2y^2} \\ u'x + u &= \frac{x(ux)}{x^2 + 2(ux)^2} \\ u'x + u &= \frac{u}{1 + 2u^2} \\ \frac{du}{dx}x &= \frac{u}{1 + 2u^2} - \frac{u(1 + 2u^2)}{1 + 2u^2} \\ \frac{du}{dx}x &= -\frac{2u^3}{1 + 2u^2} \\ \frac{1 + 2u^2}{2u^3} du &= -\frac{dx}{x} \\ \int \frac{1 + 2u^2}{2u^3} du &= \int -\frac{dx}{x} \\ -\frac{1}{4}u^{-2} + \ln|u| &= -\ln|x| + C \\ -\frac{1}{4u^2} + \ln|ux| &= C }$
To finish off consider that the original equation started with $x$'s and $y$'s, but so far we have a solution in terms of $x$'s and $u$'s. So replace all of the $u$'s to get:
$\eqalign{ -\frac{x^2}{4y^2} + \ln|y| = C }$
Now using the initial condition:
$\eqalign{ C &= -\frac{x^2}{4y^2} + \ln|y| \\ &= -\frac{(-1)^2}{4(1)^2} + \ln|(1)| \\ &= -\frac{1}{4} }$
the final solution is written down as:
$\eqalign{ -\frac{x^2}{4y^2} + \ln|y| = -\frac{1}{4} }$
