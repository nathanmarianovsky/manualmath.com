Problem
Solve the differential equation:
$\eqalign{ (x + ye^{\frac{y}{x}}) dx - xe^{\frac{y}{x}} dy = 0 }$
where $y(1) = 0$.
Solution
First rewriting this gives:
$\eqalign{ (x + ye^{\frac{y}{x}}) dx - xe^{\frac{y}{x}} dy &= 0 \\ xe^{\frac{y}{x}} dy &= (x + ye^{\frac{y}{x}}) dx \\ \frac{dy}{dx} &= e^{-\frac{y}{x}} + \frac{y}{x} }$
Now to see if the homogeneous substitution can be used, first lets check that the function is homogeneous with degree zero:
$\eqalign{ f(x,y) &= e^{-\frac{y}{x}} + \frac{y}{x} \\ f(tx,ty) &= e^{-\frac{(ty)}{(tx)}} + \frac{(ty)}{(tx)} \\ &= e^{-\frac{y}{x}} + \frac{y}{x} \\ &= f(x,y) }$
With the condition checked, using the substitution:
$\eqalign{ y &= ux \\ y' &= u'x + u }$
and plugging in gives:
$\eqalign{ y' &= e^{-\frac{y}{x}} + \frac{y}{x} \\ u'x + u &= e^{-\frac{ux}{x}} + \frac{ux}{x} \\ u'x + u &= e^{-u} + u \\ \frac{du}{dx}x &= e^{-u} \\ e^u du &= \frac{dx}{x} \\ \int e^u du &= \int \frac{dx}{x} \\ e^u &= \ln|x| + C \\ e^u - \ln|x| &= C }$
To finish off consider that the original equation started with $x$'s and $y$'s, but so far we have a solution in terms of $x$'s and $u$'s. So replace all of the $u$'s to get:
$\eqalign{ e^{\frac{y}{x}} - \ln|x| = C }$
Now using the initial condition:
$\eqalign{ C &= e^{\frac{y}{x}} - \ln|x| \\ &= e^{\frac{0}{1}} - \ln(1) \\ &= 1 }$
the final solution is written down as:
$\eqalign{ e^{\frac{y}{x}} - \ln|x| = 1 }$
In this scenario it is even possible to get the solution in explicit form:
$\eqalign{ e^{\frac{y}{x}} - \ln|x| &= 1 \\ e^{\frac{y}{x}} &= \ln|x| + 1 \\ \frac{y}{x} &= \ln(\ln|x| + 1) \\ y &= x\ln(\ln|x| + 1) }$
