Problem
Solve the differential equation:
$\eqalign{ (x - y) dx + x dy = 0 }$
Solution
First rewriting this gives:
$\eqalign{ (x - y) dx + x dy &= 0 \\ x dy &= (y - x) dx \\ \frac{dy}{dx} &= \frac{y - x}{x} }$
Now to see if the homogeneous substitution can be used, first lets check that the function is homogeneous with degree zero:
$\eqalign{ f(x,y) &= \frac{y - x}{x} \\ f(tx,ty) &= \frac{(ty) - (tx)}{(tx)} \\ &= \frac{y - x}{x} \\ &= f(x,y) }$
With the condition checked, using the substitution:
$\eqalign{ y &= ux \\ y' &= u'x + u }$
and plugging in gives:
$\eqalign{ y' &= \frac{y - x}{x} \\ u'x + u &= \frac{ux - x}{x} \\ u'x + u &= u - 1 \\ u'x &= -1 \\ \frac{du}{dx} x &= -1 \\ du &= -\frac{dx}{x} \\ \int du &= \int -\frac{dx}{x} \\ u &= -\ln|x| + C \\ u + \ln|x| &= C }$
To finish off consider that the original equation started with $x$'s and $y$'s, but so far we have a solution in terms of $x$'s and $u$'s. So replace all of the $u$'s to get:
$\eqalign{ \frac{y}{x} + \ln|x| = C }$
Typically solutions are left in implicit form, but this solution can actually be written in explicit form:
$\eqalign{ \frac{y}{x} + \ln|x| &= C \\ \frac{y}{x} &= -\ln|x| + C \\ y &= -x\ln|x| + Cx }$
