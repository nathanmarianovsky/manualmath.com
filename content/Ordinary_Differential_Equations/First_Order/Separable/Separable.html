The Setup
Given a differential equation in the form:
$\eqalign{ y' = f(x,y) }$
where $y'$ denotes the first derivative, under a separable function the equation can be solved in a simple manner.
Separation under Multiplication
Assume that the function can be separated under multiplication as:
$\eqalign{ f(x,y) = j(x)k(y) }$
Plugging this into the differential equation yields:
$\eqalign{ y' &= j(x)k(y) }$
The next step is to rewrite the derivative in Leibniz's notation and separate the differentials so that both sides can be integrated:
$\eqalign{ \frac{dy}{dx} &= j(x)k(y) \\ \frac{dy}{k(y)} &= j(x) dx \\ \int \frac{dy}{k(y)} &= \int j(x) dx \\ K(y) &= J(x) + C \\ K(y) - J(x) &= C }$
where:
$\eqalign{ K(y) = \int \frac{dy}{k(y)} \hspace{.3cm} \text{and} \hspace{.3cm} J(x) = \int j(x) dx }$
Note that for the above to hold true $k(y) \neq 0$ since it is in the denominator. If it is zero, just go back to beginning to notice that the problem reduces down to solving $y' = 0$. The solution to this is always some constant, $y = C$. For all other cases, the above solution was left in implicit form, but sometimes the solution can be put in explicit form if it is possible to isolate $y$.
Separation under Addition
I now want to show that when talking about separability in relation to first order differential equations, separability under addition is never useful to solving the equation at hand. To see this first hand assume that the function can be separated under addition as:
$\eqalign{ f(x,y) = g(x) + h(y) }$
Plugging this into the differential equation and following through like before:
$\eqalign{ y' &= g(x) + h(y) \\ \frac{dy}{dx} &= g(x) + h(y) \\ dy &= \Big( g(x) + h(y) \Big) dx }$
At that last step is should become apparent that no matter what algebraic manipulation you perform, there is no way to put all of the $x$'s and $y$'s on independent sides. So whenever someone mentions separable differential equations, it is usually implied that it is separability under multiplication.
