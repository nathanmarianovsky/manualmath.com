Problem
Solve the following differential equation:
$\eqalign{ \frac{dy}{dt} = \frac{4\sin(2t)}{y} }$
where $y(0) = 1$.
Solution
Since the function is separable:
$\eqalign{ \frac{dy}{dt} &= \frac{4\sin(2t)}{y} \\ y dy &= 4\sin(2t) dt \\ \int y dy &= \int 4\sin(2t) dt \\ \frac{y^2}{2} &= -2\cos(2t) + C \\ \frac{y^2}{2} + 2\cos(2t) &= C }$
The above solution could have been put in explicit form, but that would involve using square roots, which I prefer to avoid for the time being. Now to satisfy the intial value we are given that $y(0) = 1$ which is the same as saying that $(0,1)$ is a point that must lie on the curve of the solution:
$\eqalign{ C &= \frac{y^2}{2} + 2\cos(2t) \\ &= \frac{(1)^2}{2} + 2\cos(2(0)) \\ &= \frac{1}{2} + 2 \\ &= \frac{5}{2} }$
With this the final solution can be written as:
$\eqalign{ \frac{y^2}{2} + 2\cos(2t) = \frac{5}{2} }$
In case an implicit solution does not satisfy you, here is how the explicit solution can be found:
$\eqalign{ \frac{y^2}{2} + 2\cos(2t) &= \frac{5}{2} \\ \frac{y^2}{2} &= \frac{5}{2} - 2\cos(2t) \\ y^2 &= 5 - 4\cos(2t) \\ y &= \pm \sqrt{5 - 4\cos(2t)} }$
Now a singular questions remains: which root to pick? This can only be checked if an initial value is given. In this case the point $(0,1)$ must lie on the curve:
$\eqalign{ y(0) &= \sqrt{5 - 4\cos(2(0))} = \sqrt{5 - 4} = 1 \\ y(0) &= -\sqrt{5 - 4\cos(2(0))} = -\sqrt{5 - 4} = -1 }$
Since the positive root produces the needed result, it is the correct solution in explicit form:
$\eqalign{ y = \sqrt{5 - 4\cos(2t)} }$
