The Setup
Given a differential equation in the form:
$\eqalign{ 0 = M(x,y) dx + N(x,y) dy }$
the method used to obtain the solution is analogous to finding the potential function to a conservative vector field.
Motivating the Approach
Before mentioning the differential equation again, I first need to mention $\textit{Schwarz's Theorem}$ which states that when given a function $f: \mathbb{R}^n \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$, then:
$\eqalign{ \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_i \partial x_j} (w_1,\dots,w_n) = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_j \partial x_i} (w_1,\dots,w_n) }$
where $i$ and $j$ are some integers and:
$\eqalign{ (w_1,w_2,w_3,\dots,w_n) \in \mathbb{R}^n \hspace{.5cm} \text{and} \hspace{.5cm} f \in C^2(\mathbb{R}^n) }$
which is essentially a formal way of saying that second partial derivatives commute for any two variables given a function has continuous second partial derivatives at some point. This will lay the groundwork for how the differential equation is handled.
Solving the Equation
To understand the approach in full detail I need to first explain the differential of a function. Given any function $F(x,y)$, the differential of the function is defined as:
$\eqalign{ dF = \frac{\partial F}{\partial x} dx + \frac{\partial F}{\partial y} dy }$
which is basically a way to measure how the overall function changes with respect to changes in the variables. Notice right away that this differential pretty much matches the differential equation we want to solve. So now playing the matching game gives:
$\eqalign{ \frac{\partial F}{\partial x} &= M(x,y) \\ \frac{\partial F}{\partial y} &= N(x,y) \\ dF &= 0 }$
The first two can easily be understood, but how do we interpret the last statement? If the differential of a function is defined to be zero, then it is essentially the same as saying that over the course of some curve, the function remains unchanged. This of course only happens when a function is constant. Another question you may want to now ask is how do we know that this approach can be used to obtain a solution? In vector calculus a differential equating to zero is the same as saying that the function represents a conservative vector field and it happens that there is a condition which will guarantee a solution:
$\eqalign{ \frac{\partial M}{\partial y} = \frac{\partial N}{\partial x} }$
which is the same as stating:
$\eqalign{ \frac{\partial^2 F}{\partial x \partial y} &= \frac{\partial^2 F}{\partial y \partial x} }$
which has to hold true given that the function is twice differentiable. So now to solve the differential equation we are left with two equations to handle. The first of which is:
$\eqalign{ \frac{\partial F}{\partial x} &= M(x,y) \\ \frac{dF}{dx} &= M(x,y) \\ dF &= M(x,y) dx \\ \int dF &= \int M(x,y) dx \\ F(x,y) &= f(x,y) + h(x) }$
and the second:
$\eqalign{ \frac{\partial F}{\partial y} &= N(x,y) \\ \frac{dF}{dy} &= N(x,y) \\ dF &= N(x,y) dy \\ \int dF &= \int N(x,y) dy \\ F(x,y) &= f(x,y) + g(y) }$
Notice that both integrations will spit out stuff that is common but also stuff that is independent. Formally the final solution is given as:
$\eqalign{ C = f(x,y) + h(x) + g(x) }$
where the $C$ handles the constants of integration that should have come out of the integration above.
Cases with an Integration Factor
Sometimes it may happen that the necessary condition is not true:
$\eqalign{ \frac{\partial M}{\partial y} \neq \frac{\partial N}{\partial x} }$
and though it may seem hopeless, there is a way to fix this situation. Let us assume that there exists a factor $\mu(x,y)$ such that:
$\eqalign{ \frac{\partial}{\partial y} \Big( \mu M \Big) &= \frac{\partial}{\partial x} \Big( \mu N \Big) \\ \frac{\partial \mu}{\partial y} M + \mu \frac{\partial M}{\partial y} &= \frac{\partial \mu}{\partial x} N + \mu \frac{\partial N}{\partial x} }$
The above is a partial differential equation which is a little too hard to solve. So we can consider two common cases instead. First assume that $\mu$ is strictly a function of $x$ giving:
$\eqalign{ \frac{\partial \mu}{\partial x} N + \mu \frac{\partial N}{\partial x} = \mu \frac{\partial M}{\partial y} \\ }$
I can now abandon the partial derivative for a normal derivative on the $\mu$ since it is a function of one variable and in order to make the work cleaner I will use subscripts to denote partial derivatives where needed:
$\eqalign{ \frac{d\mu}{dx} N &= \mu \Big( M_y - N_x \Big) \\ \frac{d\mu}{\mu} &= \frac{M_y - N_x}{N} dx \\ \int \frac{d\mu}{\mu} &= \int \frac{M_y - N_x}{N} dx \\ \ln|\mu| &= \int \frac{M_y - N_x}{N} dx \\ \mu &= e^{\int \frac{M_y - N_x}{N} dx} \\ \mu &= e^{K(x) + C} \\ \mu &= e^{K(x)}e^C \\ \mu &= De^{K(x)} \\ }$
where:
$\eqalign{ D = e^c \hspace{.2cm} \text{and} \hspace{.2cm} K(x) = \int \frac{M_y - N_x}{N} dx }$
In order for the above integrating factor to work note that the integrand must be a function of $x$ strictly. Now for the case where $\mu$ is a function of $y$ only:
$\eqalign{ \frac{\partial \mu}{\partial y} M + \mu \frac{\partial M}{\partial y} = \mu \frac{\partial N}{\partial x} \\ }$
Just like before I will use subscripts to denote partial derivatives and turn the partial derivative into a normal derivative for $\mu$:
$\eqalign{ \frac{d\mu}{dy} M &= \mu \Big( N_x - M_y \Big) \\ \frac{d\mu}{\mu} &= \frac{N_x - M_y}{M} dy \\ \int \frac{d\mu}{\mu} &= \int \frac{N_x - M_y}{M} dy \\ \ln|\mu| &= \int \frac{N_x - M_y}{M} dy \\ \mu &= e^{\int \frac{N_x - M_y}{M} dy} \\ \mu &= e^{J(y) + C} \\ \mu &= e^{J(y)}e^C \\ \mu &= De^{J(y)} }$
where:
$\eqalign{ D = e^c \hspace{.2cm} \text{and} \hspace{.2cm} J(y) = \int \frac{N_x - M_y}{M} dy }$
Like before, the integrand must be a function of $y$ strictly so that the integrating factor works. In both cases we get a whole family of integrating factors similar to the scenario from the integrating factor for first order linear differential equations. So once again we choose the easy path and let $D = 1$ so that we have:
$\eqalign{ \mu &= e^{K(x)} \hspace{.3cm} \text{or} \hspace{.3cm} \mu &= e^{J(y)} }$
