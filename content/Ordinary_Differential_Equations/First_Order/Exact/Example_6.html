Problem
Solve the differential equation:
$\eqalign{ \Big( \frac{1}{1 + y^2} + \cos(x) - 2xy \Big) \frac{dy}{dx} = y(y + \sin(x)) }$
where $y(0) = 1$.
Solution
If we are to guess that this equation is exact, first reorganize:
$\eqalign{ \Big( \frac{1}{1 + y^2} + \cos(x) - 2xy \Big) \frac{dy}{dx} &= y(y + \sin(x)) \\ -y(y + \sin(x)) dx + \Big( \frac{1}{1 + y^2} + \cos(x) - 2xy \Big) dy &= 0 }$
and identify the components as:
$\eqalign{ M(x,y) &= -y(y + \sin(x)) \\ N(x,y) &= \frac{1}{1 + y^2} + \cos(x) - 2xy }$
Now to check if these satisfy the condition:
$\eqalign{ M_y &= N_x \\ \frac{\partial}{\partial y} (-y(y + \sin(x))) &= \frac{\partial}{\partial x} \Big( \frac{1}{1 + y^2} + \cos(x) - 2xy \Big) \\ -(y + \sin(x)) - y &= -\sin(x) - 2y }$
With the condition checked, to determine the solution:
$\eqalign{ \int M(x,y) dx &= \int -y(y + \sin(x)) dx \\ &= -y^2x + y\cos(x) \\ \int N(x,y) dy &= \int \Big( \frac{1}{1 + y^2} + \cos(x) - 2xy \Big) dy \\ &= \arctan(y) + y\cos(x) - xy^2 }$
and with this the final solution is written down as:
$\eqalign{ C &= y\cos(x) - xy^2 + \arctan(y) }$
Now to satisfy the initial condition the solution must lie along the curve that passes through the point $(0,1)$:
$\eqalign{ C &= y\cos(x) - xy^2 + \arctan(y) \\ &= (1)\cos(0) - (0)(1)^2 + \arctan(1) \\ &= 1 - \frac{\pi}{4} \\ &= \frac{4 - \pi}{4} }$
and so the final solution is written as:
$\eqalign{ \frac{4 - \pi}{4} &= y\cos(x) - xy^2 + \arctan(y) }$
