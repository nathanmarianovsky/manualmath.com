Problem
Keeping substitution in mind, rewrite the differential equation:
$\eqalign{ \frac{dy}{dx} = f(x)e^y + g(x) }$
as a first order linear differential equation.
Solution
Lets try the substitution:
$\eqalign{ u &= e^y \\ u' &= uy' }$
and plugging in:
$\eqalign{ y' &= f(x)e^y + g(x) \\ \frac{u'}{u} &= f(x)u + g(x) \\ u' &= f(x)u^2 + g(x)u \\ u' - g(x)u &= f(x)u^2 }$
which unfortunately is a Bernoulli differential equation. Even though another substitution can be used to transform the Bernoulli equation into a first order linear one, instead try the substitution:
$\eqalign{ u &= e^{-y} \\ u' &= -uy' }$
on the original equation:
$\eqalign{ y' &= f(x)e^y + g(x) \\ -\frac{u'}{u} &= f(x)u{-1} + g(x) \\ -u' &= f(x) + g(x)u \\ u' + g(x)u &= -f(x) }$
which gives a first order linear differential equation.
