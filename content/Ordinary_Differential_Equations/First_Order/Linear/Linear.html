The Setup
Given a differential equation in the form:
$\eqalign{ y' + p(x)y = q(x) }$
where $y'$ denotes the first derivative, under any functions $p(x)$ and $q(x)$ that are bounded and have only a finite number of discontinuties the solution can always be obtained.
Motivating the Need for an Integrating Factor
When approaching the differential equation above, it might be a good idea if the left side can be rewritten as a product rule. Unfortunately, the given equation does not conform to a product rule, but perhaps we can define some factor such that:
$\eqalign{ \mu y' + \mu p(x)y = \frac{d}{dx} (\mu y) }$
If this is to hold true, then we must have it such that:
$\eqalign{ \mu' &= \mu p(x) \\ \frac{d\mu}{dx} &= \mu p(x) \\ \frac{d\mu}{\mu} &= p(x) dx \\ \int \frac{d\mu}{\mu} &= \int p(x) dx \\ \ln|\mu| &= P(x) + C \\ \mu &= e^{P(x) + C} \\ \mu &= e^C e^{P(x)} \\ \mu &= D e^{P(x)} }$
where $P(x)$ denotes the antiderivative of $p(x)$. Towards the end I specifically went out of my way to stress how the constant plays out. Notice that there is not one definite integrating factor, but rather a whole family of them. So which one do we choose? As a human being you want to make your life easier, so just let $D = 1$ and move on. With this choice the integrating factor is officially defined as:
$\eqalign{ \mu = e^{P(x)} }$
Obtaining General Solution First
With the integrating factor officially defined, solving the differential equation becomes a trivial task:
$\eqalign{ y' + p(x)y &= q(x) \\ \mu(x) y' + \mu(x) p(x)y &= \mu(x) q(x) \\ \frac{d}{dx} (\mu(x) y) &= \mu(x) q(x) \\ d(\mu(x) y) &= \mu(x) q(x) dx \\ \int d(\mu(x) y) &= \int \mu(x) q(x) dx \\ \mu(x) y(x) &= \int \mu(x) q(x) dx \\ y(x) &= \frac{1}{\mu(x)} \int \mu(x) q(x) dx }$
The integral on the right hand side will spit out a constant that is usually left in the general solution. Given an initial condition, plug in the point to determine the value of the constant for the particular solution.
Getting the Particular Solution First
In the previous calculation I used the indefinite integral and I did so for a reason. You may not always be given an initial condition, so the general solution is what is wanted. Even though you can take the general solution with some initial value to get the particular solution, lets now use the definite integral to calculate the particular solution right off the bat given that $y(x_0) = y_0$:
$\eqalign{ y' + p(s)y &= q(s) \\ \mu(s) y' + \mu(s) p(s)y &= \mu(s) q(s) \\ \frac{d}{ds} (\mu(s) y) &= \mu(s) q(s) \\ d(\mu(s) y) &= \mu(s) q(s) ds \\ \int_{x_0}^x d(\mu(s) y) &= \int_{x_0}^x \mu(s) q(s) ds \\ (\mu(s) y) \Big|_{x_0}^x &= \int_{x_0}^x \mu(s) q(s) ds \\ (\mu(x) y(x)) - (\mu(x_0) y(x_0)) &= \int_{x_0}^x \mu(s) q(s) ds \\ \mu(x) y(x) &= \mu(x_0) y(x_0) + \int_{x_0}^x \mu(s) q(s) ds \\ y(x) &= \frac{1}{\mu(x)} \Big( \mu(x_0) y(x_0) + \int_{x_0}^x \mu(s) q(s) ds \Big) }$
Now lets comment on a few things. I first of all switched the functions in the beginning to have variable $s$ instead of $x$ so that I could use $x$ on the bounds of the integrals. Now formally:
$\eqalign{ \mu(x) = e^{\int_{x_0}^x p(s) ds} }$
which implies
$\eqalign{ \mu(x_0) = e^{\int_{x_0}^{x_0} p(s) ds} = e^0 = 1 }$
and allows us to write down the final solution as:
$\eqalign{ y(x) = \frac{1}{\mu(x)} \Big( y_0 + \int_{x_0}^x \mu(s) q(s) ds \Big) }$
Note that whether using the indefinite or definite integral, the final solution should always come out to be the same as can easily be confirmed by close comparison of the final formulas for both methods.
