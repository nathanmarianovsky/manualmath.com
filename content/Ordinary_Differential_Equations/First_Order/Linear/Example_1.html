Problem
Solve the following differential equation:
$\eqalign{ ty' + 2y = t^2 - t + 1 }$
where $y(1) = \frac{1}{2}$.
Solution
First rearranging gives:
$\eqalign{ ty' + 2y &= t^2 - t + 1 \\ y' + \frac{2}{t}y &= t - 1 + \frac{1}{t} }$
Now this is in the form of a first-order linear differential equation. Next the integrating factor is needed:
$\eqalign{ \mu &= e^{\int \frac{2}{t} dt} \\ &= e^{2\ln|t|} \\ &= e^{\ln(|t|^2)} \\ &= (|t|)^2 \\ &= t^2 }$
Now multiplying the equation throughout by the integrating factor gives:
$\eqalign{ y' + \frac{2}{t}y &= t - 1 + \frac{1}{t} \\ t^2y' + 2ty &= t^3 - t^2 + t \\ \frac{d}{dt} (t^2 y) &= t^3 - t^2 + t \\ d(t^2 y) &= (t^3 - t^2 + t) dt \\ \int d(t^2 y) &= \int (t^3 - t^2 + t) dt \\ t^2 y &= \frac{t^4}{4} - \frac{t^3}{3} + \frac{t^2}{2} + C \\ y &= \frac{t^2}{4} - \frac{t}{3} + \frac{1}{2} + \frac{C}{t^2} }$
To determine the value of $C$ use the initial value given:
$\eqalign{ \frac{1}{2} &= y(1) \\ \frac{1}{2} &= \frac{(1)^2}{4} - \frac{(1)}{3} + \frac{1}{2} + \frac{C}{(1)^2} \\ 0 &= \frac{1}{4} - \frac{1}{3} + C \\ C &= \frac{1}{12} }$
so that the final solution can be written down as:
$\eqalign{ y = \frac{t^2}{4} - \frac{t}{3} + \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{12t^2} }$
