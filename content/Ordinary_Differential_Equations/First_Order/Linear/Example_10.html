Problem
Determine a continuous solution to:
$\eqalign{ y' + P(x)y = 4x }$
where:
$\eqalign{ P(x) = \begin{cases} 2 & 0 \leq x \leq 1 \\ -\frac{2}{x} & x > 1 \end{cases} }$
and $y(0) = 3$.
Solution
To approach this, just consider the different cases separately. First for $x \in [0,1]$:
$\eqalign{ y' + 2y = 4x }$
has the integrating factor:
$\eqalign{ \mu &= e^{\int P(x) dx} \\ &= e^{\int 2 dx} \\ &= e^{2x} }$
which leads to the solution:
$\eqalign{ y' + 2y &= 4x \\ e^{2x}y' + 2e^{2x}y &= 4xe^{2x} \\ \frac{d}{dx} (e^{2x}y) &= 4xe^{2x} \\ d(e^{2x}y) &= 4xe^{2x} dx \\ \int d(e^{2x}y) &= \int 4xe^{2x} dx \\ e^{2x}y &= e^{2x}(2x - 1) + C_1 \\ y &= 2x - 1 + C_1e^{-2x} }$
Now for $x \in (1, \infty)$:
$\eqalign{ y' - \frac{2}{x}y = 4x }$
has the integrating factor:
$\eqalign{ \mu &= e^{\int P(x) dx} \\ &= e^{\int -\frac{2}{x} dx} \\ &= e^{-2\ln|x|} \\ &= e^{\ln(|x|^{-2})} \\ &= |x|^{-2} \\ &= x^{-2} }$
which leads to the solution:
$\eqalign{ y' - \frac{2}{x}y &= 4x \\ x^{-2}y' - 2x^{-3}y &= 4x^{-1} \\ \frac{d}{dx} (x^{-2}y) &= 4x^{-1} \\ d(x^{-2}y) &= 4x^{-1} dx \\ \int d(x^{-2}y) &= \int 4x^{-1} dx \\ x^{-2}y &= 4\ln|x| + C_2 \\ y &= 4x^2\ln|x| + C_2x^2 }$
Putting these two solutions together gives:
$\eqalign{ y(x) = \begin{cases} 2x - 1 + C_1e^{-2x} & 0 \leq x \leq 1 \\ 4x^2\ln|x| + C_2x^2 & x > 1 \end{cases} }$
Now using the initial condition:
$\eqalign{ 3 &= y(0) \\ 3 &= 2(0) - 1 + C_1e^{-2(0)} \\ 3 &= -1 + C_1 \\ C_1 &= 4 }$
To determine the other constant use the fact that the solution has to be continuous which means that at $x = 1$ the two pieces must evaluate to the same value:
$\eqalign{ 2(1) - 1 + 4e^{-2(1)} &= 4(1)^2\ln(1) + C_2(1)^2 \\ 1 + 4e^{-2} &= C_2 }$
Putting all of this together gives the final solution:
$\eqalign{ y(x) = \begin{cases} 2x - 1 + 4e^{-2x} & 0 \leq x \leq 1 \\ 4x^2\ln|x| + (1 + 4e^{-2})x^2 & x > 1 \end{cases} }$
