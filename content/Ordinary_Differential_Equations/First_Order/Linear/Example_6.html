Problem
Determine all solutions to:
$\eqalign{ x^2y' + 2xy = \cos^2(x) }$
Once the family of solutions is obtained, plot the curves for $C = -2, -1, 0, 1, 2$. From the graph what is the behavior of $y(x)$ as $x \rightarrow \infty$ or $x \rightarrow -\infty$? Justify why this makes sense.
Solution
Take notice that the left hand side is already in the form of a product rule. Just proceed to solving:
$\eqalign{ x^2y' + 2xy &= \cos^2(x) \\ \frac{d}{dx}(x^2y) &= \cos^2(x) \\ d(x^2y) &= \cos^2(x) dx \\ \int d(x^2y) &= \int \cos^2(x) dx \\ x^2y &= \frac{1}{2}(x + \sin(x)\cos(x)) + C \\ y &= \frac{1}{2x^2}(x + \sin(x)\cos(x)) + \frac{C}{x^2} }$
Now for the family of curves:
The graph seems to point to the fact that:
$\eqalign{ \lim_{x \rightarrow \infty} y(x) = \lim_{x \rightarrow -\infty} y(x) = 0 }$
To make sense of this, take the limits on the general solution:
$\eqalign{ \lim_{x \rightarrow \infty} y(x) &= \lim_{x \rightarrow \infty} \Big( \frac{1}{2x^2}(x + \sin(x)\cos(x)) + \frac{C}{x^2} \Big) \\ &= \lim_{x \rightarrow \infty} \frac{x + \sin(x)\cos(x)}{2x^2} + \lim_{x \rightarrow \infty} \frac{C}{x^2} \\ &= 0 \\ \lim_{x \rightarrow -\infty} y(x) &= \lim_{x \rightarrow -\infty} \Big( \frac{1}{2x^2}(x + \sin(x)\cos(x)) + \frac{C}{x^2} \Big) \\ &= \lim_{x \rightarrow -\infty} \frac{x + \sin(x)\cos(x)}{2x^2} + \lim_{x \rightarrow -\infty} \frac{C}{x^2} \\ &= 0 }$
So no matter the value of $C$, the end behavior is consistent for the whole family.
