Problem
Solve the following differential equation for $t > 0$:
$\eqalign{ 2t\frac{dy}{dt} - y = t + 1 }$
where $y(2) = 4$.
Solution
First rearranging gives:
$\eqalign{ 2t\frac{dy}{dt} - y &= t + 1 \\ \frac{dy}{dt} - \frac{1}{2t}y = \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{2t} }$
Now this is in the form of a first-order linear differential equation. Next the integrating factor is needed:
$\eqalign{ \mu &= e^{\int -\frac{1}{2t} dt} \\ &= e^{-\frac{1}{2}\ln|t|} \\ &= e^{\ln(|t|^{-\frac{1}{2}})} \\ &= |t|^{-\frac{1}{2}} \\ &= t^{-\frac{1}{2}} }$
where dropping the absolute value is only allowed since $t > 0$ is given as a condition. Now multiplying the equation throughout by the integrating factor gives:
$\eqalign{ t^{-\frac{1}{2}}\frac{dy}{dt} - \frac{1}{2}t^{-\frac{3}{2}}y &= \frac{1}{2}t^{-\frac{1}{2}} + \frac{1}{2}t^{-\frac{3}{2}} \\ \frac{d}{dt} \Big( t^{-\frac{1}{2}}y \Big) &= \frac{1}{2}t^{-\frac{1}{2}} + \frac{1}{2}t^{-\frac{3}{2}} \\ d\Big( t^{-\frac{1}{2}}y \Big) &= \Big( \frac{1}{2}t^{-\frac{1}{2}} + \frac{1}{2}t^{-\frac{3}{2}} \Big) dt \\ \int d\Big( t^{-\frac{1}{2}}y \Big) &= \int \Big( \frac{1}{2}t^{-\frac{1}{2}} + \frac{1}{2}t^{-\frac{3}{2}} \Big) dt \\ t^{-\frac{1}{2}}y &= t^\frac{1}{2} - t^{-\frac{1}{2}} + C \\ y &= t - 1 + Ct^\frac{1}{2} }$
To determine the value of $C$ use the initial value given:
$\eqalign{ 4 &= y(2) \\ 4 &= (2) - 1 + C(2)^\frac{1}{2} \\ 3 &= \sqrt{2}C \\ C &= \frac{3}{\sqrt{2}} }$
so that the final solution can be written down as:
$\eqalign{ y = t - 1 + 3\sqrt{\frac{t}{2}} }$
