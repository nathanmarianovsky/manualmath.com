Problem
Determine all solutions to:
$\eqalign{ xy' = y + x^2\sin(x) }$
Once the family of solutions is obtained, plot the curves for $C = -2, -1, 0, 1, 2$. Do all the plots feature a common intersection point?
Solution
First rearranging gives:
$\eqalign{ xy' &= y + x^2\sin(x) \\ xy' - y &= x^2\sin(x) \\ y' - x^{-1}y &= x\sin(x) }$
Now this is in the form of a first-order linear differential equation. Next the integrating factor is needed:
$\eqalign{ \mu &= e^{\int -x^{-1} dx} \\ &= e^{-\ln|x|} \\ &= e^{\ln(|x|^{-1})} \\ &= |x|^{-1} \\ &= x^{-1} }$
where dropping the absolute value is allowed since multiplying by a negative gives the same result. Now multiplying the equation throughout by the integrating factor gives:
$\eqalign{ x^{-1}y' - x^{-2}y &= \sin(x) \\ \frac{d}{dx}(x^{-1}y) &= \sin(x) \\ d(x^{-1}y) &= \sin(x) dx \\ \int d(x^{-1}y) &= \int \sin(x) dx \\ x^{-1}y &= -\cos(x) + C \\ y &= -x\cos(x) + Cx }$

The graph seems to point to the fact that all the different plots intersect at the origin.
