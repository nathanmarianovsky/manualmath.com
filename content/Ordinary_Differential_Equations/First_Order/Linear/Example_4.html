Problem
Solve the following differential equation:
$\eqalign{ \cos^2(t)\sin(t)y' = -\cos^3(t)y + 1 }$
where $y\Big( \frac{\pi}{4} \Big) = 0$.
Solution
First rearranging gives:
$\eqalign{ \cos^2(t)\sin(t)y' &= -\cos^3(t)y + 1 \\ \cos^2(t)\sin(t)y' + \cos^3(t)y &= 1 \\ y' + \frac{\cos(t)}{\sin(t)}y &= \frac{1}{\cos^2(t)\sin(t)} }$
Now this is in the form of a first-order linear differential equation. Next the integrating factor is needed:
$\eqalign{ \mu &= e^{\int \frac{\cos(t)}{\sin(t)} dt} \\ &= e^{\ln|\sin(t)|} \\ &= |\sin(t)| \\ &= \sin(t) }$
where the absolute value is allowed to be dropped since any multiple of the integrating factor is also an integrating factor. So putting a negative sign will not change the outcome. Now multiplying the equation throughout by the integrating factor gives:
$\eqalign{ \sin(t)y' + \cos(t)y &= \frac{1}{\cos^2(t)} \\ \frac{d}{dt} (\sin(t)y) &= \sec^2(t) \\ d(\sin(t)y) &= \sec^2(t) dt \\ \int d(\sin(t)y) &= \int \sec^2(t) dt \\ \sin(t)y &= \tan(t) + C \\ y &= \frac{\tan(t)}{\sin(t)} + \frac{C}{\sin(t)} \\ y &= \sec(t) + C\csc(t) }$
To determine the value of $C$ use the initial value given:
$\eqalign{ 0 &= y\Big( \frac{\pi}{4} \Big) \\ 0 &= \sec\Big( \frac{\pi}{4} \Big) + C\csc\Big( \frac{\pi}{4} \Big) \\ 0 &= \sqrt{2} + C\sqrt{2} \\ C &= -1 }$
so that the final solution can be written down as:
$\eqalign{ y = \sec(t) - \csc(t) }$
