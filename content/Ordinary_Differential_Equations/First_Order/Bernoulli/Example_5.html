Problem
Determine the family of solutions to:
$\eqalign{ \frac{dy}{dx} + \frac{2}{x}y = -x^2\cos(x)y^2 }$
Solution
Right away it is clear that this differential equation conforms to the Bernoulli one. To begin divide throughout by the non-linear term:
$\eqalign{ \frac{dy}{dx} + \frac{2}{x}y &= -x^2\cos(x)y^2 \\ y'y^{-2} + \frac{2}{x}y^{-1} &= -x^2\cos(x) }$
Now make the substitution:
$\eqalign{ u &= y^{-1} \\ u' &= -y^{-2}y' }$
and plug in to get:
$\eqalign{ y'y^{-2} + \frac{2}{x}y^{-1} &= -x^2\cos(x) \\ -u' + \frac{2}{x}u &= -x^2\cos(x) \\ u' - \frac{2}{x}u &= x^2\cos(x) }$
a first-order linear differential equation. Now an integrating factor is needed:
$\eqalign{ \mu &= e^{\int p(x) dx} \\ &= e^{\int -\frac{2}{x} dx} \\ &= e^{-2\ln|x|} \\ &= e^{\ln(|x|^{-2})} \\ &= \frac{1}{|x|^2} \\ &= \frac{1}{x^2} }$
where dropping the absolute value is allowed since squaring guarantees a positive result. Next multiplying throughout and solving for $u$ gives:
$\eqalign{ u' - \frac{2}{x}u &= x^2\cos(x) \\ \frac{1}{x^2} - \frac{2}{x^3}u &= \cos(x) \\ \frac{d}{dx}\Big( \frac{u}{x^2} \Big) &= \cos(x) \\ d\Big( \frac{u}{x^2} \Big) &= \cos(x) dx \\ \int d\Big( \frac{u}{x^2} \Big) &= \int \cos(x) dx \\ \frac{u}{x^2} &= \sin(x) + C \\ u &= x^2\sin(x) + Cx^2 }$
Now to solve for $y$ use the original substitution:
$\eqalign{ y^{-1} &= u \\ y &= u^{-1} \\ &= \frac{1}{x^2\sin(x) + Cx^2} }$
