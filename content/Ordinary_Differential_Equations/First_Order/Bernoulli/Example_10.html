Problem
Solve the following differential equation for an explicit solution and determine its interval of existance:
$\eqalign{ (xy + x^2y^3)y' = 1 }$
where $y\Big( -\frac{1}{2} \Big) = 2$.
Solution
Begin by rearranging to see the Bernoulli form and divide throughout by the non-linear term:
$\eqalign{ (xy + x^2y^3)y' &= 1 \\ \frac{dy}{dx} &= \frac{1}{xy + x^2y^3} \\ \frac{dx}{dy} &= xy + x^2y^3 \\ x' - yx &= y^3x^2 \\ x'x^{-2} - yx^{-1} &= y^3 }$
Now make the substitution:
$\eqalign{ u &= x^{-1} \\ u' &= -x^{-2}x' }$
and plug in to get:
$\eqalign{ x'x^{-2} - yx^{-1} &= y^3 \\ -u' - yu &= y^3 \\ u' + yu &= -y^3 }$
a first order linear differential equation. Now an integrating factor is needed:
$\eqalign{ \mu &= e^{\int p(y) dy} \\ &= e^{\int y dy} \\ &= e^{\frac{y^2}{2}} }$
Next multiplying throughout and solving for $u$ gives:
$\eqalign{ u' + yu &= -y^3 \\ e^{\frac{y^2}{2}}u' + ye^{\frac{y^2}{2}}u &= -y^3e^{\frac{y^2}{2}} \\ \frac{d}{dy}(e^{\frac{y^2}{2}}u) &= -y^3e^{\frac{y^2}{2}} \\ d(e^{\frac{y^2}{2}}u) &= -y^3e^{\frac{y^2}{2}} dy \\ \int d(e^{\frac{y^2}{2}}u) &= \int -y^3e^{\frac{y^2}{2}} dy \\ e^{\frac{y^2}{2}}u &= -e^{\frac{y^2}{2}}(y^2 - 2) + C \\ u &= -y^2 + 2 + Ce^{-\frac{y^2}{2}} }$
Now to solve for $x$ use the original substitution:
$\eqalign{ x^{-1} &= u \\ x &= u^{-1} \\ &= \frac{1}{-y^2 + 2 + Ce^{-\frac{y^2}{2}}} }$
Given that $y\Big( \frac{1}{2} \Big) = 2$ is the same as saying that the point $\Big( -\frac{1}{2}, 2 \Big)$ must lie on the curve representing the particular solution. So:
$\eqalign{ x &= \frac{1}{-y^2 + 2 + Ce^{-\frac{(2)^2}{2}}} \\ -\frac{1}{2} &= \frac{1}{-(2)^2 + 2 + Ce^{-2}} \\ -2 &= -2 + Ce^{-2} \\ 0 &= Ce^{-2} \\ C &= 0 }$
With the constant determined, the particular solution takes the form:
$\eqalign{ x = \frac{1}{-y^2 + 2} \\ }$
To determine the interval of existance, we just have to account for the vertical asymptotes of the function:
$\eqalign{ 0 &= -y^2 + 2 \\ y^2 &= 2 \\ y &= \pm \sqrt{2} }$
With the bad points accounted for, the interval of existance for the particular solution is:
$\eqalign{ (-\infty, -\sqrt{2}) \cup (-\sqrt{2}, \sqrt{2}) \cup (\sqrt{2}, \infty) }$
