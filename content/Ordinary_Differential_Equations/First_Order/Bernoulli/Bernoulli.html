The Setup
Given a differential equation in the form:
$\eqalign{ y' + m(x)y = k(x)y^N }$
where $y'$ denotes the first derivative and $N \in \mathbb{R} \setminus \{0,1\}$ no matter the functions $m(x)$ and $k(x)$ a solution can always be obtained. We restrict $N$ to not be zero because that case would be trivial since the problem reduces down to a first-order linear differential equation. For $N = 1$ just reorganize to see that the same thing happens:
$\eqalign{ y' + m(x)y &= k(x)y \\ y' + m(x)y - k(x)y &= 0 \\ y' + (m(x) - k(x))y &= 0 \\ y' + g(x)y &= 0 }$
where I used $g(x) = m(x) - k(x)$ as a way to conform to the standard first-order linear differential equation.
Solving the Equation
The whole point of Bernoulli's differential equation is to use the right substitution such that the problem is reduced down to a first-order linear differential equation. To begin first devide both sides by the non-linear term:
$\eqalign{ y' + m(x)y &= k(x)y^N \\ y'y^{-N} + m(x)y^{1-N} &= k(x) }$
Now make the following substitution:
$\eqalign{ u &= y^{1-N} \\ u' &= (1-N)y'y^{-N} }$
and plug in:
$\eqalign{ \frac{u'}{1-N} + m(x)u &= k(x) \\ u' + (1-N)m(x)u &= (1-N)k(x) }$
To put the equation in the standard form let $M(x) = (1-N)m(x)$ and $K(x) = (1-N)k(x)$ so that:
$\eqalign{ u' + M(x)u = K(x) }$
Using the solution from the first-order linear section states that:
$\eqalign{ u &= \frac{1}{\mu} \int \mu K(x) dx \\ &= \Big( e^{\int M(x) dx} \Big)^{-1} \int e^{\int M(x) dx} K(x) dx \\ &= e^{-(1-N) \int m(x) dx} \int e^{(1-N) \int m(x) dx} (1-N) k(x) dx \\ &= (1-N) e^{(N-1) \int m(x) dx} \int e^{(1-N) \int m(x) dx} k(x) dx }$
Now there is just one thing left. The original differential equation was asking for a solution to $y$, not $u$. So to go back use the substitution:
$\eqalign{ y^{1-N} &= u \\ y &= u^\frac{1}{1-N} \\ y &= \Big[ (1-N) e^{(N-1) \int m(x) dx} \int e^{(1-N) \int m(x) dx} k(x) dx \Big]^\frac{1}{1-N} }$
