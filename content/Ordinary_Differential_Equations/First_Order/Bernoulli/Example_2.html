Problem
Solve the following differential equation:
$\eqalign{ y' + \frac{y}{x} - \sqrt{y} = 0 }$
where $y(1) = 0$.
Solution
Begin by rearranging to see the Bernoulli form and divide throughout by the non-linear term:
$\eqalign{ y' + \frac{y}{x} - \sqrt{y} &= 0 \\ y' + \frac{1}{x}y &= \sqrt{y} \\ y'y^{-\frac{1}{2}} + \frac{1}{x}y^\frac{1}{2} &= 1 }$
Now make the substitution:
$\eqalign{ u &= y^\frac{1}{2} \\ u' &= \frac{1}{2}y^{-\frac{1}{2}}y' }$
and plug in to get:
$\eqalign{ y'y^{-\frac{1}{2}} + \frac{1}{x}y^\frac{1}{2} &= 1 \\ 2u' + \frac{1}{x}u &= 1 \\ u' + \frac{1}{2x}u &= \frac{1}{2} }$
a first-order linear differential equation. Now an integrating factor is needed:
$\eqalign{ \mu &= e^{\int p(x) dx} \\ &= e^{\int \frac{1}{2x} dx} \\ &= e^{\frac{1}{2}\ln|x|} \\ &= e^{\ln(|x|^\frac{1}{2})} \\ &= |x|^\frac{1}{2} \\ &= x^\frac{1}{2} }$
where dropping the absolute value is allowed since putting a negative sign in front will not chance anything. Next multiplying throughout and solving for $u$ gives:
$\eqalign{ u' + \frac{1}{2x}u &= \frac{1}{2} \\ x^\frac{1}{2}u' + \frac{1}{2x^\frac{1}{2}}u &= \frac{1}{2}x^\frac{1}{2} \\ \frac{d}{dx}(x^\frac{1}{2}u) &= \frac{1}{2}x^\frac{1}{2} \\ d(x^\frac{1}{2}u) &= \frac{1}{2}x^\frac{1}{2} dx \\ \int d(x^\frac{1}{2}u) &= \frac{1}{2} \int x^\frac{1}{2} dx \\ x^\frac{1}{2}u &= \frac{1}{3}x^\frac{3}{2} + C \\ u &= \frac{1}{3}x^\frac{1}{2} + Cx^{-\frac{1}{2}} }$
Now to solve for $y$ use the original substitution:
$\eqalign{ y^\frac{1}{2} &= u \\ y &= u^2 \\ &= \Big[ \frac{1}{3}x^\frac{1}{2} + Cx^{-\frac{1}{2}} \Big]^2 }$
Now using the initial condition, the constant of integration comes out to be:
$\eqalign{ 0 &= y(1) \\ 0 &= \Big[ \frac{1}{3}(1)^\frac{1}{2} + C(1)^{-\frac{1}{2}} \Big]^2 \\ 0 &= \Big[ \frac{1}{3} + C \Big]^2 \\ C &= -\frac{1}{3} }$
With this the final solution can be written down as:
$\eqalign{ y &= \Big[ \frac{1}{3}x^\frac{1}{2} - \frac{1}{3}x^{-\frac{1}{2}} \Big]^2 }$
