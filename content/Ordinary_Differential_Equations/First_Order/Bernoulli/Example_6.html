Problem
Solve the following differential equation:
$\eqalign{ x\frac{dy}{dx} + 5y = 2x^2y^4 }$
where $y(1) = 3$.
Solution
Begin by rearranging to see the Bernoulli form and divide throughout by the non-linear term:
$\eqalign{ x\frac{dy}{dx} + 5y &= 2x^2y^4 \\ y' + \frac{5}{x}y &= 2xy^4 \\ y'y^{-4} + \frac{5}{x}y^{-3} &= 2x }$
Now make the substitution:
$\eqalign{ u &= y^{-3} \\ u' &= -3y^{-4}y' }$
and plug in to get:
$\eqalign{ y'y^{-4} + \frac{5}{x}y^{-3} &= 2x \\ -\frac{1}{3}u' + \frac{5}{x}u &= 2x \\ u' - \frac{15}{x}u &= -6x }$
a first order linear differential equation. Now an integrating factor is needed:
$\eqalign{ \mu &= e^{\int p(x) dx} \\ &= e^{\int -\frac{15}{x} dx} \\ &= e^{-15\ln|x|} \\ &= e^{\ln(|x|^{-15})} \\ &= |x|^{-15} \\ &= x^{-15} }$
where dropping the absolute value is allowed since putting a negative sign in front will not change anything. Next multiplying throughout and solving for $u$ gives:
$\eqalign{ u' - \frac{15}{x}u &= -6x \\ x^{-15}u' - 15x^{-16}u &= -6x^{-14} \\ \frac{d}{dx}(x^{-15}u) &= -6x^{-14} \\ d(x^{-15}u) &= -6x^{-14} dx \\ \int d(x^{-15}u) &= \int -6x^{-14} dx \\ x^{-15}u &= \frac{6}{13}x^{-13} + C \\ u &= \frac{6}{13}x^2 + Cx^15 }$
Now to solve for $y$ use the original substitution:
$\eqalign{ y^{-3} &= u \\ y &= u^{-\frac{1}{3}} \\ &= \Big[ \frac{6}{13}x^2 + Cx^15 \Big]^{-\frac{1}{3}} }$
Now using the initial condition, the constant of integration comes out to be:
$\eqalign{ 3 &= y(1) \\ 3 &= \Big[ \frac{6}{13}(1)^2 + C(1)^15 \Big]^{-\frac{1}{3}} \\ (3)^{-3} &= \frac{6}{13} + C \\ C &= -\frac{149}{351} }$
With this the final solution can be written down as:
$\eqalign{ y &= \Big[ \frac{6}{13}x^2 - \frac{149}{351}x^15 \Big]^{-\frac{1}{3}} }$
