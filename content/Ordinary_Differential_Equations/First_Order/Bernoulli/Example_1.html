Problem
Solve the following differential equation:
$\eqalign{ 6y' - 2y = xy^4 }$
where $y(0) = -2$.
Solution
Right away it is clear that this differential equation conforms to the Bernoulli one. To begin divide throughout by the non-linear term:
$\eqalign{ 6y' - 2y &= xy^4 \\ 6y'y^{-4} - 2y^{-3} &= x }$
Now make the substitution:
$\eqalign{ u &= y^{-3} \\ u' &= -3y^{-4}y' }$
and plug in to get:
$\eqalign{ 6y'y^{-4} - 2y^{-3} &= x \\ -2u' - 2u &= x \\ u' + u &= -\frac{x}{2} }$
a first-order linear differential equation. Now an integrating factor is needed:
$\eqalign{ \mu &= e^{\int p(x) dx} \\ &= e^{\int dx} \\ &= e^x }$
Multiplying throughout and solving for $u$ gives:
$\eqalign{ u' + u &= -\frac{x}{2} \\ e^xu' + e^xu &= -\frac{x}{2}e^x \\ \frac{d}{dx}(e^xu) &= -\frac{x}{2}e^x \\ d(e^xu) &= -\frac{x}{2}e^x dx \\ \int d(e^xu) &= \int -\frac{x}{2}e^x \\ e^xu &= -\frac{1}{2}e^x(x - 1) + C \\ u &= -\frac{1}{2}(x - 1) + Ce^{-x} }$
Now to solve for $y$ use the original substitution:
$\eqalign{ y^{-3} &= u \\ y &= u^{-\frac{1}{3}} \\ &= \Big[ -\frac{1}{2}(x - 1) + Ce^{-x} \Big]^{-\frac{1}{3}} }$
Now using the initial condition, the constant of integration comes out to be:
$\eqalign{ -2 &= y(0) \\ -2 &= \Big[ -\frac{1}{2}((0) - 1) + Ce^{-(0)} \Big]^{-\frac{1}{3}} \\ -2 &= \Big[ \frac{1}{2} + C \Big]^{-\frac{1}{3}} \\ (-2)^{-3} &= \frac{1}{2} + C \\ C &= -\frac{5}{8} }$
With this the final solution can be written down as:
$\eqalign{ y &= \Big[ -\frac{1}{2}(x - 1) - \frac{5}{8}e^{-x} \Big]^{-\frac{1}{3}} }$
