Problem
Given the differential equation:
$\eqalign{ y'' + y = \tan(x) }$
where $y''$ represents the second derivative, show that:
$\eqalign{ y = -\cos(x)\ln(\sec(x) + \tan(x)) }$
is a solution.
Solution
First we need to find the first derivative:
$\eqalign{ y &= -\cos(x)\ln(\sec(x) + \tan(x)) \\ \frac{d}{dx} \Big[ y \Big] &= \frac{d}{dx} \Big[ -\cos(x)\ln(\sec(x) + \tan(x)) \Big] \\ y' &= \sin(x)\ln(\sec(x) + \tan(x)) - \cos(x) \cdot \frac{\sec(x)\tan(x) + \sec^2(x)}{\sec(x) + \tan(x)} \\ y' &= \sin(x)\ln(\sec(x) + \tan(x)) - \cos(x)\sec(x) \\ y' &= \sin(x)\ln(\sec(x) + \tan(x)) - 1 }$
Now for the second derivative:
$\eqalign{ y' &= \sin(x)\ln(\sec(x) + \tan(x)) - 1 \\ \frac{d}{dx} \Big[ y' \Big] &= \frac{d}{dx} \Big[ \sin(x)\ln(\sec(x) + \tan(x)) - 1 \Big] \\ y'' &= \cos(x)\ln(\sec(x) + \tan(x)) + \sin(x) \cdot \frac{\sec(x)\tan(x) + \sec^2(x)}{\sec(x) + \tan(x)} \\ y'' &= \cos(x)\ln(\sec(x) + \tan(x)) + \sin(x)\sec(x) \\ y'' &= \cos(x)\ln(\sec(x) + \tan(x)) + \tan(x) }$
With this we are ready to verify the whether this function is a solution or not:
$\eqalign{ y'' + y &= \tan(x) \\ y'' &= \tan(x) - y \\ \cos(x)\ln(\sec(x) + \tan(x)) + \tan(x) &= \tan(x) + \cos(x)\ln(\sec(x) + \tan(x)) \\ 0 &= 0 }$
