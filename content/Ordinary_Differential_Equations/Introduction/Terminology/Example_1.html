Problem
Show that given:
$\eqalign{ y = \frac{t^2}{4} - \frac{t}{3} + \frac{1}{2} + \frac{C}{t^2} \hspace{.3cm} \text{where} \hspace{.3cm} C \in \mathbb{R} }$
it is a solution to the following differential equation:
$\eqalign{ ty' + 2y = t^2 - t + 1 }$
where $y'$ denotes the first derivative.
Solution
First we need the derivative:
$\eqalign{ y &= \frac{t^2}{4} - \frac{t}{3} + \frac{1}{2} + \frac{C}{t^2} \\ y' &= \frac{t}{2} - \frac{1}{3} - \frac{2C}{t^3} }$
Now plugging in gives:
$\eqalign{ \require{cancel} ty' + 2y &= t^2 - t + 1 \\ t\Big( \frac{t}{2} - \frac{1}{3} - \frac{2C}{t^3} \Big) + 2\Big( \frac{t^2}{4} - \frac{t}{3} + \frac{1}{2} + \frac{C}{t^2} \Big) &= t^2 - t + 1 \\ \frac{t^2}{2} - \frac{t}{3} - \cancel{\frac{2C}{t^2}} + \frac{t^2}{2} - \frac{2t}{3} + 1 + \cancel{\frac{2C}{t^2}} &= t^2 - t + 1 \\ t^2 - t + 1 &= t^2 - t + 1 \\ 0 &= 0 }$
which is always true.
