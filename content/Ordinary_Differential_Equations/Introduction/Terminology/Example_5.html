Problem
Given the differential equation:
$\eqalign{ \frac{dy}{dx} + 2xy = 1 }$
show that:
$\eqalign{ y = e^{-x^2} \int_0^x e^{t^2} dt + Ce^{-x^2} \hspace{.3cm} \text{where} \hspace{.3cm} C \in \mathbb{R} }$
is a solution.
Solution
First we need the first derivative which requires taking the derivative of the integral. The integrand happens to not have a known closed form therefore, there must be a different way. Using the $\textit{Second Fundamental Theorem of Calculus}$:
$\eqalign{ y &= e^{-x^2} \int_0^x e^{t^2} dt + Ce^{-x^2} \\ \frac{d}{dx} \Big[ y \Big] &= \frac{d}{dx} \Big[ e^{-x^2} \int_0^x e^{t^2} dt + Ce^{-x^2} \Big] \\ \frac{dy}{dx} &= -2xe^{-x^2}\int_0^x e^{t^2} dt + e^{-x^2} \cdot \frac{d}{dx} \int_0^x e^{t^2} dt - 2Cxe^{-x^2} \\ \frac{dy}{dx} &= -2xe^{-x^2}\int_0^x e^{t^2} dt + e^{-x^2} \cdot e^{x^2} - 2Cxe^{-x^2} \\ \frac{dy}{dx} &= -2xe^{-x^2}\int_0^x e^{t^2} dt + 1 - 2Cxe^{-x^2} }$
Plugging into the differential equation yields:
$\eqalign{ \frac{dy}{dx} + 2xy &= 1 \\ \frac{dy}{dx} &= 1 - 2xy \\ -2xe^{-x^2}\int_0^x e^{t^2} dt + 1 - 2Cxe^{-x^2} &= 1 - 2x\Big[ e^{-x^2} \int_0^x e^{t^2} dt + Ce^{-x^2} \Big] \\ -2xe^{-x^2}\int_0^x e^{t^2} dt - 2Cxe^{-x^2} &= -2xe^{-x^2} \int_0^x e^{t^2} dt - 2Cxe^{-x^2} \\ 0 &= 0 }$
