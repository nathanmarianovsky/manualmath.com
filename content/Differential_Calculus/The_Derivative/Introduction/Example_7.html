Problem
Suppose the position of a particle is modeled by the function:
$\eqalign{ f(x) = 2x^2 - 16x + 30 }$
At what position is the particle not moving? On what intervals is the distance increasing and decreasing?
Solution
To accomplish all of this we need the derivative first:
$\eqalign{ \frac{df}{dx} &= \lim_{h \rightarrow 0} \frac{(2(x + h)^2 - 16(x + h) + 30) - (2x^2 - 16x + 30)}{h} \\ &= \lim_{h \rightarrow 0} \frac{(2x^2 - 16x + 30 + 4hx + 2h^2 - 16h) - (2x^2 - 16x + 30)}{h} \\ &= \lim_{h \rightarrow 0} \frac{4hx + 2h^2 - 16h}{h} \\ &= \lim_{h \rightarrow 0} (4x + 2h - 16) \\ &= 4x - 16 }$
Now for the particle to not be moving, then the instantaneous rate of rate has to equate to zero. This occurs when the derivative is zero:
$\eqalign{ 0 &= \frac{df}{dx} \\ 0 &= 4x - 16 \\ x &= 4 }$
So at $x = 4$ the particle is not moving. Observe that to the left of $x = 4$, the derivative is negative thus giving a negative rate of change. So the distance is decreasing on the interval $(-\infty, 4)$. On the opposite side the derivative is positive, thus producing a positive rate of change. As a result the distance is increasing on the interval $(4, \infty)$.
