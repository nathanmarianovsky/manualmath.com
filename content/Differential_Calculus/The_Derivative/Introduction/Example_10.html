Problem
Explain why a function that is differentiable at a point is also continuous at that point. Does the reverse hold true?
Solution
A function, $f(x)$, that is differentiable at a point states that:
$\eqalign{ f'(a) = \lim_{x \rightarrow a} \frac{f(x) - f(a)}{x - a} }$
and $f'(a)$ is guaranteed to exist. Now assuming that $x \neq a$ we have:
$\eqalign{ \lim_{x \rightarrow a} (f(x) - f(a)) &= \lim_{x \rightarrow a} \Big[ \frac{f(x) - f(a)}{x - a} \cdot (x - a) \Big] \\ &= \lim_{x \rightarrow a} \frac{f(x) - f(a)}{x - a} \cdot \lim_{x \rightarrow a} (x - a) \\ &= f'(a) \cdot 0 \\ &= 0 }$
So we have proven:
$\eqalign{ \lim_{x \rightarrow a} (f(x) - f(a)) = 0 }$
and by using properties of limits:
$\eqalign{ 0 &= \lim_{x \rightarrow a} (f(x) - f(a)) \\ 0 &= \lim_{x \rightarrow a} f(x) - \lim_{x \rightarrow a} f(a) \\ \lim_{x \rightarrow a} f(x) &= \lim_{x \rightarrow a} f(a) \\ \lim_{x \rightarrow a} f(x) &= f(a) }$
The above statement is the exact definition of continuity. As for the reverse order consider the function:
$\eqalign{ k(x) = |x| }$
which is known to be continuous at $x = 0$, but not differentiable. With a single counter example present, the statement does not hold true.
