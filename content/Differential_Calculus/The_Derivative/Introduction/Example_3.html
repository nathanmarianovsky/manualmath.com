Problem
Given the function:
$\eqalign{ f(x) = |x| }$
Determine $f'(0)$.
Solution
From the left side:
$\eqalign{ &\lim_{h \rightarrow 0^-} \frac{f(0 + h) - f(0)}{h} \\ &= \lim_{h \rightarrow 0^-} \frac{-(0 + h) - (-0)}{h} \\ &= \lim_{h \rightarrow 0^-} \frac{-h}{h} \\ &= \lim_{h \rightarrow 0^-} -1 \\ &= -1 }$
and from the right side:
$\eqalign{ &\lim_{h \rightarrow 0^+} \frac{f(0 + h) - f(0)}{h} \\ &= \lim_{h \rightarrow 0^+} \frac{(0 + h) - (0)}{h} \\ &= \lim_{h \rightarrow 0^+} \frac{h}{h} \\ &= \lim_{h \rightarrow 0^+} 1 \\ &= 1 }$
Since the limit does not exist, then neither does the derivative:
$\eqalign{ f'(0) = \text{DNE} }$
