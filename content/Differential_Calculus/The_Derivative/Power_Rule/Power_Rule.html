Definition
Given $f(x) = x^n$ for some $n \in \mathbb{R}$, the derivative is defined as:
$f'(x) = nx^{n-1}$
where the case at $n = 0$, a constant function, is the exception to the rule. This occurs because it is known that the derivative of a constant function is always going to be zero, but according to the power rule it would introduce an unnecessary singularity at $x = 0$.
Proof by Induction for $n \in \mathbb{N}$
First assume that the power rule holds true $\forall n \in \mathbb{N}$. For the base case consider $n = 1$:
$f(x) = x \implies f'(x) = 1$
which is a known fact since the rate of change of a linear function is always equal to constant slope. Now for the inductive step, lets prove that for $n+1$ we have $f'(x) = (n+1)x^n$:
$\eqalign{ f(x) &= x^{n+1} \\ f(x) &= x^n \cdot x \\ \frac{d}{dx} f(x) &= \frac{d}{dx} \Big( x^n \cdot x \Big) \\ f'(x) &= \frac{d}{dx} \Big( x^n \Big) \cdot x + x^n \cdot \frac{d}{dx} \Big( x \Big) \\ f'(x) &= nx^{n-1} \cdot x + x^n \cdot 1 \\ f'(x) &= nx^n + x^n \\ f'(x) &= (n+1)x^n }$
$\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$
Proof with Binomial Theorem for $n \in \mathbb{N}$
A more rigorous approach is to exploit the Binomial Theorem which states that:
$\eqalign{(1 + x)^n = \sum_{i=0}^n {n \choose i} x^i}$
where $n \in \mathbb{N}$ and
$\eqalign{ {n \choose i} = \frac{n!}{i!(n - i)!} }$
is known as the binomial coefficient. Now approach finding the derivative through the normal limit definition:
$\eqalign{ f'(x) &= \lim_{h \rightarrow 0} \frac{(x+h)^n - x^n}{h} \\ &= \lim_{h \rightarrow 0} \frac{1}{h} \Big( \Big(h \Big( 1 + \frac{x}{h} \Big) \Big)^n - x^n \Big) \\ &= \lim_{h \rightarrow 0} \frac{1}{h} \Big( h^n \Big( 1 + \frac{x}{h} \Big)^n - x^n \Big) \\ &= \lim_{h \rightarrow 0} \frac{1}{h} \Big( h^n \sum_{i=0}^n {n \choose i} \Big( \frac{x}{h} \Big)^i - x^n \Big) \\ &= \lim_{h \rightarrow 0} \frac{1}{h} \Big( \sum_{i=0}^n {n \choose i} x^i h^{n-i} - x^n \Big) \\ &= \lim_{h \rightarrow 0} \frac{1}{h} \Big( \sum_{i=0}^{n-1} {n \choose i} x^i h^{n-i} \Big) \\ &= \lim_{h \rightarrow 0} \sum_{i=0}^{n-1} {n \choose i} x^i h^{n-i-1} \\ &= \lim_{h \rightarrow 0} \Big( {n \choose 0} h^{n-1} + {n \choose 1} x h^{n-2} + \dots + {n \choose n-1} x^{n-1} \Big) \\ &= {n \choose n-1} x^{n-1} \\ &= \frac{n!}{(n-1)!(n-(n-1))!} x^{n-1} \\ &= \frac{n!}{(n-1)!} x^{n-1} \\ &= nx^{n-1} }$
$\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$
Proof by Chain Rule for $n \in \mathbb{Q}$
First assume that the power rule holds true $\forall n \in \mathbb{N}$. Now to prove for some $k \in \mathbb{Q}$, let $k = \frac{p}{q}$:
$\eqalign{ \frac{d}{dx} x^k &= \frac{d}{dx} x^\frac{p}{q} \\ &= \frac{d}{dx} \Big( x^p \Big)^\frac{1}{q} \\ &= \frac{1}{q} \Big( x^p \Big)^{\frac{1}{q} - 1} \cdot px^{p-1} \\ &= \frac{p}{q} x^{\frac{p}{q} - p} \cdot x^{p-1} \\ &= \frac{p}{q} x^{\frac{p}{q} - 1} \\ &= k x^{k-1} }$
$\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$
Proof for $n \in \mathbb{R}$
Surprisingly the following proof is the easiest of all in my opinion, and yet it covers the most general case:
$\eqalign{ \frac{d}{dx} x^k &= \frac{d}{dx} e^{\ln(x^k)} \\ &= \frac{d}{dx} e^{k\ln(x)} \\ &= \frac{d}{dx} (k\ln(x)) \cdot e^{k\ln(x)} \\ &= \frac{k}{x} \cdot x^k \\ &= kx^{k - 1} }$
Of course this assumes that we know the chain rule and the derivative of the exponential function. $\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$
