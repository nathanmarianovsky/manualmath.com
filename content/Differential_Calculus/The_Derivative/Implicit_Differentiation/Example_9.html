Problem
Use implicit differentiation to prove that:
$\eqalign{ \frac{d}{dx} \arcsin(x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}} }$
You are allowed to use the derivative of sine.
Solution
Rewrite the equation and differentiate:
$\eqalign{ y &= \arcsin(x) \\ \sin(y) &= x \\ \frac{d}{dx} \sin(y) &= \frac{d}{dx} x \\ \cos(y)y' &= 1 \\ y' &= \frac{1}{\cos(y)} \\ &= \frac{1}{\cos(\arcsin(x))} }$
To make sense of the last line let $\theta = \arcsin(x)$ which implies $\sin(\theta) = x$. A right triangle with such an angle has a opposite side with length $x$ and hypotenuse length $1$. Using the Pythagorean Theorem, the adjacent side has length $\sqrt{1 - x^2}$. So the cosine of the angle is $\cos(\theta) = \sqrt{1 - x^2}$. With this the derivative takes the form:
$\eqalign{ y' &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}} }$
