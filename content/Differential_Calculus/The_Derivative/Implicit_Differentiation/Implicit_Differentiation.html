Explicit Function
Any function that can be written as:
$\eqalign{ y = f(x) }$
is known as an $\textit{explicit}$ function.
Implicit Function
Any function that cannot be written in explicit form, but rather as:
$\eqalign{ F(x,y) = C \hspace{.3cm} \text{where} \hspace{.3cm} C \in \mathbb{R} }$
is known as an $\textit{implicit}$ function.
Implicit Differentiation
Even though $y$ cannot be written as a function of $x$ when dealing with an implicit function, the derivative of $y$ with respect to $x$ can still be determined. The general approach is to take the derivative of both sides. Consider $y$ to be a second variable and apply all of the standard differentiation rules. The only difference that comes up is when $y$ is differentiated, a $y'$ always has to be attached. At the end we solve for $y'$ by isolating it. As an example take the unit circle:
$\eqalign{ x^2 + y^2 = 1 }$
and differentiate both sides to arrive at:
$\eqalign{ 2x + 2yy' = 0 }$
Now solving for $y'$:
$\eqalign{ y' = -\frac{x}{y} }$
Proof of Implicit Differentiation
To understand why the derivative of $y$ with respect to $x$ can be determined when dealing with an implicit function, I must first show you the chain rule for a multivariable function:
$\eqalign{ \frac{d}{dt} F(x(t), y(t)) = \frac{\partial F}{\partial x} \frac{dx}{dt} + \frac{\partial F}{\partial y} \frac{dy}{dt} }$
So applying this to our situation we arrive at:
$\eqalign{ \frac{d}{dx} F(x(x), y(x)) &= \frac{d}{dx} C \\ \frac{\partial F}{\partial x} \frac{dx}{dx} + \frac{\partial F}{\partial y} \frac{dy}{dx} &= 0 \\ \frac{\partial F}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial F}{\partial y} \frac{dy}{dx} &= 0 \\ \frac{\partial F}{\partial y} \frac{dy}{dx} &= -\frac{\partial F}{\partial x} \\ \frac{dy}{dx} &= -\frac{\frac{\partial F}{\partial x}}{\frac{\partial F}{\partial y}} \\ }$
a consistent formula to determine the derivative of $y$ with respect to $x$ so long as the denominator is not equal to zero, which happens only if there are no $y$'s present.
