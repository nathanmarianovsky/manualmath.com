Problem
Find the equation of the tangent line to the graph of:
$\eqalign{ (x^2 + y^2)^3 = 8x^2y^2 }$
at the point $(-1,1)$.
Solution
To accomplish this we are going to find the instantaneous rate of change at the point of interest. First the derivative:
$\eqalign{ \frac{d}{dx} (x^2 + y^2)^3 &= \frac{d}{dx} 8x^2y^2 \\ 3(x^2 + y^2)^2 \cdot (2x + 2yy') &= 16xy^2 + 16x^2yy' \\ y'(6y(x^2 + y^2)^2 - 16x^2y) &= 16xy^2 - 6x(x^2 + y^2)^2 \\ y' &= \frac{16xy^2 - 6x(x^2 + y^2)^2}{6y(x^2 + y^2)^2 - 16x^2y} }$
Now to determine the slope:
$\eqalign{ \frac{dy}{dx} \Bigg|_{(-1,1)} &= \frac{16(-1)(1)^2 - 6(-1)((-1)^2 + (1)^2)^2}{6(1)((-1)^2 + (1)^2)^2 - 16(-1)^2(1)} \\ &= \frac{8}{8} \\ &= 1 }$
Next we form the line as:
$\eqalign{ g(x) = x + b }$
where we evaluate $b$ by using the point already known:
$\eqalign{ g(-1) &= -1 + b \\ 1 &= -1 + b \\ b &= 2 }$
So the final solution is:
$\eqalign{ g(x) = x + 2 }$
