Problem
Prove that the derivative of $h(x) = \arcsin(x)$ is in fact $h'(x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}}$.
Solution
Given a function, $f(x)$, we can determine the derivative of its inverse according to:
$\eqalign{ (f^{-1})'(x) = \frac{1}{f'(f^{-1}(x))} }$
so we have the setup:
$\eqalign{ f(x) &= \sin(x) \\ f'(x) &= \cos(x) \\ f^{-1}(x) &= \arcsin(x) }$
which when plugged in provides:
$\eqalign{ \frac{d}{dx} \arcsin(x) = \frac{1}{\cos(\arcsin(x))} }$
To simplify the denominator, begin by setting:
$\eqalign{ \theta = \arcsin(x) \implies \sin(\theta) = x }$
If you draw out a right triangle and label one of the angles as $\theta$, then opposite the angle is side with length $x$ and hypotenuse with length 1. Using the $\textit{Pythagorean Theorem}$, the adjacent side is $\sqrt{1 - x^2}$. Now to determine the rest:
$\eqalign{ \cos(\theta) &= \frac{\text{adjacent}}{\text{hypotenuse}} \\ &= \sqrt{1 - x^2} }$
which simplifies the derivative down to:
$\eqalign{ \frac{d}{dx} \arcsin(x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}} }$
