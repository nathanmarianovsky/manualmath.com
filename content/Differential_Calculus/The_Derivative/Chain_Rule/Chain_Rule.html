Definition
Given $k(x) = g(f(x))$, the derivative is defined as:
$\eqalign{ k'(x) = g'(f(x)) \cdot f'(x) }$
where $f(x)$ and $g(x)$ must be differentiable at $x$ and $f(x)$ respectively.
Proof by Formal Definition
Using the limit definition of the derivative:
$\eqalign{ k'(x) &= \lim_{h \rightarrow 0} \frac{k(x + h) - k(x)}{h} \\ &= \lim_{h \rightarrow 0} \frac{g(f(x + h)) - g(h(x))}{h} \\ &= \lim_{h \rightarrow 0} \frac{g(f(x + h)) - g(f(x))}{h} \cdot \frac{f(x + h) - f(x)}{f(x + h) - f(x)} \\ &= \lim_{h \rightarrow 0} \frac{g(f(x + h)) - g(f(x))}{h(x + h) - h(x)} \cdot \frac{f(x + h) - f(x)}{h} \\ &= \lim_{h \rightarrow 0} \frac{g(f(x + h)) - g(f(x))}{f(x + h) - f(x)} \cdot \lim_{h \rightarrow 0} \frac{f(x + h) - f(x)}{h} }$
Now the trick is to let $\Delta = h(x+h) - h(x)$ and note that as $h \rightarrow 0$ we have $\Delta \rightarrow 0$ so that:
$\eqalign{ k'(x) &= \lim_{\Delta \rightarrow 0} \frac{g(f(x) + \Delta) - g(f(x))}{\Delta} \cdot \lim_{h \rightarrow 0} \frac{f(x + h) - f(x)}{h} \\ &= g'(f(x)) \cdot f'(x) }$
Now to be a little formal we should consider the fact that during the evaluation an extra factor was brought into the denominator. Usually this does not present a problem unless that factor is zero. So what happens if $\Delta = 0$? To figure this out go back to the beginning of the proof:
$\eqalign{ k'(x) &= \lim_{h \rightarrow 0} \frac{k(x+h) - k(x)}{h} \\ &= \lim_{h \rightarrow 0} \frac{g(f(x + h)) - g(f(x))}{h} \\ &= \lim_{h \rightarrow 0} \frac{g(f(x + \Delta)) - g(f(x))}{h} \\ &= \lim_{h \rightarrow 0} \frac{g(f(x)) - g(f(x))}{h} \\ &= 0 }$
So in the end if that extra factor happens to be zero, then the derivative is equivalently zero. $\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$
Determining the Derivative of the Inverse
A rather useful consequence of the chain rule is the following relationship:
$\eqalign{ (f^{-1})'(x) = \frac{1}{f'(f^{-1}(x))} }$
which provides a method of determining the derivative of the inverse function. Deriving this takes one step when starting from the fundamental relationship between a continuous function and its inverse:
$\eqalign{ f(f^{-1}(x)) &= x \\ \frac{d}{dx} f(f^{-1}(x)) &= \frac{d}{dx} x \\ f'(f^{-1}(x)) \cdot (f^{-1})'(x) &= 1 \\ (f^{-1})'(x) &= \frac{1}{f'(f^{-1}(x))} }$
