Definition
Given $f(x) = g(h(x))$, the derivative is defined as:
$f'(x) = g'(h(x)) \cdot h'(x)$
where a prime denotes the first derivative. For the above to work $h(x)$ and $g(x)$ must be differentiable at $x$ and $h(x)$ respectively.
Proof by Formal Definition
Using the limit definition of the derivative:
$\eqalign{ f'(x) &= \lim_{h \rightarrow 0} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h} \\ &= \lim_{h \rightarrow 0} \frac{g(h(x+h)) - g(h(x))}{h} \\ &= \lim_{h \rightarrow 0} \frac{g(h(x+h)) - g(h(x))}{h} \cdot \frac{h(x+h) - h(x)}{h(x+h) - h(x)} \\ &= \lim_{h \rightarrow 0} \frac{g(h(x+h)) - g(h(x))}{h(x+h) - h(x)} \cdot \frac{h(x+h) - h(x)}{h} \\ &= \lim_{h \rightarrow 0} \frac{g(h(x+h)) - g(h(x))}{h(x+h) - h(x)} \cdot \lim_{h \rightarrow 0} \frac{h(x+h) - h(x)}{h} }$
Now the trick is to let $\Delta = h(x+h) - h(x)$ so that:
$\eqalign{ f'(x) &= \lim_{h \rightarrow 0} \frac{g(h(x) + \Delta) - g(h(x))}{\Delta} \cdot \lim_{h \rightarrow 0} \frac{h(x+h) - h(x)}{h} \\ &= g'(h(x)) \cdot h'(x) }$
Now to be a little formal consider the fact that during the evaluation an extra factor was brought into the denominator. Usually this does not present a problem unless that factor is zero. So what happens if $\Delta = 0$? To figure this out go back to the beginning of the proof:
$\eqalign{ f'(x) &= \lim_{h \rightarrow 0} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h} \\ &= \lim_{h \rightarrow 0} \frac{g(h(x+h)) - g(h(x))}{h} \\ &= \lim_{h \rightarrow 0} \frac{g(h(x + \Delta)) - g(h(x))}{h} \\ &= \lim_{h \rightarrow 0} \frac{g(h(x)) - g(h(x))}{h} \\ &= 0 }$
So in the end if that extra factor happens to be zero, then the derivative is equivalently zero.
