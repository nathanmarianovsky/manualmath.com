Problem
Determine the derivative of $h(x) = \ln(x)$ without using the fact that $h'(x) = \frac{1}{x}$. This essentially means that the standard limit process will not work since it would require $\textit{L'Hospital's Rule}$ which uses the derivative. You are allowed to use the fact that $h^{-1}(x) = e^x$ and that $(h^{-1})'(x) = e^x$.
Solution
Given a function, $f(x)$, we can determine the derivative of its inverse according to:
$\eqalign{ (f^{-1})'(x) = \frac{1}{f'(f^{-1}(x))} }$
so we have the setup:
$\eqalign{ f(x) &= e^x \\ f'(x) &= e^x \\ f^{-1}(x) &= \ln(x) }$
which when plugged in provides:
$\eqalign{ \frac{d}{dx} \ln(x) &= \frac{1}{e^{\ln(x)}} \\ &= \frac{1}{x} }$
