Definition
Given $h(x) = f(x)g(x)$, the derivative is defined as:
$h'(x) = f'(x)g(x) + f(x)g'(x)$
where $f(x)$ and $g(x)$ are differentiable at $x$.
Proof by Formal Definition
Using the limit definition of the derivative and some simple trickery:
$\eqalign{ h'(x) &= \lim_{h \rightarrow 0} \frac{h(x+h) - h(x)}{h} \\ &= \lim_{h \rightarrow 0} \frac{f(x+h)g(x+h) - f(x)g(x)}{h} \\ &= \lim_{h \rightarrow 0} \frac{f(x+h)g(x+h) - f(x)g(x+h) + f(x)g(x+h) - f(x)g(x)}{h} \\ &= \lim_{h \rightarrow 0} \Big( \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h} \cdot g(x+h) \Big) + \lim_{h \rightarrow 0} \Big( f(x+h) \cdot \frac{g(x+h) - g(x)}{h} \Big) \\ &= \lim_{h \rightarrow 0} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h} \cdot \lim_{h \rightarrow 0} g(x+h) + \lim_{h \rightarrow 0} f(x+h) \cdot \lim_{h \rightarrow 0} \frac{g(x+h) - g(x)}{h} \\ &= f'(x)g(x) + f(x)g'(x) }$
Proof by Logarithmic Differentiation
This proof essentially consists of taking the logarithm of both sides, differentiating both sides, and simplifying:
$\eqalign{ h(x) &= f(x)g(x) \\ \ln(h(x)) &= \ln(f(x)g(x)) \\ \ln(h(x)) &= \ln(f(x)) + \ln(g(x)) \\ \frac{d}{dx} \ln(h(x)) &= \frac{d}{dx} \Big( \ln(f(x)) + \ln(g(x)) \Big) \\ \frac{h'(x)}{h(x)} &= \frac{f'(x)}{f(x)} + \frac{g'(x)}{g(x)} \\ h'(x) &= f(x)g(x) \Big( \frac{f'(x)}{f(x)} + \frac{g'(x)}{g(x)} \Big) \\ h'(x) &= f'(x)g(x) + f(x)g'(x) }$
