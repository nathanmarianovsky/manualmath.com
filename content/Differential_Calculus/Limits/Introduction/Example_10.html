Problem
Evaluate:
$\eqalign{ \lim_{x \rightarrow 0} \sum_{i = 0}^n x^i \Bigg[ \prod_{k = 0}^m x^k \Bigg]^{-1} }$
assuming that $n$ and $m$ are finite and no relationship exists between the two.
Solution
This can be broken up into two different scenarios. By directly simplification:
$\eqalign{ \lim_{x \rightarrow 0} \sum_{i = 0}^n x^i \Bigg[ \prod_{k = 0}^m x^k \Bigg]^{-1} &= \lim_{x \rightarrow 0} \sum_{i = 0}^n x^i \Bigg[ (1)(x)(x^2)\dots(x^m) \Bigg]^{-1} \\ &= \lim_{x \rightarrow 0} \sum_{i = 0}^n x^i \Bigg[ x^{1 + 2 + \dots + m} \Bigg]^{-1} \\ &= \lim_{x \rightarrow 0} \sum_{i = 0}^n x^i \Bigg[ x^{\sum_{k = 1}^m k} \Bigg]^{-1} \\ &= \lim_{x \rightarrow 0} \sum_{i = 0}^n x^{i - \sum_{k = 1}^m k} }$
To evaluate the last line, we have to consider separate cases. First if there exists an $i$ s.t. one of the powers is negative, then:
$\eqalign{ \lim_{x \rightarrow 0} \sum_{i = 0}^n x^{i - \sum_{k = 1}^m k} = \infty }$
since one of the terms will have $x$ end up on the bottom, sending the limit to infinity which happens whenever you get infinitesimally close to dividing a positive number by zero. Second if there exists an $i$ s.t. one of the power is zero, then:
$\eqalign{ \lim_{x \rightarrow 0} \sum_{i = 0}^n x^{i - \sum_{k = 1}^m k} = 1 }$
assuming that the rest of the terms have strictly positive powers. Lastly if the powers are all strictly positive, then:
$\eqalign{ \lim_{x \rightarrow 0} \sum_{i = 0}^n x^{i - \sum_{k = 1}^m k} = 0 }$
since it reduces down to a polynomial which is known to be a continuous function.
