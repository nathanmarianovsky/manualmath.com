Definition
In the previous section I discussed the idea of a limit and mentioned that it only exists if the function approaches the same value from the left and right sides. Now to be more formal, I provide for you a representation of the left-limit as $x \rightarrow a$:
$\eqalign{ \lim_{x \rightarrow a^-} f(x) = L_1 }$
and right-limit:
$\eqalign{ \lim_{x \rightarrow a^+} f(x) = L_2 }$
where $L_1$ and $L_2$ are some real numbers that do not necessarily have to be the same. To determine the above limits, we only have to observe one side of the function now rather than making sure both sides equate.
Existence of a Limit
With the above notation for one-sided limits, we can now state that a limit of a function, $f(x)$, as $x \rightarrow a$ will exist iff:
$\eqalign{ \lim_{x \rightarrow a^-} f(x) = \lim_{x \rightarrow a^+} f(x) }$
Vertical Asymptotes
If any one of the following occurs as $x \rightarrow a$ for a function $f(x)$:
$\eqalign{ \lim_{x \rightarrow a^-} f(x) &= -\infty \\ \lim_{x \rightarrow a^-} f(x) &= \infty \\ \lim_{x \rightarrow a^+} f(x) &= -\infty \\ \lim_{x \rightarrow a^+} f(x) &= \infty }$
then the funciton has a $\textit{vertical asymptote}$ at $x = a$.
