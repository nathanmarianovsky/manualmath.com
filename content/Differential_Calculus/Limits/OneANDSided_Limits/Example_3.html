Problem
Given the $\textit{sign}$ function:
$\eqalign{ \DeclareMathOperator{\sgn}{sgn} \sgn(x) = \frac{x}{|x|} }$
determine the following:
$\eqalign{ \lim_{x \rightarrow 0^-} \sgn(x) \hspace{.3cm} \text{and} \hspace{.3cm} \lim_{x \rightarrow 0^+} \sgn(x) }$
using an analytical approach. After having found the results for the above, what can be said of the limit as $x \rightarrow 0$?
Solution
To approach this analytically, we have to split up the sign function into components:
$\eqalign{ \sgn(x) = \begin{cases} -1 & x < 0 \\ 1 & x > 0 \end{cases} }$
where we do not define the function for $x = 0$ since it is a singularity. Now the limits produce:
$\eqalign{ \lim_{x \rightarrow 0^-} \sgn(x) &= -1 \\ \lim_{x \rightarrow 0^+} \sgn(x) &= 1 }$
Since the limits do not equate, the general limit is:
$\eqalign{ \lim_{x \rightarrow 0} \sgn(x) = \text{DNE} }$
