Problem
Prove that:
$\eqalign{ \lim_{x \rightarrow a} (f(x) + g(x)) = \lim_{x \rightarrow a} f(x) + \lim_{x \rightarrow a} g(x) }$
by using the $(\delta, \epsilon)$ definition of the limit and assuming that the limits on the right-hand side exist.
Solution
Assuming that the limit of the functions exist:
$\eqalign{ \lim_{x \rightarrow a} f(x) &= L_1 \\ \lim_{x \rightarrow a} g(x) &= L_2 }$
then we know $\forall \epsilon > 0, \exists \delta_1, \delta_2 > 0$ s.t.:
$\eqalign{ |f(x) - L_1| < \frac{\epsilon}{2} \hspace{.3cm} &\text{whenever} \hspace{.3cm} |x - a| < \delta_1 \\ |f(x) - L_2| < \frac{\epsilon}{2} \hspace{.3cm} &\text{whenever} \hspace{.3cm} |x - a| < \delta_2 }$
We need to show:
$\eqalign{ |(f(x) + g(x)) - (L_1 + L_2)| &< \epsilon \\ \text{whenever} \hspace{.3cm} |x - a| &< \delta }$
So we choose $\delta = \min\{\delta_1, \delta_2\}$ to use the inequalities for $f(x)$ and $g(x)$ to obtain:
$\eqalign{ &|(f(x) + g(x)) - (L_1 + L_2)| \\ &= |(f(x) - L_1) + (g(x) - L_2)| \\ &\leq |f(x) - L_1| + |g(x) - L_2| \\ &< \frac{\epsilon}{2} + \frac{\epsilon}{2} \\ &= \epsilon }$
where the Triangle Inequality was used to break down the absolute value.
