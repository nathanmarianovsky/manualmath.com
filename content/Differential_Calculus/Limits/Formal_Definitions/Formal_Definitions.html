Introduction
All previous sections talked about limits in terms of how they are evaluated and the associated properties, but not once mentioned the basic notion of where limits actually come from and any formal definitions. To fix this, below I present to you the formal definitions of everything covered in the previous sections with proof-based examples to follow.
Definition of a Limit
The formal definition states that the limit:
$\eqalign{ \lim_{x \rightarrow a} f(x) = L }$
exists if $\forall \epsilon > 0, \exists \delta > 0$ s.t.:
$\eqalign{ |f(x) - L| < \epsilon \hspace{.3cm} \text{whenever} \hspace{.3cm} |x - a| < \delta }$
In all essense this is just a rigorous way of stating that a limit at a point will exist if for every range close to the limit, a value can be determined that also instills a range on the independent variable. So no matter how close you get to the limit, there will always exist a restriction on the domain s.t. the closer you get to the limit, the closer you get to $x = a$. Visually this looks like:
Left Limit
The left-hand limit:
$\eqalign{ \lim_{x \rightarrow a^-} f(x) = L_1 }$
exists if $\forall \epsilon > 0, \exists \delta > 0$ s.t.:
$\eqalign{ |f(x) - L_1| < \epsilon \hspace{.3cm} \text{whenever} \hspace{.3cm} -\delta < x - a < 0 }$
This is exactly the same as the definition of the limit, except we now only care about the behavior on the left-hand side as seen on the restriction of $x$.
Right Limit
The right-hand limit:
$\eqalign{ \lim_{x \rightarrow a^+} f(x) = L_2 }$
exists if $\forall \epsilon > 0, \exists \delta > 0$ s.t.:
$\eqalign{ |f(x) - L_2| < \epsilon \hspace{.3cm} \text{whenever} \hspace{.3cm} 0 < x - a < \delta }$
This is exactly the same as the definition of the limit, except we now only care about the behavior on the right-hand side as seen on the restriction of $x$.
Limit Leading to Positive Infinity
We say that:
$\eqalign{ \lim_{x \rightarrow a} f(x) = \infty }$
if $\forall \Omega > 0, \exists \delta > 0$ s.t.:
$\eqalign{ f(x) > \Omega \hspace{.3cm} \text{whenever} \hspace{.3cm} |x - a| < \delta }$
In essense this states that no matter how large a value you pick, $f(x)$ will always attain a larger value on some domain close to the value $x = a$.
Limit Leading to Negative Infinity
We say that:
$\eqalign{ \lim_{x \rightarrow a} f(x) = -\infty }$
if $\forall \Gamma < 0, \exists \delta > 0$ s.t.:
$\eqalign{ f(x) < \Gamma \hspace{.3cm} \text{whenever} \hspace{.3cm} |x - a| < \delta }$
In essense this states that no matter how small a value you pick, $f(x)$ will always attain a smaller value on some domain close to the value $x = a$.
Limit at Positive Infinity
We say that:
$\eqalign{ \lim_{x \rightarrow \infty} f(x) = \kappa_1 }$
if $\forall \epsilon > 0, \exists \Omega > 0$ s.t.:
$\eqalign{ |f(x) - \kappa_1| < \epsilon \hspace{.3cm} \text{whenever} \hspace{.3cm} x > \Omega }$
In essense this states that $f(x)$ will attain values close to $\kappa_1$ at large values of $x$, and as $x$ gets larger $f(x)$ will tend towards exactly $\kappa_1$.
Limit at Negative Infinity
We say that:
$\eqalign{ \lim_{x \rightarrow -\infty} f(x) = \kappa_2 }$
if $\forall \epsilon > 0, \exists \Gamma < 0$ s.t.:
$\eqalign{ |f(x) - \kappa_2| < \epsilon \hspace{.3cm} \text{whenever} \hspace{.3cm} x < \Gamma }$
In essense this states that $f(x)$ will attain values close to $\kappa_2$ at small values of $x$, and as $x$ gets smaller $f(x)$ will tend towards exactly $\kappa_2$.
Continuity at a Point
We say that a function is continuous at a point $x = a$ if $\forall \epsilon > 0, \exists \delta > 0$ s.t.:
$\eqalign{ |f(x) - f(a)| < \epsilon \hspace{.3cm} \text{whenever} \hspace{.3cm} |x - a| < \delta }$
In essense this statement is exactly the same as definition of the limit, except we replace the arbitrary real number, $L$, with the function at that point, $f(a)$.
