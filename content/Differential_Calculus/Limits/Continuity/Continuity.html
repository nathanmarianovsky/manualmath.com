Introduction
Now we finally introduce continuity and unravel the "nice" behavior that is seen across all continuous functions.
Continuity at a Point
A function, $f(x)$, is said to be $\textit{continuous at a point}$, $x = a$, if:
$\eqalign{ \lim_{x \rightarrow a} &f(x) \hspace{.2cm} \text{exists}, \hspace{.1cm} f(a) \hspace{.2cm} \text{is defined,} \\ &\text{and} \hspace{.2cm} f(a) = \lim_{x \rightarrow a} f(x) }$
Continuity on an Interval
A function, $f(x)$, is said to be $\textit{continuous}$ on an interval, $S \subseteq \mathbb{R}$, if it is continuous at every point in $S$.
Continuous Functions
Given two continuous functions, $f(x)$ and $g(x)$, the following are also continuous:
$\eqalign{ \text{Scalar Multiple:}& \hspace{.3cm} \alpha f(x) \hspace{.3cm} \text{where} \hspace{.3cm} \alpha \in \mathbb{R} \\ \text{Sum:}& \hspace{.3cm} f(x) + g(x) \\ \text{Product:}& \hspace{.3cm} f(x)g(x) \\ \text{Quotient:}& \hspace{.3cm} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} \hspace{.3cm} \text{where} \hspace{.3cm} g(x) \neq 0 }$
Limit of Composition of Functions
Given that a function, $f(x)$, is continuous at $x = L$ where:
$\eqalign{ \lim_{x \rightarrow a} g(x) = L }$
then:
$\eqalign{ \lim_{x \rightarrow a} f(g(x)) = f\Big( \lim_{x \rightarrow a} g(x) \Big) }$
Squeeze Theorem
Limits are known to preserve inequalities, so given two functions s.t.:
$\eqalign{ f(x) \leq g(x) }$
then:
$\eqalign{ \lim_{x \rightarrow a} f(x) \leq \lim_{x \rightarrow a} g(x) }$
Using this information consider a scenario where we have three functions s.t.:
$\eqalign{ h(x) \leq f(x) \leq g(x) }$
and it is known that:
$\eqalign{ \lim_{x \rightarrow a} h(x) = \lim_{x \rightarrow a} g(x) = L }$
then:
$\eqalign{ \lim_{x \rightarrow a} f(x) = L }$
Bolzano's Theorem
Given a continuous function on the closed interval $[a,b]$ where $f(a)$ and $f(b)$ have opposite signs, then:
$\eqalign{ \exists \hspace{.2cm} c \in (a,b) \hspace{.5cm} \text{s.t.} f(c) = 0 }$
In other words Bolzano's Theorem guarantees that a continuous function attains a zero on the interval so long as the function evaluation at the endpoints produces opposite signs.
Proof of Bolzano's Theorem
While the theorem is very straight forward, the proof requires a bit more insight. First lets assume that we have a continuous function on the compact interval $[a,b]$ s.t. $f(a) < 0$ and $f(b) > 0$. Now define a new point:
$\eqalign{ a_1 = \frac{a + b}{2} }$
to be the average of the two endpoints where three cases can occur:
$\eqalign{ f(a_1) = 0 \implies &\text{the theorem is proved} \\ f(a_1) > 0 \implies &\text{define a new interval} \\ & I_1 = (a, a_1) \\ f(a_1) < 0 \implies &\text{define a new interval} \\ & I_1 = (a_1, b) \\ }$
Ignoring the trivial case we now have the original interval $I_0 = (a,b)$ and $I_1 \subset I_0$ where the length of the new interval is $|I_1| = \frac{|I_0|}{2}$. Repeating the above procedure will define a succession of open intervals $I_n = (a_n, b_n)$ s.t. $I_{n + 1} \subset I_n$, $|I_n| = 2^{-n} |I_0|$, and $f(a_n) < 0 < f(b_n)$. The succession $c_n = a_n$ and $c_{n + 1} = b_n$ is Cauchy by construction since:
$\eqalign{ \forall m > n \hspace{.5cm} |c_m - c_n| < 2^{-\frac{n}{2}} |I_0| }$
which implies that $c_n$ is convergent, $c_n \rightarrow c \in (a,b)$. Since $a_n$ and $b_n$ are sub-successions they also tend to the same limit. So by construction:
$\eqalign{ f(a_n) &< 0 \\ \lim_{n \rightarrow \infty} f(a_n) &< 0 \\ f\Big( \lim_{n \rightarrow \infty} a_n \Big) &\leq 0 \\ f(c) &\leq 0 }$
and:
$\eqalign{ f(b_n) &> 0 \\ \lim_{n \rightarrow \infty} f(b_n) &> 0 \\ f\Big( \lim_{n \rightarrow \infty} b_n \Big) &\geq 0 \\ f(c) &\geq 0 }$
With both of the restrictions in mind we must have it s.t. $f(c) = 0$. To finish up we state that $c \in (a,b)$ because $f(a) < 0$ and $f(b) > 0$. Of course it is the same process for proving the case where $f(a) > 0$ and $f(b) < 0$ if you consider the scenario for $-f(x)$.
Intermediate Value Theorem
Given a continuous function on the closed interval $[a,b]$, according to the $\textit{Intermediate Value Theorem}$ the function is guaranteed to attain the range:
$\eqalign{ \mathcal{R}(f) = \Big[ \min\{f(a),f(b)\}, \max\{f(a),f(b)\} \Big] }$
In other words:
$\eqalign{ \forall \alpha \in \mathcal{R}(f) \hspace{.1cm} \exists \hspace{.1cm} c \in [a,b] \hspace{.3cm} \text{s.t.} \hspace{.3cm} f(c) = \alpha }$
Proof of Intermediate Value Theorem
Begin by assuming we have a continuous function, $f(x)$, on the compact interval $[a,b]$ and want to know whether a $c \in (a,b)$ exists s.t. $f(c) = L$ where $L$ is a real number between $f(a)$ and $f(b)$. Define a new function:
$\eqalign{ g(x) = f(x) - L }$
and now we know that $g(a)$ and $g(b)$ will have opposite signs. Therefore, by Bolzano's Theorem there will exist a $c \in (a,b)$ s.t. $g(c) = 0$ which implies:
$\eqalign{ f(c) = L }$
Since $L$ is any arbitrary number in between $f(a)$ and $f(b)$, we know that the function must attain every value in between including the boundaries.
