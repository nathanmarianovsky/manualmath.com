Problem
Given the equation:
$\eqalign{ \ln(x) = x^2 - 4 }$
approximate the rightmost solution up to $5$ decimal places by using Halley's Method to determine $x_5$.
Solution
Before anything else we need to determine a suitable interval to search. This can be accomplished by graphing the left and right hand sides independently:
Looking at the graph shows that $[1,3]$ is a proper interval. Now we rewrite the equation as:
$\eqalign{ 0 = \ln(x) - x^2 + 4 }$
and in this scenario Halley's Method takes the form:
$\eqalign{ x_{n + 1} &= x_n - \frac{2(\ln(x_n) - x_n^2 + 4)\Big( \frac{1}{x_n} - 2x_n \Big)}{2\Big( \frac{1}{x_n} - 2x_n \Big)^2 + (\ln(x_n) - x_n^2 + 4)\Big( \frac{1}{x_n^2} + 2 \Big)} }$
We can choose the starting point to be $x = 1$ and the iterations take on the values:
$\textbf{n}$ $\textbf{x}_n$
$0$ $1.00000$
$1$ $1.54545$
$2$ $2.13759$
$3$ $2.18688$
$4$ $2.18689$
$5$ $2.18689$
