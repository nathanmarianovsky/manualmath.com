Problem
Describe the concavity of the function:
$\eqalign{ f(x) = x^2 + \frac{1}{x} }$
and classify the critical points according to the Second Derivative Test.
Solution
To get everything we need, we have to determine both the first and second derivatives:
$\eqalign{ f'(x) &= 2x - \frac{1}{x^2} \\ f''(x) &= 2 + \frac{2}{x^3} }$
Now to determine the critical points we equate the first derivative to zero:
$\eqalign{ 0 &= 2x - \frac{1}{x^2} \\ 0 &= \frac{2x^3 - 1}{x^2} \\ 0 &= 2x^3 - 1 \\ \frac{1}{2} &= x^3 \\ x &= \frac{1}{2^{\frac{1}{3}}} }$
and to classify the point we plug it into the second derivative:
$\eqalign{ f''(0) &= \hspace{.1cm} \text{DNE} \\ f''\Big( \frac{1}{2^{\frac{1}{3}}} \Big) &= 6 }$
So we arrive at stating that:
$\eqalign{ x = \frac{1}{2^{\frac{1}{3}}} \hspace{.3cm} \text{is a relative minimum} }$
To observe the concavity behavior we need to determine where the second derivative is zero:
$\eqalign{ 0 &= 2 + \frac{2}{x^3} \\ 0 &= \frac{2x^3 + 2}{x^3} \\ 0 &= x^3 + 1 \\ x &= -1 }$
which allows us to state:
$\eqalign{ f(x)& \hspace{.3cm} \text{is concave upwards on} \hspace{.3cm} (-\infty, -1) \\ f(x)& \hspace{.3cm} \text{is concave downwards on} \hspace{.3cm} (-1, 0) \\ f(x)& \hspace{.3cm} \text{is concave upwards on} \hspace{.3cm} (0, \infty) }$
