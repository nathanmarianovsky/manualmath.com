Inflection Points
An $\textit{inflection point}$ of a function is any value that satisfies:
$\eqalign{ f''(x) = 0 \hspace{.3cm} \text{or} \hspace{.3cm} f''(x) = \text{DNE} }$
where there are no restrictions on the amount of inflection points that a function may attain.
A Function's Behavior Due to the Second Derivative
Any function that satisfies the condition
$\eqalign{ f(x) > f(a) &+ f'(a)(x - a) \hspace{.3cm} \forall x \in I \\ &\text{where} \hspace{.3cm} a \in I \subseteq \mathbb{R} }$
is a $\textit{strictly concave upwards}$ function. In other terms a continuously differentiable function is $\textit{convex}$ iff the function lies above all of its tangents. The behavior is attained exactly when the second derivative is positive, but on the other hand any function with a negative second derivative will satisfy:
$\eqalign{ f(x) < f(a) &+ f'(a)(x - a) \hspace{.3cm} \forall x \in I \\ &\text{where} \hspace{.3cm} a \in I \subseteq \mathbb{R} }$
which is to say that the function is $\textit{strictly concave downwards}$. In other terms a continuously differentiable function is $\textit{concave}$ iff the function lies below all of its tangents. Lastly if the second derivative is exactly zero, then the function must be a line since it is exactly the shape with no bending anywhere.
Proof of Concavity Behavior
By the Mean Value Theorem we have the setup:
$\eqalign{ \exists \hspace{.1cm} c &\in (a,x) \hspace{.3cm} \text{s.t.} \\ &f(x) - f(a) = f'(c)(x - a) }$
If we now assume that the second derivative is positive, then the first derivative must be increasing:
$\eqalign{ f'(c) &> f'(a) \\ f'(c)(x - a) &> f'(a)(x - a) \\ f(a) + f'(c)(x - a) &> f(a) + f'(a)(x - a) \\ f(x) &> f(a) + f'(a)(x - a) }$
where the last line states that our function will lie strictly above its tangents. $\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$ Now for the second case we assume that the second derivative is negative, thus the first derivative is decreasing:
$\eqalign{ f'(c) &< f'(a) \\ f'(c)(x - a) &< f'(a)(x - a) \\ f(a) + f'(c)(x - a) &< f(a) + f'(a)(x - a) \\ f(x) &< f(a) + f'(a)(x - a) }$
where the last line states that our function will lie strictly below its tangents. $\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$ For the final case where the second derivative is zero, we begin by noting that with the Mean Value Theorem:
$\eqalign{ \exists \hspace{.1cm} c &\in (a,x) \hspace{.3cm} \text{s.t.} \\ &f'(x) - f'(a) = f''(c)(x - a) }$
which provides that:
$\eqalign{ f(x) = f(a) }$
since the second derivative is zero, and holds true for all points in the interval since $a$ and $x$ are arbitrary. Using this setup again:
$\eqalign{ \exists \hspace{.1cm} c &\in (a,x) \hspace{.3cm} \text{s.t.} \\ &f(x) - f(a) = f'(c)(x - a) }$
which provides us with:
$\eqalign{ f(x) - f(a) &= f'(c)(x - a) \\ f(x) &= f(a) + f'(c)(x - a) }$
with a line because the first derivative is constant for all values in the interval. $\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$
The Second Derivative Test
Similar to the first derivative, the second derivative also has a test associated to it that can determine the extrema type for a critical point. Specifically, according to the $\textit{Second Derivative Test}$ we identify a critical point, $x = c$, as:
$f''(c)$ $\textbf{Type of Extrema}$
$+$ Minimum
$-$ Maximum
$0$ Can be either or neither
Proof of the Second Derivative Test
By the Mean Value Theorem we have the setup:
$\eqalign{ \exists \hspace{.1cm} c &\in (a,x) \hspace{.3cm} \text{s.t.} \\ &f'(x) - f'(a) = f''(c)(x - a) }$
If the point we center ourselves at $x = a$ is the critical point then we reduce the above down to:
$\eqalign{ \exists \hspace{.1cm} c &\in (a,x) \hspace{.3cm} \text{s.t.} \\ &f'(x) = f''(c)(x - a) }$
So now if the second derivative is positive, then we must have the first derivative be positive as we approach from the right side and negative from the left side. With this behavior we can classify this critical point as a minimum due to the First Derivative Test. $\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$ On the other hand if the second derivative is negative the exact opposite behavior occurs giving a maximum due to the First Derivative Test. $\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$ Lastly to see why the second derivative equating to zero is useless information, observe the three following functions:
$\eqalign{ f_1(x) &= x^2 \\ f_2(x) &= -x^2 \\ f_3(x) &= x^3 }$
All three have a single critical point at $x = 0$. The first one will produce a minimum, the second a maximum, and the third neither. What makes this setup the most interesting is that all of the second derivatives evaluate to zero at the critical point. It is exactly because of a counterexample like this that we cannot say anything about critical points with zero second derivative. $\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$
