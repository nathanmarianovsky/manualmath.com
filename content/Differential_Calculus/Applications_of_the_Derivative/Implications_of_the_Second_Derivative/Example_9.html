Problem
Describe the concavity along with the inflection points of the graph corresponding to:
$\eqalign{ x^2 + y^2 = c^2 \hspace{.3cm} \text{where} \hspace{.3cm} c \in \mathbb{R} }$
Solution
To get a feeling for the concavity we need the second derivative, but before that we determine the first derivative using implicit differentiation:
$\eqalign{ \frac{d}{dx} \Big[ x^2 + y^2 \Big] &= \frac{d}{dx} \Big[ c^2 \Big] \\ 2x + 2yy' &= 0 \\ y' &= -\frac{x}{y} }$
Applying the differentiation operator again:
$\eqalign{ \frac{d}{dx} \Big[ y' \Big] &= \frac{d}{dx} \Big[ -\frac{x}{y} \Big] \\ y'' &= -\frac{y - xy'}{y^2} \\ &= -\frac{y + \frac{x^2}{y}}{y^2} \\ &= -\frac{y^2 + x^2}{y^3} }$
Now we observe the behavior of the above. Notice that the numerator is strictly positive, and thus the overall sign behavior will depend strictly on the denominator. So if $y$ is positive the second derivative is overall negative, and vice versa for negative $y$. When observing that the original implicit function represents a circle centered at the origin with radius $c$, the results make sense since the top half is concave down corresponding to positive $y$, and bottom half is concave up corresponding to negative $y$.
