Problem
In the context of Special Relativity, the $\textit{Kinetic Energy}$ is defined as:
$\eqalign{ E_k = \sqrt{p^2c^2 + m^2c^4} - mc^2 }$
where $p$ is the momentum, $m$ is the rest mass, and $c$ is the speed of light. By using a linear approximation with respect to $p$, and the classical definition:
$\eqalign{ p = mv }$
where $v$ is the velocity, show that:
$\eqalign{ E_k \approx \frac{1}{2}mv^2 }$
Solution
From the previous example we know the following:
$\eqalign{ \sqrt{1 + x} \approx 1 + \frac{1}{2}x }$
So in our scenario we have:
$\eqalign{ E_k &= \sqrt{p^2c^2 + m^2c^4} - mc^2 \\ &= mc^2\sqrt{1 + \Big( \frac{p}{m^2c^2} \Big)^2} - mc^2 \\ &= mc^2\Big[ \sqrt{1 + \Big( \frac{p}{m^2c^2} \Big)^2} - 1 \Big] \\ &\approx mc^2\Big[ \Big( 1 + \frac{p^2}{2m^2c^2} \Big) - 1 \Big] \\ &= mc^2\Big[ \frac{p^2}{2m^2c^2} \Big] \\ &= \frac{p^2}{2m} \\ &= \frac{(mv)^2}{2m} \\ &= \frac{1}{2}mv^2 \\ }$
