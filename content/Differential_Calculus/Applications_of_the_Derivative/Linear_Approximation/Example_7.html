Problem
Determine a linear approximation to the function:
$\eqalign{ f(x) = mx + b \hspace{.3cm} \text{near} \hspace{.3cm} x = 0 \hspace{.3cm} \text{where} \hspace{.3cm} m,b \in \mathbb{R} }$
Why does the approximation come out to be the original function?
Solution
To determine a linear approximation we need the first derivative:
$\eqalign{ f'(x) = m }$
which evaluated at $x = 0$ produces:
$\eqalign{ f'(0) = m }$
Now we can write down the approximation as:
$\eqalign{ f(x) &\approx f(0) + f'(0)(x - 0) \\ &= b + m(x - 0) \\ &= mx + b }$
and it should be no surprise that the approximation is in fact the original function. This happens in this scenario specifically because a linear approximation uses a line to approximate, and so when approximating a line with a line you get back the result that the original line is the best one for approximation.
