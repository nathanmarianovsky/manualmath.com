Statement
Given two differentiable functions $f(x)$ and $g(x)$ s.t.:
$\eqalign{ \lim_{x \rightarrow c} f(x) = \lim_{x \rightarrow c} g(x) = 0 \hspace{.3cm} \text{or} \hspace{.3cm} \lim_{x \rightarrow c} f(x) = \lim_{x \rightarrow c} g(x) = \pm \infty \hspace{.3cm} \text{where} \hspace{.3cm} c \in \mathbb{R} }$
then $\textit{L'Hospital's Rule}$ states that:
$\eqalign{ \lim_{x \rightarrow c} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \lim_{x \rightarrow c} \frac{f'(x)}{g'(x)} }$
In essense this provides a systematic approach to determining limits that involve indeterminate forms, but note that if the limit does not approach either $\frac{0}{0}$ or $\pm \frac{\infty}{\infty}$, then the rule cannot be used. In some cases the new limit may also approach an indeterminate form which gives reason to use the rule again, except using the second derivatives. In general if you keep getting the indeterminate forms you keep taking derivatives until it approaches something either finite or non-existant.
Proof of $\frac{0}{0}$
Given two differentiable functions $k(x)$ and $j(x)$ s.t.:
$\eqalign{ k(c) = j(c) = 0 \hspace{.3cm} \text{and} \hspace{.3cm} j'(c) \neq 0 }$
then assuming that $x \neq c$ we do the following:
$\eqalign{ \lim_{x \rightarrow c} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} &= \lim_{x \rightarrow c} \frac{f(x) - 0}{g(x) - 0} \\ &= \lim_{x \rightarrow c} \frac{f(x) - f(c)}{g(x) - g(c)} \\ &= \lim_{x \rightarrow c} \frac{\frac{f(x) - f(c)}{x - c}}{\frac{g(x) - g(c)}{x - c}} \\ &= \Bigg[ \lim_{x \rightarrow c} \frac{f(x) - f(c)}{x - c} \Bigg]\Bigg[ \lim_{x \rightarrow c} \frac{g(x) - g(c)}{x - c} \Bigg]^{-1} \\ &= \frac{f'(c)}{g'(c)} \\ &= \Bigg[ \lim_{x \rightarrow c} f'(x) \Bigg]\Bigg[ \lim_{x \rightarrow c} g'(x) \Bigg]^{-1} \\ &= \lim_{x \rightarrow c} \frac{f'(x)}{g'(x)} }$
$\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$
Proof of $\frac{0}{0}$ Using Cauchy's Mean Value Theorem
Given two differentiable functions $k(x)$ and $j(x)$ s.t.:
$\eqalign{ k(a) = j(a) = 0 \hspace{.3cm} \text{and} \hspace{.3cm} j'(a) \neq 0 }$
then by Cachy's Mean Value Theorem on the interval $[a,b]$:
$\eqalign{ \exists \hspace{.1cm} c \in (a,b) \hspace{.3cm} \text{s.t.} \hspace{.3cm} k'(c)(j(b) - j(a)) = j'(c)(k(b) - k(a)) }$
Now we can take any pair of domains $[a,x]$ and $(a,x)$ s.t. both functions are continuous and differentiable on them respectively where $x \leq b$. Using the Mean Value Theorem and our given situation:
$\eqalign{ k'(c)j(x) &= j'(c)k(x) \\ \frac{k'(c)}{j'(c)} &= \frac{k(x)}{j(x)} }$
To finish up we carefully apply limits that will push the variables to the same spot:
$\eqalign{ \frac{k'(c)}{j'(c)} &= \frac{k(x)}{j(x)} \\ \lim_{c \rightarrow a^+} \frac{k'(c)}{j'(c)} &= \lim_{x \rightarrow a^+} \frac{k(x)}{j(x)} \\ \lim_{x \rightarrow a^+} \frac{k'(x)}{j'(x)} &= \lim_{x \rightarrow a^+} \frac{k(x)}{j(x)} }$
Since $a$ was chosen randomly this applies to any real number, and not just from the right side. $\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$
Proof of $\pm \frac{\infty}{\infty}$
Given two differentiable functions $k(x)$ and $j(x)$ s.t.:
$\eqalign{ k, j \rightarrow \pm \infty \hspace{.3cm} \text{as} \hspace{.3cm} x \rightarrow c }$
Now suppose that we define the limit:
$\eqalign{ L = \lim_{x \rightarrow c} \frac{k(x)}{j(x)} \hspace{.3cm} \text{where} \hspace{.3cm} L \in \mathbb{R} \setminus \{0\} }$
If we accept L'Hospital's Rule for the case of $\frac{0}{0}$, then we can state:
$\eqalign{ L &= \lim_{x \rightarrow c} \frac{k(x)}{j(x)} \\ &= \lim_{x \rightarrow c} \frac{\frac{1}{j(x)}}{\frac{1}{k(x)}} \\ &= \lim_{x \rightarrow c} \frac{-\frac{j'(x)}{(j(x))^2}}{-\frac{k'(x)}{(k(x))^2}} \\ &= \lim_{x \rightarrow c} \frac{(k(x))^2j'(x)}{(j(x))^2k'(x)} \\ &= \lim_{x \rightarrow c} \Big( \frac{k(x)}{j(x)} \Big)^2 \cdot \lim_{x \rightarrow c} \frac{j'(x)}{k'(x)} \\ &= L^2 \cdot \lim_{x \rightarrow c} \frac{j'(x)}{k'(x)} \\ 0 &= L\Big( L \cdot \lim_{x \rightarrow c} \frac{j'(x)}{k'(x)} - 1 \Big) \\ L &= \lim_{x \rightarrow c} \frac{k'(x)}{j'(x)} }$
where $L$ cannot be zero at the last line due to our initial assumption. To handle the zero case let us assume:
$\eqalign{ 0 = \lim_{x \rightarrow c} \frac{k(x)}{j(x)} }$
Observe how the above can be used with some trickery:
$\eqalign{ 1 + \lim_{x \rightarrow c} \frac{k(x)}{j(x)} &= \lim_{x \rightarrow c} \frac{j(x) + k(x)}{j(x)} \\ &= \lim_{x \rightarrow c} \frac{j'(x) + k'(x)}{j'(x)} \\ &= 1 + \lim_{x \rightarrow c} \frac{k'(x)}{j'(x)} \\ \lim_{x \rightarrow c} \frac{k(x)}{j(x)} &= \lim_{x \rightarrow c} \frac{k'(x)}{j'(x)} }$
$\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$
Proof of Limits at Infinity
Say we have the following scenario:
$\eqalign{ \lim_{x \rightarrow \infty} \frac{k(x)}{j(x)} }$
Now we define a new variable:
$\eqalign{ t = \frac{1}{x} }$
so as to rewrite the limit and use L'Hospital's Rule for a case we have already proven:
$\eqalign{ \lim_{x \rightarrow \infty} \frac{k(x)}{j(x)} &= \lim_{t \rightarrow 0^+} \frac{k\Big( \frac{1}{t} \Big)}{j\Big( \frac{1}{t} \Big)} \\ &= \lim_{t \rightarrow 0^+} \frac{-\frac{k'\Big( \frac{1}{t} \Big)}{t^2}}{-\frac{j'\Big( \frac{1}{t} \Big)}{t^2}} \\ &= \lim_{t \rightarrow 0^+} \frac{k'\Big( \frac{1}{t} \Big)}{j'\Big( \frac{1}{t} \Big)} \\ &= \lim_{x \rightarrow \infty} \frac{k'(x)}{j'(x)} }$
The remaining case can be accomplished in a similar fashion:
$\eqalign{ \lim_{x \rightarrow -\infty} \frac{k(x)}{j(x)} &= \lim_{t \rightarrow 0^-} \frac{k\Big( \frac{1}{t} \Big)}{j\Big( \frac{1}{t} \Big)} \\ &= \lim_{t \rightarrow 0^-} \frac{-\frac{k'\Big( \frac{1}{t} \Big)}{t^2}}{-\frac{j'\Big( \frac{1}{t} \Big)}{t^2}} \\ &= \lim_{t \rightarrow 0^-} \frac{k'\Big( \frac{1}{t} \Big)}{j'\Big( \frac{1}{t} \Big)} \\ &= \lim_{x \rightarrow -\infty} \frac{k'(x)}{j'(x)} }$
$\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$
