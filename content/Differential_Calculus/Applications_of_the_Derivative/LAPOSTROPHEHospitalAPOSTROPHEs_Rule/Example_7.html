Problem
Evaluate the following using L'Hospital's Rule:
$\eqalign{ \lim_{x \rightarrow 0^+} x^{\sin(x)} }$
Solution
The initial limit will approach:
$\eqalign{ \lim_{x \rightarrow 0^+} x^{\sin(x)} = 0^0 }$
The result is an indeterminate form, and so using the logarithm:
$\eqalign{ y &= x^{\sin(x)} \\ \ln(y) &= \sin(x) \ln(x) \\ \lim_{x \rightarrow 0^+} \ln(y) &= \lim_{x \rightarrow 0^+} \sin(x) \ln(x) \\ \ln\Big( \lim_{x \rightarrow 0^+} y \Big) &= \lim_{x \rightarrow 0^+} \sin(x) \ln(x) }$
Now the limit approaches:
$\eqalign{ \lim_{x \rightarrow 0^+} \frac{\ln(x)}{\csc(x)} = \frac{\infty}{\infty} }$
This provides reasoning to use L'Hospital's Rule:
$\eqalign{ \lim_{x \rightarrow 0^+} \frac{\ln(x)}{\csc(x)} &= \lim_{x \rightarrow 0^+} -\frac{\frac{1}{x}}{\csc(x)\cot(x)} \\ &= -\lim_{x \rightarrow 0^+} \frac{\sin(x)\tan(x)}{x} \\ &= \frac{0}{0} }$
Since the result is still an indeterminate form, we use the rule once more:
$\eqalign{ \lim_{x \rightarrow 0^+} -\frac{\sin(x)\tan(x)}{x} &= \lim_{x \rightarrow 0^+} -(\sin(x) + \sec(x)\tan(x)) \\ &= 0 }$
This allows us to state:
$\eqalign{ \ln\Big( \lim_{x \rightarrow 0^+} y \Big) &= \lim_{x \rightarrow 0^+} \sin(x) \ln(x) \\ \ln\Big( \lim_{x \rightarrow 0^+} y \Big) &= 0 \\ \lim_{x \rightarrow 0^+} y &= 1 \\ \lim_{x \rightarrow 0^+} x^{\sin(x)} &= 1 }$
