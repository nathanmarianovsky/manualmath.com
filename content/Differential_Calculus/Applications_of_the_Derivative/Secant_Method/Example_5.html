Problem
Given the equation:
$\eqalign{ \ln(x) = x^2 - 4 }$
approximate the rightmost solution up to $5$ decimal places by using the Secant Method to determine $x_6$. Let $x_0$ and $x_1$ represent the two initial points.
Solution
Before anything else we need to determine a suitable interval to search. This can be accomplished by graphing the left and right hand sides independently:
Looking at the graph shows that $[1,3]$ is a proper interval. Now we rewrite the equation as:
$\eqalign{ 0 = \ln(x) - x^2 + 4 }$
and in this scenario the Secant Method takes the form:
$\eqalign{ &x_{n + 1} \\ &= x_n - \Big[ (\ln(x_n) - x_n^2 + 4)(x_n - x_{n - 1}) \Big] \\ & \hspace{.2cm} \cdot \Big[ (\ln(x_n) - \ln(x_{n - 1})) - (x_n^2 - x_{n - 1}^2) \Big]^{-1} }$
We can choose the starting points to be $x_0 = 1$ and $x_1 = 3$. Now the iterations take on the values:
$\textbf{n}$ $\textbf{x}_n$
$0$ $1.00000$
$1$ $3.00000$
$2$ $1.86939$
$3$ $2.12349$
$4$ $2.19334$
$5$ $2.18677$
$6$ $2.18689$
