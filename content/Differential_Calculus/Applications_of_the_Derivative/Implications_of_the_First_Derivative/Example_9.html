Problem
Find the point $(x,y)$ on the graph:
$\eqalign{ y = 4 - x^{\frac{1}{2}} }$
that is closest to the point $(3,4)$.
Solution
We setup the distance function to be:
$\eqalign{ d^2(x) &= (x - x_0)^2 + (y - y_0)^2 \\ &= (x - 3)^2 + (4 - x^{\frac{1}{2}} - 4)^2 \\ &= (x - 3)^2 + x }$
Now we want to minimize the distance function, which is equivalent to minimizing the distance squared. So let:
$\eqalign{ f(x) = d^2(x) }$
where the derivative is:
$\eqalign{ f'(x) = 2(x - 3) + 1 = 2x - 5 }$
providing the critical point:
$\eqalign{ x = \frac{5}{2} }$
Now we observe the behavior of the derivative:
Domain $f'(x)$ Behavior
$\Big( -\infty, \frac{5}{2} \Big)$ $-$
$\Big( \frac{5}{2}, \infty \Big)$ $+$
According to the First Derivative Test we can state that our critical point is a relative minimum. Therefore, the closest point is:
$\eqalign{ \Bigg( \frac{5}{2}, 4 - \sqrt{\frac{5}{2}} \Bigg) }$
