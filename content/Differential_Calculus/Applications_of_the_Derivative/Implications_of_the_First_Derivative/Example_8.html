Problem
Let:
$\eqalign{ f(x) = ax^2 + bx + c \hspace{.3cm} \text{where} \hspace{.3cm} a \neq 0 }$
Show that $f$ has exactly one critical point and give conditions on $a$ and $b$ which guarantee that the critical point will be a maximum.
Solution
First we need the derivative:
$\eqalign{ f'(x) = 2ax + b }$
where the critical point is:
$\eqalign{ 0 &= 2ax + b \\ x &= -\frac{b}{2a} }$
Now we observe that the derivative is nothing but a straight line and if we want the critical point to be a relative maximum then by the First Derivative Test the derivative has to transition from positive to negative. This transition can only occur if the slope is negative and no matter the value for $b$. Therefore, to always attain a maximum we enforce:
$\eqalign{ a < 0 \hspace{.3cm} \text{and} \hspace{.3cm} b \in \mathbb{R} }$
