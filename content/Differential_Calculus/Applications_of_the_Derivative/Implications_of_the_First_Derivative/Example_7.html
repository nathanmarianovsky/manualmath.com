Problem
For the function:
$\eqalign{ f(x) = x^4 - 4x^3 }$
provide a rough sketch based on the behavior of the derivative.
Solution
To observe the behavior we need the first derivative:
$\eqalign{ f'(x) = 4x^3 - 12x^2 }$
where the critical points are:
$\eqalign{ 0 &= 4x^3 - 12x^2 \\ &= 4x^2(x - 3) \\ x &= 0, 3 }$
Now we observe the behavior of the derivative:
Domain $f'(x)$ Behavior
$(-\infty, 0)$ $-$
$(0, 3)$ $-$
$(3, \infty)$ $+$
With this we can state:
$\eqalign{ f(x) \hspace{.3cm} &\text{is decreasing on} \hspace{.3cm} (-\infty, 0) \cup (0, 3) \\ f(x) \hspace{.3cm} &\text{is increasing on} \hspace{.3cm} (3, \infty) \\ x = 3 \hspace{.3cm} &\text{is a relative minimum} }$
Something else that may interest us are the roots:
$\eqalign{ 0 &= x^4 - 4x^3 \\ &= x^3(x - 4) \\ x &= 0, 4 }$
