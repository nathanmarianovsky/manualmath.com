Problem
Show that the function:
$\eqalign{ g(x) = \ln(f(x)) }$
attains the same critical point(s) as $f(x)$ and that at the critical point(s) $x = c$:
$\eqalign{ f(c) = \lim_{x \rightarrow c} e^{g(x)} }$
Also show how on a bounded interval, $[a,b]$, the absolute extremas for $f(x)$ can be obtained through $g(x)$.
Solution
Differentiating the function gives:
$\eqalign{ g'(x) = \frac{f'(x)}{f(x)} }$
where the critical points are:
$\eqalign{ g'(x) &= 0 \hspace{.3cm} \text{whenever} \hspace{.3cm} f(x) \neq 0, f'(x) = 0 \\ g'(x) &= \text{DNE} \hspace{.3cm} \text{whenever} \hspace{.3cm} f(x) = 0 }$
Since we care about $f'(x)$ equating to zero typically we only care about the first scenario. Next we can use the original relationship:
$\eqalign{ \ln(f(x)) &= g(x) \\ f(x) &= e^{g(x)} \\ \lim_{x \rightarrow c} f(x) &= \lim_{x \rightarrow c} e^{g(x)} \\ f(c) &= \lim_{x \rightarrow c} e^{g(x)} }$
and so if we wanted to determine the absolute extremas we would compare the values:
$\eqalign{ f(a) &= \lim_{x \rightarrow a^+} e^{g(x)} \\ f(c) &= \lim_{x \rightarrow c} e^{g(x)} \\ f(b) &= \lim_{x \rightarrow b^-} e^{g(x)} }$
