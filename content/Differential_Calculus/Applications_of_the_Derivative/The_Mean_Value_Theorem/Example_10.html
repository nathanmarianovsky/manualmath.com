Problem
Prove the following: Given two function, $f(x)$ and $h(x)$, that satisfy all of the following:
$\eqalign{ &1. \hspace{.3cm} \text{Continuous on the closed interval} \hspace{.1cm} [a,b] \\ &2. \hspace{.3cm} \text{Differentiable on the open interval} \hspace{.1cm} (a,b) }$
then:
$\eqalign{ \exists \hspace{.1cm} c \in &(a,b) \hspace{.3cm} \text{s.t.} \\ f'(c)(h(b) - h(a)) &= h'(c)(f(b) - f(a)) }$
This is known as Cauchy's Mean Value Theorem and can be approached with a method similar to the proof of the Mean Value Theorem.
Solution
To prove the generalized mean value theorem we define a new function:
$\eqalign{ g(x) = f(x) - \kappa h(x) \hspace{.3cm} \text{where} \hspace{.3cm} \kappa \in \mathbb{R} }$
that also satisfies the two conditions. We now want to determine a value for $\kappa$ s.t. the function satisfies Rolle's Theorem:
$\eqalign{ f(a) - \kappa h(a) &= f(b) - \kappa h(b) \\ \kappa(h(b) - h(a)) &= f(b) - f(a) \\ \kappa &= \frac{f(b) - f(a)}{h(b) - h(a)} }$
With our constant chosen, all of the conditions are met for Rolle's Theorem. Therefore:
$\eqalign{ \exists \hspace{.1cm} c \in (a,b) \hspace{.3cm} \text{s.t.} \hspace{.3cm} g'(c) = 0 }$
and directly by our definition of $g$:
$\eqalign{ g'(c) &= 0 \\ f'(c) - \kappa h'(c) &= 0 \\ f'(c) &= \kappa h'(c) \\ f'(c) &= \frac{f(b) - f(a)}{h(b) - h(a)} h'(c) \\ f'(c)(h(b) - h(a)) &= h'(c)(f(b) - f(a)) }$
