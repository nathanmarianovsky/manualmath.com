Problem
Show that the equation:
$\eqalign{ 2x^3 + 3x^2 + 6x + 1 = 0 }$
has exactly one real root.
Solution
Consider the two points:
$\eqalign{ f(-1) &= -4 \\ f(0) &= 1 }$
and so by the Intermediate Value Theorem we know that:
$\eqalign{ \exists \hspace{.1cm} \in [-1,0] \hspace{.3cm} \text{s.t.} \hspace{.3cm} f(c) = 0 }$
Now to prove that there is exactly one root, we assume that there exist two roots s.t.:
$\eqalign{ f(\alpha) = f(\beta) = 0 }$
If this holds true, then by Rolle's Theorem we have the setup:
$\eqalign{ f'(x) &= 0 \\ 6x^2 + 6x + 6 &= 0 \\ x^2 + x + 1 &= 0 \\ x &= \frac{-1 \pm \sqrt{1 - 4}}{2} \\ x &= \frac{-1 \pm \sqrt{3}i}{2} }$
Since the setup for Rolle's Theorem does not have an associated value, we have a contradiction to our original assumption. Thus there is exactly one root that is real.
