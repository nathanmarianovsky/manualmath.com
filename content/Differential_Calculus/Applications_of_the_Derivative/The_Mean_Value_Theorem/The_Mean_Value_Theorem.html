Introduction
In this section I introduce a couple of theorems that pertain deeply to differential calculus starting with Fermat's Theorem, the Extreme Value Theorem, Rolle's Theorem, and finally the Mean Value Theorem.
Fermat's Theorem
A differentiable function has a local extremum at some point if its derivative is exactly zero at that point.
Proof of Fermat's Theorem
This proof requires showing implication in both directions. So to begin I will first assume that for our function:
$\eqalign{ f: \hspace{.1cm} (a,b) \rightarrow \mathbb{R} }$
we have:
$\eqalign{ c \in (a,b) \hspace{.3cm} \text{s.t.} \hspace{.3cm} f(c) \geq f(c + h) \hspace{.3cm} \text{where} \hspace{.3cm} h \neq 0 }$
Using the inequality:
$\eqalign{ f(c + h) - f(c) &\leq 0 \\ \frac{f(c + h) - f(c)}{h} &\leq 0 \\ \lim_{h \rightarrow 0^+} \frac{f(c + h) - f(c)}{h} &\leq \lim_{h \rightarrow 0^+} 0 \\ \lim_{h \rightarrow 0^+} \frac{f(c + h) - f(c)}{h} &\leq 0 }$
I assumed that $h$ is positive and determined the restriction for the right-hand limit. Now for the left-hand limit:
$\eqalign{ f(c + h) - f(c) &\leq 0 \\ \frac{f(c + h) - f(c)}{h} &\geq 0 \\ \lim_{h \rightarrow 0^-} \frac{f(c + h) - f(c)}{h} &\geq \lim_{h \rightarrow 0^-} 0 \\ \lim_{h \rightarrow 0^-} \frac{f(c + h) - f(c)}{h} &\geq 0 }$
Now if the derivative exists at that point we must have:
$\eqalign{ f'(c) = \lim_{h \rightarrow 0^-} \frac{f(c + h) - f(c)}{h} = \lim_{h \rightarrow 0^+} \frac{f(c + h) - f(c)}{h} = 0 }$
To tackle the minimum we state that we have:
$\eqalign{ d \in (a,b) \hspace{.3cm} \text{s.t.} \hspace{.3cm} f(d) \leq f(d + h) \hspace{.3cm} \text{where} \hspace{.3cm} h \neq 0 }$
Using the inequality:
$\eqalign{ f(d + h) - f(d) &\geq 0 \\ \frac{f(d + h) - f(d)}{h} &\geq 0 \\ \lim_{h \rightarrow 0^+} \frac{f(d + h) - f(d)}{h} &\geq \lim_{h \rightarrow 0^+} 0 \\ \lim_{h \rightarrow 0^+} \frac{f(d + h) - f(d)}{h} &\geq 0 }$
I assumed that $h$ is positive and determined the restriction for the right-hand limit. Now for the left-hand limit:
$\eqalign{ f(d + h) - f(d) &\geq 0 \\ \frac{f(d + h) - f(d)}{h} &\leq 0 \\ \lim_{h \rightarrow 0^-} \frac{f(d + h) - f(d)}{h} &\leq \lim_{h \rightarrow 0^-} 0 \\ \lim_{h \rightarrow 0^-} \frac{f(d + h) - f(d)}{h} &\leq 0 }$
Now if the derivative exists at that point we must have:
$\eqalign{ f'(d) = \lim_{h \rightarrow 0^-} \frac{f(d + h) - f(d)}{h} = \lim_{h \rightarrow 0^+} \frac{f(d + h) - f(d)}{h} = 0 \tag*{$\blacksquare$} }$
Extreme Value Theorem
Given a continuous function, $f(x)$, on the interval $[a,b]$:
$\eqalign{ \exists \hspace{.1cm} c,d \in [a,b] \hspace{.3cm} \text{s.t.} \hspace{.3cm} f(c) \leq f(x) \leq f(d) \hspace{.3cm} \forall x \in [a,b] }$
Proof of Extreme Value Theorem
To approach a proof by contradiction we assume there is a least upper bound on the function:
$\eqalign{ M = \sup_{x \in [a,b]} f(x) }$
but no associated value in the domain that attains it:
$\eqalign{ \nexists \hspace{.1cm} d \in [a,b] \hspace{.3cm} \text{s.t.} \hspace{.3cm} f(d) = M }$
Next up we form a new function:
$\eqalign{ g(x) = \frac{1}{M - f(x)} }$
which is continuous on our interval since both the numerator and denominator are continuous functions. There is also the restriction that the botton never equates to zero on our interval from what we have assumed. Similar to our original function, we define a least upper bound:
$\eqalign{ N = \sup_{x \in [a,b]} g(x) }$
and use it:
$\eqalign{ g(x) &\leq N \\ \frac{1}{M - f(x)} &\leq N \\ 1 &\leq MN - Nf(x) \\ Nf(x) &\leq MN - 1 \\ f(x) &\leq M - \frac{1}{N} }$
where the last line provides us with a contradiction since we assumed that $M$ was the least upper bound for our function. Therefore:
$\eqalign{ \exists \hspace{.1cm} d \in [a,b] \hspace{.3cm} \text{s.t.} \hspace{.3cm} f(d) = M \tag*{$\blacksquare$} }$
To prove the existance of the minimum, the approach is similar. We begin by defining the greatest lower bound for the function:
$\eqalign{ P = \inf_{x \in [a,b]} f(x) }$
but no associated value in the domain that attains it:
$\eqalign{ \nexists \hspace{.1cm} c \in [a,b] \hspace{.3cm} \text{s.t.} \hspace{.3cm} f(c) = P }$
Next up we form a new function:
$\eqalign{ h(x) = \frac{1}{f(x) - P} }$
which is continuous on our interval since both the numerator and denominator are continuous functions. There is also the restriction that the botton never equates to zero on our interval from what we have assumed. Now we define a least upper bound:
$\eqalign{ Q = \sup_{x \in [a,b]} h(x) }$
and use it:
$\eqalign{ h(x) &\leq Q \\ \frac{1}{f(x) - P} &\leq Q \\ 1 &\leq Qf(x) - PQ \\ PQ + 1 &\leq Qf(x) \\ P + \frac{1}{Q} &\leq f(x) }$
where the last line provides us with a contradiction since we assumed that $P$ was the greatest lower bound for our function. Therefore:
$\eqalign{ \exists \hspace{.1cm} c \in [a,b] \hspace{.3cm} \text{s.t.} \hspace{.3cm} f(c) = P \tag*{$\blacksquare$} }$
Rolle's Theorem
Given a function, $f(x)$, that satisfies all of the following:
$\eqalign{ &1. \hspace{.3cm} \text{Continuous on the closed interval} \hspace{.1cm} [a,b] \\ &2. \hspace{.3cm} \text{Differentiable on the open interval} \hspace{.1cm} (a,b) \\ &3. \hspace{.3cm} f(a) = f(b) }$
then:
$\eqalign{ \exists \hspace{.1cm} c \in (a,b) \hspace{.3cm} \text{s.t.} \hspace{.3cm} f'(c) = 0 }$
Proof of Rolle's Theorem
The proof can be split into three separate cases. The first case deals with constant functions, and Rolle's Theorem clearly holds true since $c$ can be chosen to be any value on the open interval. The next case deals with assuming:
$\eqalign{ \exists \hspace{.1cm} d \in (a,b) \hspace{.3cm} \text{s.t.} \hspace{.3cm} f(d) > f(a) }$
By the Extreme Value Theorem we know there must exist some value in the open interval where a maximum is achieved. Since this value is in the interior the function will actually attain a relative maximum there and by the second condition we know that the derivative exists at that point. By Fermat's Theorem we conclude that since this is a relative extrema and the derivative exists at the point, then it is a critical point s.t.:
$\eqalign{ f'(c) = 0 \hspace{.3cm} \text{where} \hspace{.3cm} c \in (a,b) }$
The third case deals with assuming:
$\eqalign{ \exists \hspace{.1cm} d \in (a,b) \hspace{.3cm} \text{s.t.} \hspace{.3cm} f(d) < f(a) }$
By the Extreme Value Theorem we know there must exist some value in the open interval where a minimum is achieved. Since this value is in the interior the function will actually attain a relative minimum there and by the second condition we know that the derivative exists at that point. By Fermat's Theorem we conclude that since this is a relative extrema and the derivative exists at the point, then it is a critical point s.t.:
$\eqalign{ f'(c) = 0 \hspace{.3cm} \text{where} \hspace{.3cm} c \in (a,b) \tag*{$\blacksquare$} }$
Mean Value Theorem
Given a function, $f(x)$, that satisfies all of the following:
$\eqalign{ &1. \hspace{.3cm} \text{Continuous on the closed interval} \hspace{.1cm} [a,b] \\ &2. \hspace{.3cm} \text{Differentiable on the open interval} \hspace{.1cm} (a,b) }$
then:
$\eqalign{ \exists \hspace{.1cm} c \in (a,b) \hspace{.3cm} \text{s.t.} \hspace{.3cm} f'(c)(b - a) = f(b) - f(a) }$
Proof of Mean Value Theorem
To prove the mean value theorem we define a new function:
$\eqalign{ g(x) = f(x) - \kappa x \hspace{.3cm} \text{where} \hspace{.3cm} \kappa \in \mathbb{R} }$
that also satisfies the two conditions. We now want to determine a value for $\kappa$ s.t. the function satisfies Rolle's Theorem:
$\eqalign{ f(a) - \kappa a &= f(b) - \kappa b \\ \kappa(b - a) &= f(b) - f(a) \\ \kappa &= \frac{f(b) - f(a)}{b - a} }$
With our constant chosen, all of the conditions are met for Rolle's Theorem. Therefore:
$\eqalign{ \exists \hspace{.1cm} c \in (a,b) \hspace{.3cm} \text{s.t.} \hspace{.3cm} g'(c) = 0 }$
and directly by our definition of $g$:
$\eqalign{ g'(c) &= 0 \\ f'(c) - \kappa &= 0 \\ f'(c) &= \kappa \\ f'(c) &= \frac{f(b) - f(a)}{b - a} \\ f'(c)(b - a) &= f(b) - f(a) }$
$\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$
