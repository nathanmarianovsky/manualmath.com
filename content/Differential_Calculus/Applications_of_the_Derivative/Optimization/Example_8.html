Problem
Of all lines tangent to the graph of:
$\eqalign{ y = \frac{6}{x^2 + 3} }$
Find the tangent lines of minimum and maximum slope.
Solution
First identify that the function of interest is really the first derivative:
$\eqalign{ f(x) &= y' \\ &= \frac{-12x}{(x^2 + 3)^2} }$
whose extremas we want to determine. To accomplish this we look at the second derivative:
$\eqalign{ y'' &= \frac{d}{dx} \frac{-12x}{(x^2 + 3)^2} \\ &= \frac{-12(x^2 + 3)^2 + 48x^2(x^2 + 3)}{(x^2 + 3)^4} }$
which gives the critical points of the first derivative:
$\eqalign{ 0 &= \frac{-12(x^2 + 3)^2 + 48x^2(x^2 + 3)}{(x^2 + 3)^4} \\ 0 &= -12(x^2 + 3)^2 + 48x^2(x^2 + 3) \\ 0 &= 12(x^2 + 3)(-(x^2 + 3) + 4x^2) \\ 0 &= 12(x^2 + 3)(3x^2 - 3) \\ x &= \pm 1 }$
Now the neccesary evaluations are:
$\eqalign{ f(-\infty) &= 0 \\ f(-1) &= \frac{3}{4} \\ f(1) &= -\frac{3}{4} \\ f(\infty) &= 0 }$
Thus we can state that the maximum and minimum slopes occur at $x = -1$ and $x = 1$ respectively. To determine the tangent lines we use the point-slope form:
$\eqalign{ y - \frac{3}{2} &= \frac{3}{4}(x + 1) \\ y - \frac{3}{2} &= -\frac{3}{4}(x - 1) }$
