The Exact Area Under a Curve
Finally after discussing five different approximations, in this section it will shown how the exact area under a curve is computed. For starters let:
$\eqalign{ \int_a^b f(x) dx }$
represent the exact area under the curve $f(x)$ on the range $[a,b]$. To compute this $\textit{definite integral}$, the Riemann Sum is defined either through the right approximation:
$\eqalign{ \int_a^b f(x) dx &= \lim_{\Delta x \rightarrow 0} \sum_{i=1}^n f(x_i) \Delta x \\ &= \lim_{n \rightarrow \infty} \frac{b-a}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n f(x_i) }$
or the left approximation:
$\eqalign{ \int_a^b f(x) dx &= \lim_{\Delta x \rightarrow 0} \sum_{i=0}^{n-1} f(x_i) \Delta x \\ &= \lim_{n \rightarrow \infty} \frac{b-a}{n} \sum_{i=0}^{n-1} f(x_i) }$
and
$\eqalign{ \Delta x = \frac{b-a}{n} }$
is defined as the constant width. The reason why either the left or right approximation can be used with an infinite number of subdivisions can be found in the section relating to these approximations. Though essentially it is because as an infinite number of subdivisions are taken, both approximations will approach the exact value. With this we say:
$\eqalign{ \int_a^b f(x) dx \hspace{.5cm} \text{exists iff} \hspace{.5cm} \lim_{n \rightarrow \infty} \frac{b-a}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n f(x_i) = \lim_{n \rightarrow \infty} \frac{b-a}{n} \sum_{i=0}^{n-1} f(x_i) }$
It is exactly for this setup that we chose a constant width for the approximations since all approximations are of the form of a Riemann Sum. This setup serves as the general definition of the Riemann Integral, but certainly has a downside. Computing by hand can be done for some examples, but problems can become impossible very quickly.
Properties of Sums & Useful Identities
For examples relating to this section you will need to know the following properties of sums which make it a linear operator:
$\eqalign{ \sum_i \Big( a_i + b_i \Big) &= \sum_i a_i + \sum_i b_i \\ \sum_i \lambda a_i &= \lambda \sum_i a_i \hspace{.5cm} \text{where} \hspace{.3cm} \lambda \in \mathbb{R} }$
and the following identities for computation:
$\eqalign{ \sum_{i=0}^n i &= \frac{n(n+1)}{2} \\ \sum_{i=0}^n i^2 &= \frac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{6} \\ \sum_{i=0}^n i^3 &= \Big( \frac{n(n+1)}{2} \Big)^2 }$
