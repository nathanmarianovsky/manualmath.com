Type I Improper Integrals
When the word improper is attached to an integral, it refers to the fact that somewhere along the range of integration there is an issue. Type I improper integrals refer to the obstruction of having an infinity in the boundary of integration. To handle the case of an infinity, just use a limit to rewrite the integral and evaluate same as always:
$\eqalign{ \int_a^\infty f(x) dx &= \lim_{b \rightarrow \infty} \int_a^b f(x) dx \\ \int_{-\infty}^b f(x) dx &= \lim_{a \rightarrow \infty} \int_{-a}^b f(x) dx \\ \int_{-\infty}^\infty f(x) dx &= \lim_{a \rightarrow \infty} \int_{-a}^b f(x) dx + \lim_{c \rightarrow \infty} \int_b^c f(x) dx }$
Type II Improper Integrals
Type II improper integrals handle the case of the integrand becoming undefined somewhere along the range of integration. For example, consider that the range is $[a,b]$ and $c \in [a,b]$ is the point at which $f(c) = \infty$. Obviously this is an issue since the Riemann Integral requires that a function be not only bounded but also continous. So to handle the integration, write limits and split up where necessary:
$\eqalign{ \int_a^c f(x) dx &= \lim_{k \rightarrow c^-} \int_a^k f(x) dx \\ \int_c^b f(x) dx &= \lim_{k \rightarrow c^+} \int_k^b f(x) dx \\ \int_a^b f(x) dx &= \lim_{m \rightarrow c^-} \int_a^m f(x) dx + \lim_{n \rightarrow c^+} \int_n^b f(x) dx }$
Terminology
In cases of improper integrals, both types, an integral can have two results. An integral can either achieve a finite value after the limit evaluation, or go to infinity. In the case where the result is finite, the integral is said to $\textit{converge}$. Otherwise the integral $\textit{diverges}$.
