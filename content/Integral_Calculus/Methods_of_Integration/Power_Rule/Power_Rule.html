Polynomial Antiderivative
Given $f(x) = x^n$ where $n \in \mathbb{R} \setminus \{-1\}$, the antiderivative is defined as:
$\eqalign{ \int f(x) dx = \int x^n dx = \frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1} + C }$
where $C$ is the constant of integration.
Proof using the Power Rule for Differentiation
Assuming the power rule for differentiation holds true:
$\eqalign{ \frac{d}{dx} x^{n+1} &= (n+1)x^n \\ d(x^{n+1}) &= (n+1)x^n dx \\ \int d(x^{n+1}) &= \int (n+1)x^n dx \\ x^{n+1} + C &= (n+1) \int x^n dx \\ \frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1} + C &= \int x^n dx }$
The Special Case
Given $f(x) = x^{-1}$, the antiderivative is defined as:
$\eqalign{ \int f(x) dx = \int x^{-1} dx = \ln|x| + C }$
where $C$ is the constant of integration and $x \in \mathbb{R} \setminus \{0\}$.
Proof of the Special Case
Given $f(x) = x^{-1}$, the antiderivative can be obtained by making the following substitution if $x \in (0, \infty)$:
$\eqalign{ x &= e^u \\ dx &= e^u du = x du \\ \frac{dx}{x} &= du }$
Plugging into the integral gives:
$\eqalign{ \int f(x) dx = \int x^{-1} dx = \int du = u + C }$
Now to get convert back into a function of $x$ notice that if $x = e^u$ implies $u = \ln(x)$:
$\eqalign{ \int f(x) dx = u + C = \ln(x) + C }$
To handle the case of $x \in (-\infty, 0)$, make the following substitution:
$\eqalign{ x &= -e^u \\ dx &= -e^u du = x du \\ \frac{dx}{x} &= du }$
Plugging into the integral gives:
$\eqalign{ \int f(x) dx = \int x^{-1} dx = \int du = u + C }$
Now to get convert back into a function of $x$ notice that if $x = -e^u$ implies $u = \ln(-x)$:
$\eqalign{ \int f(x) dx = u + C = \ln(-x) + C }$
To finish off, these two cases can be put together into in a single form that handles any $x \in \mathbb{R} \setminus \{0\}$:
$\eqalign{ \int f(x) dx = \int x^{-1} dx = \ln|x| + C }$
