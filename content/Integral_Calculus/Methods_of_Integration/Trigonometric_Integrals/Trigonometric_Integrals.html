A Reminder of Useful Trigonometric Identities
Trigonometric identities come in very useful towards some integrals involving trigonometric functions. So as a fresh reminder we start with the Pythagorean Identities:
$\eqalign{ \sin^2(x) + \cos^2(x) &= 1 \\ \tan^2(x) + 1 &= \sec^2(x) \\ 1 + \cot^2(x) &= \csc^2(x) }$
Double-Angle Identities:
$\eqalign{ \sin(2x) &= 2\sin(x)\cos(x) \\ \cos(2x) &= \cos^2(x) - \sin^2(x) \\ &= 2\cos^2(x) - 1 \\ &= 1 - 2\sin^2(x) }$
and Product-to-Sum Identities:
$\eqalign{ \sin(x)\sin(y) &= \frac{1}{2} \Big( \cos(x-y) - \cos(x+y) \Big) \\ \cos(x)\cos(y) &= \frac{1}{2} \Big( \cos(x-y) + \cos(x+y) \Big) \\ \sin(x)\cos(y) &= \frac{1}{2} \Big( \sin(x+y) + \sin(x-y) \Big) }$
Of course besides these sets there are far more, but I have found that a lot of examples will usually rely on these identities.
Trigonometric Integrals
Trigonometric integrals are nothing more than a title given to integrals whose integrand contains some trigonometric function. These integrals come in many forms, but the general tactic is to choose the correct identities such that the integration simplifies. Double-Angle Identities for example can reduce down a $\sin$ or $\cos$ that is squared to a single power. Product-to-Sum Identities are especially useful since integrals can be broken down under addition. Unfortunately, for these types of integrals there are no hints since examples can vary on a wide range. All that can be said is think of what identities can be used.
