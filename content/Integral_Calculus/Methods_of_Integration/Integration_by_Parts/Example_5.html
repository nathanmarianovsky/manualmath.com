Problem
Evaluate the following:
$\eqalign{ \int x\arctan(x^2) dx }$
Solution
In this scenario the integral is bundled with a polynomial and an inverse trigonometric function. Since inverse trigonometric functions take precendence on the $\textbf{LIPET}$ acrynomy, define the components as:
$\eqalign{ u = \arctan(x^2) \rightarrow& du = \frac{2x}{x^4+1} dx \\ dv = x dx \rightarrow& v = \frac{1}{2}x^2 }$
Now applying the formula yields:
$\eqalign{ \int x\arctan(x^2) dx &= \frac{1}{2}x^2\arctan(x^2) - \int \frac{x^3}{x^4+1} dx }$
To integrate the rest consider the following substitution:
$\eqalign{ w &= x^4+1 \\ dw &= 4x^3 dx \rightarrow \frac{dw}{4} = x^3 dx }$
Plugging in gives:
$\eqalign{ \frac{1}{2}x^2\arctan(x^2) - \int \frac{x^3}{x^4+1} dx &= \frac{1}{2}x^2\arctan(x^2) - \frac{1}{4} \int \frac{dw}{w} \\ &= \frac{1}{2}x^2\arctan(x^2) - \frac{1}{4}\ln|w| + C \\ &= \frac{1}{2}x^2\arctan(x^2) - \frac{1}{4}\ln|x^4 + 1| + C }$
Notice that in the beginning a substitution could have been done to reduce the integration to just of $\arctan$. All that is left after that is to integrate $\arctan$, which in itself requires integration by parts. So in this case jumping right into the parts will definitely save some time.
