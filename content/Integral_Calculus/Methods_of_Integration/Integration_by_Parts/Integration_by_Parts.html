Definition for Indefinite Integral
Given an integral in the form of:
$\eqalign{ \int u dv }$
where a prime denotes the first derivative, the approach is to rewrite the integration as:
$\eqalign{ \int u dv = uv - \int v du }$
Definition for Definite Integral
Given an integral in the form of:
$\eqalign{ \int_a^b u dv }$
where a prime denotes the first derivative, the approach is to rewrite the integration as:
$\eqalign{ \int_a^b u dv = uv \Big|_a^b - \int_a^b v du }$
Knowing How to Choose the Components
Given an integral that is composed of two different elementary functions, how do you go about picking which component is which? Most people will say that there is an intuition when it comes to picking, and I will not disagree because enough of these should get you in the hang of what to pick. But now for the novice, take notice of the following acronym:
$\eqalign{ &\text{L} \hspace{.2cm} \text{ogarithm} \\ &\text{I} \hspace{.35cm} \text{inverse} \\ &\text{P} \hspace{.2cm} \text{olynomial} \\ &\text{E} \hspace{.2cm} \text{xponential} \\ &\text{T} \hspace{.2cm} \text{rigonometric} }$
where inverse refers to a trigonometric inverse such as $\arctan(x)$. Given an integral which consists of any two of these, let $u$ be the one higher up in acronym and $dv$ the rest.
Proof using the Product Rule for Differentiation
Assuming the product rule for differentiation holds true:
$\eqalign{ \frac{d}{dx} \Big( uv \Big) &= \frac{d}{dx} \Big( u \Big) \cdot v + u \cdot \frac{d}{dx} \Big( v \Big) \\ d(uv) &= v du + u dv \\ u dv &= d(uv) - v du }$
Now to arrive at the result known for improper integrals, take the improper integral of both sides:
$\eqalign{ u dv &= d(uv) - v du \\ \int u dv &= \int d(uv) - \int v du \\ \int u dv &= uv - \int v du }$
To obtain the result of the definite integral, take the definite integral of both sides:
$\eqalign{ u dv &= d(uv) - v du \\ \int_a^b u dv &= \int_a^b d(uv) - \int_a^b v du \\ \int_a^b u dv &= uv \Big|_a^b - \int_a^b v du }$
