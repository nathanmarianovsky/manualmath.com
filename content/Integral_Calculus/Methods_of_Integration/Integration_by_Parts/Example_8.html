Problem
Evaluate the following:
$\eqalign{ \int e^x \sin(x) dx }$
Solution
In this scenario the integral is bundled with an exponential and a trigonometric function. Since exponentials take precendence on the $\textbf{LIPET}$ acrynomy, define the components as:
$\eqalign{ u = e^x \rightarrow& du = e^x dx \\ dv = \sin(x) dx \rightarrow& v = \cos(x) }$
Now applying the formula yields:
$\eqalign{ \int e^x \sin(x) dx &= e^x \cos(x) - \int e^x \cos(x) dx }$
Try parts once more, except this time define the components as:
$\eqalign{ u = e^x \rightarrow& du = e^x dx \\ dv = \cos(x) dx \rightarrow& v = -\sin(x) }$
Now applying the formula yields:
$\eqalign{ e^x \cos(x) - \int e^x \cos(x) dx &= e^x \cos(x) - \Big( -e^x \sin(x) + \int e^x \sin(x) dx \Big) \\ &= e^x \cos(x) + e^x \sin(x) - \int e^x \sin(x) dx }$
This is the really interesting part of this integral which turns out to its recursive nature. To better understand this situation write down the initial integral and the most recent result together:
$\eqalign{ \int e^x \sin(x) dx &= e^x \cos(x) + e^x \sin(x) - \int e^x \sin(x) dx \\ 2 \int e^x \sin(x) dx &= e^x \cos(x) + e^x \sin(x) \\ \int e^x \sin(x) dx &= \frac{1}{2} e^x \Big( \cos(x) + \sin(x) \Big) }$
To finish off remember that since this is an indefinite integral, the constant of integration is needed:
$\eqalign{ \int e^x \sin(x) dx = \frac{1}{2} e^x \Big( \cos(x) + \sin(x) \Big) + C }$
