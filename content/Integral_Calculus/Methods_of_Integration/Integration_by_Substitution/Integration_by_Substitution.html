Definition for Indefinite Integral
Given an integral in the form of:
$\eqalign{ \int f(g(x)) \cdot g'(x) dx }$
where a prime denotes the first derivative, the approach to simplify the integration is to let $u = g(x)$ and $du = g'(x) dx$ such that:
$\eqalign{ \int f(g(x)) \cdot g'(x) dx = \int f(u) du }$
When writing down the final answer remember to convert back using $u = g(x)$.
Definition for Definite Integral
Given an integral in the form of:
$\eqalign{ \int_a^b f(g(x)) \cdot g'(x) dx }$
where a prime denotes the first derivative, the approach to simplify the integration is to let $u = g(x)$ and $du = g'(x) dx$ exactly the same as for the indefinite integral. The first difference with the definite integral is that the boundaries also have to be redefined such that:
$\eqalign{ \int_a^b f(g(x)) \cdot g'(x) dx = \int_{g(a)}^{g(b)} f(u) du }$
The second difference with the indefinite integral is that there is no need to substitute back since the final answer is always going to be a scalar no matter the substitution.
