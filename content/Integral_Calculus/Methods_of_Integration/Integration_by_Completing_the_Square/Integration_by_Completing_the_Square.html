A Reminder of How to Complete the Square
Given a quadratic function in the most general form:
$\eqalign{ f(x) = ax^2 + bx + c \\ }$
by completing the square the function can be put in the form:
$\eqalign{ f(x) = a(x - h)^2 + k }$
For a derivation, observe:
$\eqalign{ f(x) &= ax^2 + bx + c \\ &= a \Big( x^2 + \frac{b}{a}x + \frac{c}{a} \Big) \\ &= a \Big( x^2 + \frac{b}{a}x + \frac{b^2}{4a^2} - \frac{b^2}{4a^2} + \frac{c}{a} \Big) \\ &= a \Bigg( \Big[ x + \frac{b}{2a} \Big]^2 + \frac{c}{a} - \frac{b^2}{4a^2} \Bigg) \\ &= a \Big( x + \frac{b}{a} \Big)^2 + c - \frac{b^2}{4a} \\ &= a \Big( x + \frac{b}{a} \Big)^2 + \frac{4ac-b^2}{4a} }$
Application in Integration
So now the more important part: How does this apply towards integration? Generally speaking this is a very special case, but is definitely worth mentioning. Assume that an integral is given in the form:
$\eqalign{ \int \frac{dx}{ax^2+bx+c} }$
where the quadratic on the bottom cannot be reduced any further using strictly real roots. By completing the square:
$\eqalign{ \int \frac{dx}{ax^2+bx+c} &= \int \frac{dx}{a\Big( x + \frac{b}{a} \Big)^2 + \frac{4ac-b^2}{4a}} \\ &= \frac{4a}{4ac-b^2} \int \frac{dx}{1 + \frac{4a^2}{4ac-b^2}\Big( x + \frac{b}{a} \Big)^2} \\ &= \frac{4a}{4ac-b^2} \int \frac{dx}{1 + \Big( \frac{2a}{\sqrt{4ac-b^2}} \Big( x + \frac{b}{a} \Big) \Big)^2} }$
and using a substitution:
$\eqalign{ u &= \frac{2a}{4ac-b^2} \Big( x + \frac{a}{c} \Big) \\ du &= \frac{2a}{4ac-b^2} dx \rightarrow \frac{4ac-b^2}{2a} du = dx }$
gives:
$\eqalign{ \frac{4a}{4ac-b^2} \int \frac{dx}{1 + \Big( \frac{2a}{\sqrt{4ac-b^2}} \Big( x + \frac{b}{a} \Big) \Big)^2} &= \frac{2}{\sqrt{4ac-b^2}} \int \frac{du}{1+u^2} \\ &= \frac{2}{\sqrt{4ac-b^2}} \arctan(u) + C \\ &= \frac{2}{\sqrt{4ac-b^2}} \arctan \Bigg( \frac{2}{\sqrt{4ac-b^2}} \Big( x + \frac{b}{a} \Big) \Bigg) + C }$
